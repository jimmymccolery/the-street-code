Most substrate-output discussion treats substrate value as problem-solving. The substrate produces answers, recommendations, architectural choices. The substrate's contribution is what the substrate delivers as solutions. The framing is operationally useful and covers a wide class of substrate work; the framing also under-credits a different kind of substrate contribution that does not produce solutions but produces something arguably more valuable to operator-side workflow.

The other contribution is problem-scoping. The substrate identifies which design problems are architectural (the architecture can solve them; substrate can propose architectural choices), which are operator-judgment (the architecture cannot solve them; the operator must apply judgment), which are content-authoring (the architecture sets up the surface where content authoring happens; the content authoring is the operator's writing work), and which are runtime-testing (the architecture cannot anticipate the answer; runtime testing with users is what surfaces it). The problem-scoping work is what determines where operator bandwidth should go; the substrate's contribution at this layer is unrecognized scope-work that operates upstream of the problem-solving work.

A recent multi-substrate triangulation arc surfaced two substrates from different model lineages independently identifying the same operator-judgment surface. The two substrates' framings were complementary: one substrate surfaced the tension as a fabric-of-experience concern beyond mechanical design; the other substrate named the exact design pixel where the tension can break and framed why only operator-side playtesting catches it. The convergent identification is what makes this post substantive; the substrates were not just producing different solutions to the same problem, they were independently identifying which problems are not solvable at the architectural layer and routing them back to operator-side judgment with explicit framing.

## The two observations

The arc was an architectural-closure cycle covering a design surface in my framework where mechanical design decisions interact with tone-of-experience considerations. The arc's fifth axis was a substrate from one model family applying its native research-rigor analysis to the design surface. The substrate produced substantive architectural analysis covering mechanical considerations and then surfaced an additional observation: introducing any layer of explicit time-pressure feedback (even a binary form, even player-controlled) might subtly erode the relaxed-ownership fantasy that the broader design surface was trying to maintain. The substrate's framing was that this is a fabric-of-experience concern beyond mechanical design; the substrate did not propose a mechanical solution because the substrate did not identify a mechanical solution that would dissolve the tension.

The arc's sixth axis was a substrate from a different model family receiving the fifth axis's findings as ground-truth context. The substrate engaged the fifth-axis observation explicitly and named the exact design pixel where the tension can break. The substrate's framing was that the design's temporal-proximity yellow-accent modifier is the pixel where the operator could tip from a neutral framing of upcoming events into an urgent framing of the same events. The substrate's exact framing (paraphrased to abstract from the specific design surface): the architecture holds the line; the copy is where it can break. The substrate then explicitly framed this as a tension that is not solvable by architecture and is an operator-judgment surface; a writing-and-tone problem that only playtesting with a human ear catches.

The two observations are complementary. The fifth-axis substrate surfaced the tension as a concern beyond mechanical design without locating the exact pixel where the tension can fire. The sixth-axis substrate located the exact pixel and explicitly framed why only operator-side playtesting catches the failure mode at the located pixel. The substrates did not converge on a verdict; the substrates converged on identifying a specific operator-judgment surface that the architectural-design layer cannot resolve.

## What problem-scoping work contributes that problem-solving work does not

Problem-solving work produces solutions; the value is the solution. Problem-scoping work produces decisions about where work should go; the value is the routing. Both are operationally meaningful; both are load-bearing for substantive design work.

The two work classes operate at different layers. Problem-solving work operates inside the problem's solution surface; the substrate proposes architectural choices, evaluates tradeoffs, recommends actions. Problem-scoping work operates above the problem's solution surface; the substrate identifies whether a solution surface exists at all (is this an architectural problem or an operator-judgment problem), which layer the solution surface operates at (architectural, content-authoring, runtime-testing), and what kind of work would be needed at the solution surface.

The under-credit of problem-scoping work in substrate-output discussion has a specific consequence. Operators evaluating substrate contributions often measure substrate value by solution density (how many architectural choices the substrate proposed; how many tradeoffs the substrate analyzed; how many recommendations the substrate delivered). The measurement under-counts substrates that produce strong problem-scoping work because the problem-scoping work does not look like solution density. A substrate that identifies a problem as operator-judgment and frames why looks like it produced less solution work than a substrate that proposed three architectural choices for the same problem; the problem-scoping substrate may have done more substantive design work because the operator-judgment framing is what prevents the operator from wasting effort attempting architectural solutions to a non-architectural problem.

The problem-scoping work is also harder to instruct. A substrate prompted to solve a problem produces solution output; a substrate prompted to scope a problem produces scope output only if the substrate's analytical lens identifies scope work as part of the substrate's contribution to the cycle. The substrates at the fifth and sixth axes of my arc produced the problem-scoping work as part of their independent analytical contributions; the prompt did not explicitly ask for scope work. The convergent identification of the same operator-judgment surface across two substrates is evidence that the scope work emerged from substrate-emergent analytical lens rather than from prompt instruction.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes. First, when I evaluate substrate contributions at multi-substrate Council closing-round synthesis, I now explicitly look for problem-scoping work as a distinct value class separate from problem-solving work. The two classes operate at different layers; treating them as substitutes for each other under-counts the substrate's contribution at the scope layer. The problem-scoping work is what determines where operator bandwidth should go in subsequent cycles; the value is meaningful even if it does not appear in solution-density measurements.

Second, when I read substrate output for an architectural design cycle, I now treat substrate identification of operator-judgment surfaces as a specific signal worth surfacing at the closing-round walkthrough. The substrate's framing of why a problem is not architecturally solvable is operationally load-bearing because the framing prevents the operator from wasting cycles attempting architectural solutions. The discipline is to capture the substrate's framing of the operator-judgment surface at the closing-round walkthrough and route the surface to operator-judgment review rather than to architectural-decision review.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming substrates can identify all operator-judgment surfaces. Substrates' analytical lenses are shaped by their training; some operator-judgment surfaces are within the substrates' analytical reach (the lens can identify the surface as operator-judgment); other operator-judgment surfaces are outside the substrates' analytical reach (the lens does not identify the surface as operator-judgment because the lens does not recognize the surface's character as judgment-class). The pattern fires for surfaces within the substrates' analytical reach; the framing is not a universal claim about substrate capability.

I am not claiming the cross-substrate convergence at n=2 in my arc fully validates the pattern. The convergence is one specific instance at one specific cycle; the pattern's broader generalization to other architectural cycles depends on further reproductions. The two-substrate convergence is suggestive that the surface-identification pattern is not single-substrate-stylistic; the pattern's full generalization requires further reproductions across other architectural cycles with different substrate sets.

I am not claiming the problem-scoping work substitutes for problem-solving work. Both classes are load-bearing for substantive design work; the post elevates problem-scoping work because it is under-credited, not because problem-solving work is over-credited. Substrates doing strong problem-solving work produce substantial value; substrates doing strong problem-scoping work also produce substantial value; the two value classes complement each other rather than compete with each other.

I am not claiming all substrate identification of design tensions warrants operator-judgment routing. Some tensions the substrates identify are actually architectural and warrant architectural-decision routing; the substrate's framing of a tension as operator-judgment is the substrate's framing, not the canonical truth about the tension's solvability. The operator-side review at the closing-round walkthrough is what evaluates the substrate's framing against the operator's own judgment about the tension's character; the substrate-identified operator-judgment routing is a candidate, not the final routing decision.

I am not claiming all design problems can be cleanly classified into architectural, operator-judgment, content-authoring, and runtime-testing categories. Some design problems span multiple categories; some problems shift category as the design surface evolves; some problems resist categorization entirely. The four-category framing is a useful operational scheme for routing decisions; the framing is not a complete taxonomy of design-problem classes. Treating the categories as exhaustive would over-formalize a working operational scheme.

## What I am claiming

Substrates operating at architectural-design-density can identify operator-judgment surfaces explicitly, and the identification is substantive scope-work that determines where operator bandwidth should go in subsequent design cycles. The two-substrate convergent identification in my arc surfaced an operator-judgment surface that neither substrate proposed an architectural solution for; the substrates' framing was that the problem is not solvable by architecture and that only operator-side playtesting catches the failure mode at the specific design pixel the second substrate located.

If you are running multi-substrate Council deployments at architectural design surfaces, the problem-scoping work substrates produce is worth reading as a distinct value class separate from the problem-solving work. The two classes operate at different layers; the problem-scoping work routes problems to the right solution surface (architectural, operator-judgment, content-authoring, runtime-testing); the problem-solving work operates inside the solution surface that the routing decision specifies. Treating the two classes as substitutes for each other under-counts substrate contributions at the scope layer.

The pattern's empirical foundation is the n=2 cross-substrate cumulative observation; the methodology candidacy is open until further reproductions across other architectural cycles surface. The discipline I have committed to includes evaluating substrate problem-scoping contributions explicitly at multi-substrate Council closing-round synthesis and routing substrate-identified operator-judgment surfaces to operator-judgment review rather than to architectural-decision review when the operator-side evaluation agrees with the substrate's framing.
