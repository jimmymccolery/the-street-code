## Thesis

Two times in the course of building Modified-C-bis, the framework's own canonical record was wrong against primary text — and in both cases, the error was caught not by adding more substrates but by an orchestrator-class AI reading the primary source directly and noticing what the previous substrates had missed. The two cases are not failures of multi-substrate verification (Rule 11). They are failures of substrate-paraphrase chains that cross-verification couldn't catch because multiple substrates had read at the same depth of the same chapter and reached the same wrong conclusion. The orchestrator caught it by reading deeper, or reading differently, or reading what the prior substrates didn't quote.

The methodology lesson is sharper than "use multiple substrates": it is **even with cross-substrate convergence, primary-text re-reads by an orchestrator-class substrate can reverse the verdict**. The operator should not treat multi-substrate convergence as the final ratification gate. It is a strong signal, not a stop signal.

## The two cases

### Case 1 — Friston 2017 Appendix F (FLAG F-14)

For four rounds of philosophical research (Rounds 4 through 7), the framework's canonical record carried a flag that said the "Friston 2017 Appendix F naive/sophisticated active inference distinction" was UNCORROBORATED — probably a paper conflation between Friston 2017 (which the canonical record said did NOT contain the formal distinction) and Friston 2021 (which the canonical record said was the actual source). The flag was load-bearing: the framework's terminology discipline + Modification 6 Amendment + canonical citations all defaulted to Friston 2021 alone for sophisticated-inference attribution, with the Friston 2017 attribution hedged or removed.

In Round 8 (R2 Perplexity Deep Research), the substrate read Friston 2017 directly and found the naive/sophisticated distinction in Appendix F at pp. 42-43. The exact passage:

> "Clearly, this scheme is computationally more involved than the naive scheme and calls on recursive variational updating. This means that sophisticated agents are metacognitive in some sense because they perform belief updating (based on fictive outcomes) to optimize their belief updating."

R4 (Claude Web Madisonian Gate) independently verified by direct primary-text re-read. The "UNCORROBORATED" flag was retracted. Both Friston 2017 Appendix F and Friston 2021 are valid co-citations.

The lesson: the canonical record carried a stale wrong flag for FOUR rounds because no substrate during those rounds had read Friston 2017 Appendix F at the page-number level. Substrate paraphrases of the paper consistently said "naive/sophisticated is in Friston 2021" — and the framework accepted that. It took an orchestrator-class substrate directly reading the PDF to catch the original attribution was correct after all.

### Case 2 — Wiredu Ch 9 universalist framing (FLAG F-17)

Modified-C-bis borrowed Wiredu's reading of Akan personhood-as-achievement as a cross-cultural structural anchor for procedural-character design. For three rounds of philosophical research (Rounds 4-2 through 8 R2), the framework debated whether Wiredu's framework was *universalist* (applicable across cultures) or *Akan-particular* (specifically Ghanaian). Round 4-2 originally claimed universalist. Round 6 CW R6 flagged the universalist claim as UNCORROBORATED. Round 8 R2 (Perplexity Deep Research) re-read Wiredu Ch 9 directly and "confirmed" the UNCORROBORATED status. The framework canonicalized "Wiredu universalist UNCORROBORATED."

In Round 8 R4 (Claude Web Madisonian Gate closing-round synthesis), the orchestrator-class substrate re-read Wiredu Ch 9 directly and noticed something R2 had missed: the chapter opens (p. 193) with the line "Morality in the strictest sense is universal to human culture… essential to all human culture." Wiredu states (p. 199) that sympathetic impartiality "is the first principle of all morals" and "the logical basis of the golden rule" — explicitly cross-cultural by Wiredu's own framing. R4-2 from the original arc had been right. R2 from Round 8 had been wrong. The canonical record had sided with R2's wrong reading.

The catch happened because R2 had read pages 199-205 carefully but had not quoted the chapter-opening framing at p. 193. R4 noticed the chapter opening, read it, and reversed the flag.

The lesson: cross-substrate convergence is not proof of correctness when the substrates converge on a partial reading. R2's reading was internally consistent — sympathetic impartiality IS the Akan first principle of morals per Ch 9 p. 199. But the chapter OPENS with universalist framing that contextualizes the rest of the chapter. R2 didn't quote p. 193. The orchestrator did.

## Why these two cases belong in the same post

Both stories are the same shape:

1. Multi-substrate convergence reached a verdict.
2. The verdict became canonical state.
3. The verdict was wrong against primary text.
4. An orchestrator-class substrate caught the error by reading primary text at a different depth than the converged substrates had.
5. The canonical record was reversed.

The methodology lesson is the same in both cases. The operator's discipline that catches this — orchestrator-class re-reads, primary-text-grounding-not-paraphrase-chains — is the same.

Co-locating them in one post lets the post make the methodology point cleanly with two examples rather than diluting it across two posts.
