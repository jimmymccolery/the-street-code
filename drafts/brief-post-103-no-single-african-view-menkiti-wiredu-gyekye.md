## Thesis

Modified-C-bis's structural-borrowing from Wiredu's reading of Akan personhood-as-achievement is a borrowing from ONE philosopher's reading of his tradition. Within Akan philosophy itself, Wiredu's view is contested. Gyekye (Wiredu's co-editor of *Person and Community* 1992) defends a Kant-grounded counter-position: personhood is birth-conferred + relational-context-enhanced, NOT achievement-graded. Menkiti (Nigerian; Igbo background, not Akan) takes the achievement-graded view that Wiredu defends, but from a different cultural starting point. The SEP entry on Akan personhood gives Gyekye's counter-position equal weight; the Wiredu-Menkiti agreement on achievement is one philosophical position among multiple. Post 103 documents this intra-African disagreement explicitly so the framework + readers + future critics know the field is plural. The post is a feature of the discipline (Post 100's 5-element commitment), not an embarrassment — disclosing intra-tradition dispute is the difference between scholarship and appropriation.

## Sections (outline)

1. **Opening humility paragraph (5-element discipline).** Particularly careful framing because the post engages with intra-tradition philosophical dispute.

2. **Hook:** "I cite Wiredu's reading of Akan personhood-as-achievement. Within Akan philosophy itself, that reading is contested. This post documents the contestation."

3. **What Wiredu's view is.** Recap (from Posts 84 + 93 + 101):
   - Wiredu Ch 9 *Person and Community* 1992: personhood-as-achievement; *onipa* graduated concept; continual striving over a lifetime; sympathetic impartiality first principle (universalist; F-17 CORROBORATED)
   - Aligns with Menkiti's earlier (1984) "Person and Community in African Traditional Thought" — Menkiti argues personhood is acquired through participation in communal life + ritual incorporation

4. **What Gyekye's counter-position is.** Direct from Gyekye's contributions in *Person and Community* 1992 + earlier work:
   - Personhood is INHERENT at birth — a Kant-grounded view that humans qua humans have intrinsic moral status
   - Communal context ENHANCES personhood (through relational accountability + role fulfillment) but does NOT CONSTITUTE it
   - The Akan judgment "he is not a human person" (*onnye nipa*) applied to cruel/selfish people: Gyekye reads this as a normative judgment of MORAL VIRTUE, not ontological denial of biological humanity. Wiredu reads it as graduated personhood status.
   - Gyekye explicitly disputes Menkiti-Wiredu attainment view per Wiredu's CRVP introduction (pp. 10-11)

5. **What the SEP says.** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Akan personhood:
   - Gives Gyekye's counter-position EQUAL WEIGHT to Wiredu/Menkiti
   - Documents the philosophical dispute as ongoing within Akan/African philosophy
   - References both Wiredu and Gyekye's primary texts; the Akan tradition is plural
   - SEP framing standard for "field has not converged on a single view"

6. **Why this matters for framework integrity.**
   - Modified-C-bis adopts Wiredu's reading as STRUCTURAL inspiration (Post 84 + framework paper)
   - Presenting "personhood-as-achievement" as "the Akan view" would be MISREPRESENTATION
   - The honest framing is: "Modified-C-bis borrows the structural logic of Wiredu's *reading* of Akan personhood-as-achievement; this reading is contested within Akan philosophy itself, with Gyekye defending a birth-conferred personhood that the framework does not adopt"
   - This framing belongs in Post 84 + framework paper + every Wiredu-citing context

7. **Comparison: how would Gyekye's view affect Modified-C-bis if adopted?**
   - Under Gyekye's view: NPCs are persons at instantiation; communal context affects relational accountability + role fulfillment but doesn't change personhood status
   - This maps to a different procedural-character architecture: characters don't EARN personhood through striving; they EXPRESS personhood through relational engagement
   - Modified-C-bis's "personhood-as-achievement" structural framing relies on Wiredu's reading; if the operator wanted Gyekye's reading, the framework would be different
   - Both are philosophically defensible; the framework's choice is Wiredu's reading; the disclosure is that this is A CHOICE within a contested field

8. **The Menkiti-vs-Wiredu agreement and intra-African philosophy more broadly.**
   - Menkiti (Nigerian/Igbo) and Wiredu (Ghanaian/Akan) agree on the achievement-graded view from different cultural starting points
   - This convergence across two African philosophical traditions is sometimes cited as evidence for the achievement view's broader applicability
   - Gyekye disagrees from within Akan philosophy specifically
   - African philosophy is plural; there is no "the African view"; presenting any single view as "African" is a category error

9. **What disclosure does NOT mean.**
   - Disclosure ≠ resolution: the framework doesn't resolve the philosophical dispute; it discloses the dispute
   - Disclosure ≠ neutrality: the framework adopts Wiredu's reading; it's transparent about that choice
   - Disclosure ≠ undermining philosophical authority: Wiredu's reading remains philosophically defensible; Gyekye's counter-position remains philosophically defensible; both can coexist

10. **The retraction condition.** If a knowledgeable Akan-philosophy scholar shows that the framework materially misrepresents either Wiredu's view OR Gyekye's view OR the SEP characterization of the dispute, retract within 14 days. The framework's claim is structural-borrowing-only with explicit disclosure of intra-tradition disagreement, not representational accuracy.

11. **Closing humility paragraph (5-element discipline).** Standard close.
