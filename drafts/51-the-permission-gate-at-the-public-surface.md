## The seed: what to write about

Three sibling-class catches in a single session on the question of moving content from private surface (resume, conversation, draft) to public surface (live website, GitHub repo, blog post). All three followed the same pattern. All three needed the same discipline.

## The three fires to narrate

**Fire 1 — Named institutions on a CSM resume bullet.** The resume listed seven specific academic institutions where the operator ran customer-success kickoffs. CC recommended adding three of them to the corresponding bullet on the operator's public website to give the claim concrete depth. The operator paused. Sibling resume v7 already named them — was that implicit permission to publish on the site? It wasn't. Resume is selective distribution; public website is indexed-by-Google distribution. Same content, different exposure profile. Revert to generic category enumeration; same delivery, no customer-relationship disclosure.

**Fire 2 — Pro-bono website builds for personal contacts.** The operator had shipped two website builds for friends using the AI methodology framework. Clients now controlled the live pages. Permission-ask deferred until portfolio value justifies the cost.

## The pattern across all three

All three share structural features:

- Content already exists in a private or selective-distribution surface (resume, conversation, draft).
- The move-to-public step is what triggers the disclosure question.
- The cost of asking before the move is small (verify employment agreement; send 2-sentence text to friend; cross-check public vendor list).
- The cost of unauthorized public disclosure is non-recoverable.
- Operator-side judgment is decisive — CC's role is to surface the question, not to make the call.

## The discipline that emerged

**When content moves from private surface to public surface: don't surface OR genericize OR get explicit permission before the move.** The three remediations are ordered by cost (cheapest first):

1. **Don't surface.** Lowest cost, highest discretion. Treat the content as private until further notice. Verbal disclosure in interview/conversation contexts is much lower-friction than public-site publication.
2. **Genericize.** Preserve the achievement / capability claim; remove identifying details. "R1 universities and state library systems across the territory" instead of "Iowa State / Washington University in St. Louis / Arkansas State Library." The claim survives; the disclosure doesn't.
3. **Get explicit permission, then publish with attribution.** Highest cost, full social proof. Short ask, short answer; the asking itself is professional behavior that strengthens the relationship.

## The lesson worth surfacing

Permission isn't about whether you have the right to publish. It's about whether the entity referenced has consented to the publication. The right to publish doesn't transfer permission. The fact that something is true doesn't transfer permission. The fact that it appears on your resume doesn't transfer permission to publish it more broadly.

Default to don't surface + verify or genericize. The cost of asking is small. The cost of unauthorized disclosure is non-recoverable.
