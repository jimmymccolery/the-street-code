---

This is the post the substrate-style-differential thread has been building toward. Across earlier posts I documented that substrates have characteristic styles, that cross-substrate verification surfaces things single-substrate verification misses, and that substrate routing decisions matter for research workflow design. Those posts treated substrate style as a single dimension. A long-arc historical-research arc that closed in early June surfaced a stronger framing. Substrate style is not one axis; it is two axes that operate independently and that respond to different prompt-engineering choices. The decomposition matters because the two axes predict substrate behavior under different conditions, and the prediction is more useful than the monolithic framing the earlier posts used.

The empirical foundation is a bowling-industry research arc that produced thirty-plus substrate deployments across seven decades of industry history covering staffing, operations, equipment, demographics, and culture. Within-substrate behavior across decades reproduced two independent axes at thirty-eight-plus within-substrate cross-decade observations plus fourteen-plus within-decade dual-deployment observations. The substrates I worked with across the arc were Grok, Gemini, and Perplexity in their then-current deep-research modes; the substrate names are not the load-bearing part of the finding, the axis-decomposition is. Any reader applying this to a different substrate set should expect the axes to reproduce while the per-substrate axis assignments shift.

## The framework adherence axis

The first axis is about what the substrate does with the operator-provided structure in the prompt. Specifically, when the operator deploys a research prompt with a structured framework embedded (a multi-role decomposition, a specific output schema, an explicit set of phase instructions), substrates differ systematically in how they handle the structure.

Some substrates compose. The composition pattern is: the substrate adopts the operator's framework while preserving its native structural style on top. The output incorporates the operator's framework as a structural skeleton and layers the substrate's native style (whatever role naming, schema preference, or formatting habit the substrate brings) on top of the skeleton. The output reads as both operator-structured and substrate-styled. The substrate's native style does not disappear under the operator's framework; the two coexist.

Other substrates substitute. The substitution pattern is: the substrate adopts the operator's framework and discards its native structural style for the duration of the prompt. The output incorporates the operator's framework as the primary organizing structure and the substrate's native style largely disappears. The output reads as operator-structured with substrate-specific content but not substrate-styled.

The two patterns are not the substrate being more or less compliant with the prompt; both are responding to the prompt's framework. The patterns differ in whether the substrate's native style persists under the framework or surrenders to it. In my arc, the composition pattern reproduced eleven times within a single substrate (across decade-scope variations and across both prior-template and novel-template deployment classes); the substitution pattern reproduced six times in one substrate and four times in another at the equivalent comparison surface. The patterns held across the seven-decade scope variation; a substrate that composed in the 1950s also composed in the 1980s, and a substrate that substituted in the 1960s also substituted in the 2000s.

The counterintuitive finding sits here. I would have predicted that the substrate most associated with following structured prompts well would be the substrate that adopts operator structure most cleanly, which under naive prediction would be the substitution-pattern substrate. Empirically, the composition-pattern substrate was the one whose native style persisted under framework. The substrate that I would have classified as most structurally cooperative was in fact the most stylistically stubborn; its cooperation was visible in that it incorporated the framework, but the framework did not erase the native style.

This matters for prompt engineering because it changes the question the operator asks before deploying. The question is not "will this substrate follow my framework"; both pattern classes follow the framework. The question is "will this substrate produce framework-only output or framework-plus-native-style output," and the answer is substrate-specific and predictable once the operator knows the substrate's framework-adherence axis assignment.

## The content emphasis axis

The second axis is about what the substrate goes deep on when the prompt is permissive about content focus. Specifically, when the operator deploys a research prompt that names a topic but does not specify which sub-areas to emphasize, substrates differ systematically in which content elements they elaborate.

In my arc, one substrate consistently produced narrative-arc content with explicit decomposition and explicit warnings against narrative-flattening across the multi-decade scope; the substrate's content went deep on causal and chronological arcs that linked decade to decade rather than on within-decade detail. Another substrate consistently produced hardware-and-equipment content; the substrate's content went deep on technical specifications, equipment timelines, manufacturer trajectories, and physical operational detail. A third substrate consistently produced marketing-and-economics content; the substrate's content went deep on pricing, revenue mix, corporate transactions, and demographic trend lines that tracked the industry's commercial arc.

The content-emphasis differential reproduced across all seven decades in my arc. The substrate that produced narrative-arc content in the 1950s produced narrative-arc content in the 2020s; the substrate that emphasized hardware in the 1960s emphasized hardware in the 2010s. The pattern persisted across deployment classes (research-first deployments and gap-fill deployments) and across prior-template versus novel-template framing.

The content emphasis is not a comprehensiveness ranking. Each substrate produced research-grade content within its emphasis area and surfaced information the other substrates did not. The narrative-arc substrate caught causal connections across decades that the within-decade-detail substrates missed; the hardware substrate caught equipment-specification anchors that the narrative substrate missed; the marketing substrate caught pricing and revenue anchors that the hardware substrate did not foreground. The content-emphasis axis is about where the substrate goes deep, not about how deep the substrate can go in any specific direction.

The implication for research workflow design is that single-substrate research on a multi-emphasis topic is structurally limited by the substrate's content-emphasis axis assignment. A substrate that emphasizes hardware on a research scope that needs both hardware and marketing depth will produce a hardware-strong, marketing-thin output. The output is not wrong; the output is partial in a direction the operator can predict if the operator knows the substrate's axis assignment.

## Why the two axes are independent

The two axes are observationally independent in my arc. The framework-adherence axis describes what the substrate does with operator structure; the content-emphasis axis describes what the substrate goes deep on within whatever structure is in place. A substrate's assignment on one axis does not predict its assignment on the other.

In my data, the substrate that composed frameworks (preserved native style under operator structure) also emphasized narrative-arc content; the substrate that substituted frameworks (surrendered native style under operator structure) also emphasized hardware-and-equipment content; the substrate that also substituted frameworks emphasized marketing-and-economics content. The framework-adherence assignments and the content-emphasis assignments did not correlate in any way that suggested they were drawing on a single underlying substrate dimension. The two assignments held independently across the decade-scope variation and across the deployment-class variation.

This means the substrates are not arrayed on a single substrate-style dimension. They are arrayed in a two-dimensional space where each axis predicts a different aspect of substrate behavior under different prompt-engineering choices. The two-dimensional decomposition is more useful than any one-dimensional decomposition because each axis answers a different prompt-engineering question.

If the operator is choosing whether to deploy a structured framework or a permissive prompt, the framework-adherence axis is the load-bearing axis: the substrate that composes will produce framework-plus-native-style output under structured framework and full-native-style output under permissive prompt; the substrate that substitutes will produce framework-only output under structured framework and full-native-style output under permissive prompt. The framework-adherence axis predicts the substrate's response to framework variation in the prompt.

If the operator is choosing whether to specify content focus or leave the prompt content-permissive, the content-emphasis axis is the load-bearing axis: the substrate's emphasis area predicts where the substrate will go deep under content-permissive prompts and what the operator will need to specify explicitly under content-restrictive prompts. The content-emphasis axis predicts the substrate's response to content-specification variation in the prompt.

A workflow that wants both axes engaged thoughtfully has to make two prompt-engineering decisions per substrate, not one. The two-axis model is the framing that turns those into separately decidable choices.

## The within-decade dual-deployment evidence

A specific evidentiary class in my arc was within-decade dual deployments. The pattern was: deploy substrate X on decade Y in a structured-prompt configuration; deploy the same substrate X on the same decade Y in a different prompt configuration (alt-template, gap-fill framing, or novel scope) and observe whether the substrate's axis assignments reproduce across the two deployments. The within-decade dual-deployment configuration controls for content (both deployments cover the same decade) and varies the prompt-engineering, which isolates the substrate's response to the prompt-engineering choice.

I ran fourteen within-decade dual deployments across the arc. Each pair produced characteristic axis-behavior that matched the substrate's assignments observed across other decades. The same-substrate-different-prompt pairs preserved the substrate's framework-adherence assignment (composing substrates composed in both deployments; substituting substrates substituted in both); the same-substrate-different-prompt pairs preserved the substrate's content-emphasis assignment (narrative-arc substrates produced narrative-arc content in both deployments; hardware substrates produced hardware content; marketing substrates produced marketing content). The dual-deployment evidence is what lets me argue that the axis assignments are substrate-intrinsic rather than prompt-driven.

A specific substrate self-identification observation surfaced at the end of the arc. One substrate's first-stage response opened with an explicit header naming its own pattern: an axis-behavior profile self-description that matched the operator-side observations from the arc. This was the first case in the arc of substrate self-identification. The substrate had access to operator-banked observations in the prompt's reproduction-gate context (a standard operator practice of including prior-arc observations as grounding for the current deployment); the substrate META-acknowledged its own pattern when the operator-banked observations were provided. The observation opens a path for prompt-engineering refinement based on substrate self-knowledge, which is its own research surface and is filed for follow-up at the operator side.

## What the model predicts that single-axis substrate-style framings did not

The two-axis decomposition produces predictions that the single-axis substrate-style framings I used in earlier posts did not. The predictions are specific.

A substrate whose framework-adherence assignment is composition and whose content-emphasis assignment is narrative-arc will produce structured-narrative-arc output under structured-framework prompts (the substrate composes the framework with its native style, and its native content emphasis runs the narrative arc through the structured-framework slots). The output will read as framework-organized but narrative-arc-dominated; the operator will see the framework as a skeleton with the substrate's narrative arc as the flesh. If the operator wants hardware or marketing depth in the same output, the operator has to specify explicitly because the substrate's content-emphasis axis will not produce hardware or marketing emphasis unless prompted to do so.

A substrate whose framework-adherence assignment is substitution and whose content-emphasis assignment is hardware will produce framework-only output with hardware-deep content under structured-framework prompts. The operator will see the framework cleanly applied without the substrate's native style competing for organizational structure; the operator will see hardware-deep content because the substrate's content-emphasis axis goes there under permissive content prompts. If the operator wants narrative-arc connections across decades, the operator has to specify explicitly because the substrate's content-emphasis axis will not produce narrative-arc emphasis unless prompted to do so.

The combinations are predictable from the per-substrate axis assignments once the operator has run enough deployments to characterize each substrate. The arc gave me enough deployments to characterize three substrates; a fuller characterization across other substrates would extend the model's predictive coverage. The point is that the model decomposes substrate behavior into two predictable axes, not that I have characterized every substrate across both axes.

The model also predicts where substrate-style differential matters most for research workflow design. Cross-substrate verification picks up more signal when the verifying substrate's content-emphasis axis differs from the producing substrate's content-emphasis axis (because the verifying substrate is going deep on a different emphasis area and will catch gaps the producing substrate's emphasis missed). Cross-substrate verification picks up less signal when the verifying substrate's content-emphasis axis matches the producing substrate's (because both substrates have the same depth pattern and will miss similar gaps). The model gives the operator a predictable framing for designing cross-substrate verification chains rather than picking substrates by feel.

## What this changed in my workflow

Three specific changes. First, when I deploy a research prompt, I make two prompt-engineering decisions per substrate rather than one. I decide whether to provide structured framework or leave permissive (the framework-adherence axis decision); I decide whether to specify content focus or leave content-permissive (the content-emphasis axis decision). Each decision is informed by the substrate's axis assignments. The cost is two minutes of operator-side decision per deployment; the benefit is that the deployment is responsive to the substrate's predictable behavior rather than being a blind reach for whatever the substrate happens to produce.

Second, when I design a cross-substrate verification chain, I select substrates with different content-emphasis axis assignments rather than substrates that are simply different brand names. The substrate-style-differential value comes from the axis difference, not from the substrate-identity difference; two substrates with the same content-emphasis axis will catch similar gaps even if they are different brands, and two substrates with different content-emphasis axes will catch different gaps even if they are nominally close-brand. The selection criterion is axis-difference, which the two-axis model makes specifiable.

Third, when I deploy a substrate on a topic outside the topic surface where I have characterized the substrate's axis assignments, I treat the first deployment as a characterization deployment and run a second deployment to confirm the axis assignments hold. The within-decade dual-deployment configuration is the operational pattern: same content, different prompt-engineering, observe whether the substrate's axis behavior reproduces. If the axis behavior reproduces, the substrate's assignments hold on the new topic; if not, the substrate's behavior on the new topic is in an uncharacterized region and the operator should run more deployments before relying on substrate-axis predictions there.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the two-axis model is the only useful decomposition of substrate behavior. Substrate behavior has more than two dimensions; the literature on substrate behavior covers tone, factuality, refusal patterns, citation rigor, reasoning depth, instruction-following compliance, and many others. The two-axis model is the decomposition that emerged from the seven-decade research arc I ran; the decomposition is load-bearing for the kind of multi-substrate research workflow I do. Other decompositions are load-bearing for other workflows, and the two-axis model is not a replacement for those decompositions. The claim is that the two-axis decomposition is more useful than the one-axis substrate-style framing I used in earlier posts for the kind of work I have been doing.

I am not claiming the specific per-substrate axis assignments I observed will hold for substrates I have not characterized in this arc. The substrate landscape changes as substrate training and deployment evolve; an assignment that held in early June 2026 may not hold in late 2026 or in 2027. The model's predictive structure (two axes, observably independent, characterizable per substrate via dual-deployment evidence) is more durable than any specific assignment, but the assignments themselves are characterized for a specific substrate set at a specific time and need recharacterization as the substrates change.

I am not claiming the two-axis model is rigorously experimentally validated. The empirical foundation is thirty-eight-plus within-substrate cross-decade reproductions plus fourteen-plus within-decade dual deployments at a single operator's research arc on a single topic surface. The reproductions are evidence the operator-side patterns I observed are not random noise; the reproductions are not a controlled experiment that disentangles substrate-intrinsic patterns from operator-side prompt-engineering biases that may correlate with substrate assignments. A fuller validation would require multi-operator deployments across multiple topic surfaces with explicit prompt-randomization protocols, which is more work than a single operator-side arc supports. The model is methodology-research-grade rather than experiment-grade.

I am not claiming the within-decade dual-deployment pattern is the only way to characterize substrate axis assignments. The pattern is the operational shape that emerged in my arc because the arc's research scope included decade-scope variation and the variation supported dual-deployment configurations naturally. A research arc without decade-scope variation could characterize substrate axis assignments through other dual-deployment configurations (same-topic-different-framing; same-framing-different-topic; same-framing-same-topic-different-deployment-class). The within-decade dual deployment is the specific pattern that fit my arc; the principle (run two deployments with isolated variation to characterize substrate behavior under the variation) is general.

I am not claiming the substrate self-identification observation generalizes beyond the single case I observed. The case was: one substrate's first-stage response opened with an explicit axis-behavior-profile header that META-acknowledged its own pattern when operator-banked observations were provided in the reproduction-gate context. The observation is one case; it suggests a research direction (substrate self-knowledge as a prompt-engineering surface) that I am filing for follow-up. The single-case observation is not evidence that substrate self-identification reproduces across substrates or across prompt configurations; it is evidence that the configuration in which it occurred is worth studying further.

## What I am claiming

Substrate-style preservation, as a research-workflow design surface, decomposes into two independently observable axes. The framework-adherence axis describes what the substrate does with operator-provided structure. The content-emphasis axis describes what the substrate goes deep on under content-permissive prompts. The two axes are observably independent. The per-substrate assignments I characterized hold across decade-scope variation and across deployment-class variation.

If you are running a multi-substrate research workflow on a topic surface where the prompt-engineering decisions matter (which they do for any substantive arc), the question to ask before deploying is not which substrate but which axis assignment on which axis. Different substrates with different framework-adherence assignments respond differently to whether you provide a structured framework. Different substrates with different content-emphasis assignments go deep on different parts of the topic. The two-axis decomposition lets you ask both questions separately and answer them per substrate.

The two-axis model is doing more work for me than I expected when I surfaced it. It is replacing the one-axis substrate-style framings I used in earlier posts with a decomposition that actually predicts behavior. It is letting me design cross-substrate verification chains by axis-difference rather than by substrate-identity-difference. It is letting me characterize new substrates through dual-deployment evidence rather than through impressionistic feel.

The empirical foundation is thirty-eight-plus within-substrate cross-decade reproductions plus fourteen-plus within-decade dual-deployment observations at a single operator's research arc. The foundation is research-methodology-grade rather than controlled-experiment-grade. Multi-operator multi-topic dual-deployment validation is the open work that would extend it. The two-axis model is the methodology contribution this post commits to; the per-substrate assignments are working anchors that hold for the substrate set at the time the arc closed.
