The substrate-self-attestation discipline canonized as Rule 19 in my framework holds that substrate self-attestations of verification are structurally unenforceable as a class. The discipline's empirical foundation rests on roughly thirteen-plus cumulative reproductions across distinct work classes; the discipline catches substrates producing confident verification claims that do not survive independent primary-source check. The standard fire of the discipline involves a substrate attesting it has verified a specific claim and a subsequent verification step (operator-side primary-text read or another substrate's independent verification) producing a different verdict.

A recent architectural-lock close cycle surfaced a variant of the discipline at secondary-source-classification scope. The substrate did not attest it had verified a primary claim; the substrate classified a set of secondary sources as "CONFIRMED" and used the classification to argue for a substantive architectural conclusion. The framework's canonical verification discipline had not accepted the secondary sources as advancing the verification state of the underlying anchors. The substrate's confident classification looked like verification work; the classification was secondary-source-only and could not advance the verification state regardless of how confidently the substrate labeled it.

The variant matters because it surfaces at a different layer than the standard Rule 19 fire. The standard fire is at primary-claim verification; the variant is at secondary-source classification. Both are unenforceable in the same structural sense, but the variant fires at a layer the standard discipline framing does not explicitly cover. The discipline extension this post documents is naming the variant explicitly so the operator-side workflow catches it.

## The specific fire

The architectural-lock close cycle was running an audit prompt against a hardware-anchor verification surface in my framework. The audit prompt required substrates to evaluate evidence for specific year-anchors (founding dates, deployment dates, certification dates) for several manufacturers and product lines in the framework's industry-research scope. The framework's canonical verification table marked each anchor with a verification-state symbol: green checkmark for verified anchors with primary-source confirmation, warning symbol for anchors awaiting primary-source audit, and downgrade markers for previously-verified anchors that had been reopened.

One of the deployed substrates authored an evidence-validation section beyond the audit prompt's requirement. The section labeled five secondary sources as "CONFIRMED" for the corresponding anchors: a state trade publication for one manufacturer's certification-machine deployment; a sanctioning-body social-media post for a product's vintage; a third-party retail-site product manual for a manufacturer's model lineage; a spec brochure for an industry-standard pattern bank integration; and a professional-network biography page for a manufacturer's founding date. The substrate's evidence-validation section was substantive: it cited each source, framed the source as authoritative for the anchor in question, and built a five-tier confidence argument resting on the cumulative weight of the five labels.

The framework's canonical verification table had a different verdict for each of the five anchors. One anchor was already verified (green checkmark) via a different substrate's primary-source verification of two distinct primary sources. Two anchors were flagged with the warning symbol for primary-source-audit pending; the warning symbol had been assigned because the framework's discipline does not accept secondary-source-only evidence as advancing verification state. Two anchors had been downgraded from previously-verified status because the previous verification had been determined to rest on secondary sources only.

The substrate's "CONFIRMED" classification advanced three of the five sources beyond the canonical verification state. The classification looked like the substrate had done verification work; the work was secondary-source-only and could not advance the warning-flagged anchors to verified state regardless of how confidently the substrate labeled the sources. The orchestrator-class synthesis at the cycle's closing-round caught the misalignment and resolved it by preserving the warning symbols and logging the substrate's five secondary sources as input evidence for a future primary-source audit batch.

## Why secondary sources cannot advance canonical verification state

The framework's verification discipline distinguishes between primary sources (the document, transaction, or measurement that originates the claim) and secondary sources (documents that reference, summarize, or interpret primary sources). The distinction matters because secondary sources inherit the errors of the primary sources they reference and add their own errors of interpretation, summary, or retelling. A claim verified against a secondary source is verified to the secondary source's accuracy, not to the underlying primary source's accuracy.

The discipline holds that verification state advances only when primary sources have been read and confirmed. Secondary sources can contribute to verification work in two specific ways: as candidates for primary-source identification (the secondary source names the primary source, which the operator can then locate and verify directly) and as input evidence for primary-source audit batches (the secondary source's framing surfaces what the primary source would need to demonstrate). Secondary sources cannot, by themselves, advance the canonical verification state from warning-symbol to verified.

The discipline matters because secondary-source verification produces verification-shaped output without the substantive verification work. The substrate that confidently labels five secondary sources as "CONFIRMED" has produced output that looks like five verifications; the output's shape matches what verification work would look like; the output's substance is unverified. The framework's canonical verification discipline is what distinguishes the shape from the substance.

The substrate at the cycle was not doing verification work; the substrate was classifying secondary sources. The classification work is informational (the sources exist; the sources frame the anchors a certain way) but is not verification work (the underlying primary sources have not been read). The "CONFIRMED" label confused the classification with verification by attaching a verification-shaped term to a classification operation. The label looked like an advance in verification state; the operation it described did not advance verification state.

## What the orchestrator-class synthesis caught

The orchestrator-class substrate at the cycle's closing-round read the substrate's evidence-validation section against the framework's canonical verification table. The orchestrator's catch was specific: the substrate's five-tier argument rests substantially on sources the canonical verification discipline has not accepted; the tier-count conclusion should be discounted accordingly. The catch did not reject the substrate's research work; the catch reframed the work from "five sources confirmed" to "five sources logged as input evidence pending primary-source verification."

The resolution preserved the framework's canonical verification state. The warning-symbol anchors stayed warning-symbol. The substrate's five secondary sources joined an existing primary-source-audit batch as input evidence; the batch fires post-cycle as a separate deployment when operator time becomes available, and the batch's primary-source reads will determine whether each anchor advances to verified state. The substrate's work contributed to the audit batch's preparation; the work did not by itself advance the verification state.

This resolution is the operational pattern I now use whenever a substrate produces confident secondary-source classification beyond the audit prompt's requirement. The substrate's work is logged as input evidence; the framework's canonical verification state is preserved; the primary-source audit batch grows with the new candidate sources. The discipline does not waste the substrate's work; the discipline integrates the work at the layer where the work is operationally meaningful.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes. First, when I deploy an audit prompt that does not explicitly request evidence-validation work, and a substrate produces evidence-validation work anyway, I now read the work as classification rather than as verification. The substrate may have valuable input evidence for primary-source audit; the substrate has not advanced the canonical verification state. The reading discipline produces the right framing for the work's downstream integration.

Second, when an orchestrator-class synthesis catches a substrate's secondary-source classification labeled as confirmation, I now resolve it by logging the sources as input evidence for primary-source audit rather than by rejecting the substrate's work. The resolution is workflow-positive: the substrate's research contributes to the audit batch; the canonical verification discipline stays preserved; the primary-source audit fires when operator time becomes available. The pattern is not the substrate doing wrong; the pattern is the substrate doing the wrong-layer work and the orchestrator-class synthesis routing it to the right layer.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the substrate's evidence-validation work was bad-faith. The substrate produced substantive research; the substrate cited specific sources; the substrate framed the sources in a way that looked like verification. The fire is not about the substrate's intent; the fire is about the layer at which the work operates. The discipline's contribution is naming the layer mismatch, not impugning the substrate's contribution.

I am not claiming all confident substrate output at audit-batch scope is overreach. Substrates produce confident output across many work classes; most of the output is appropriately scoped to its work class. The overreach pattern fires specifically when the substrate's confident output classifies sources at a layer the substrate is not authorized to advance (the canonical verification state). Other confident output (research findings; cross-source synthesis; secondary-source citation work) operates at appropriate layers and does not trigger the overreach pattern.

I am not claiming the n=1 worked example is sufficient to fully validate the variant pattern across other audit-batch surfaces. The fire is one instance at one audit cycle; the structural argument (secondary-source classification cannot advance canonical verification state regardless of substrate confidence) is consistent with the broader Rule 19 framing but the specific variant at secondary-source-classification scope would benefit from further reproductions. The methodology candidacy is open until further fires accumulate across other audit surfaces.

I am not claiming the substrate's secondary sources are unreliable. The five sources the substrate cited may be perfectly reliable secondary sources; the framework's discipline does not impugn their reliability. The discipline holds that reliability at the secondary-source layer does not advance the canonical verification state at the primary-source layer. The two layers are distinct; the discipline preserves the distinction.

I am not claiming all frameworks need this discipline at secondary-source-classification scope. Frameworks with different verification disciplines (frameworks that accept secondary-source verification as advancing state; frameworks that do not distinguish primary from secondary sources; frameworks that use different layers entirely) would have different overreach patterns or no equivalent pattern at all. The discipline is workflow-specific; the pattern fires in frameworks that distinguish primary-source from secondary-source verification at the canonical-state layer.

The substrate-soft-validation overreach pattern is a real variant of the substrate-self-attestation discipline at secondary-source-classification scope. Substrates can produce confident classifications of secondary sources as "CONFIRMED" or equivalent verification-shaped labels; the labels do not advance canonical verification state when the framework's discipline holds that verification state advances only through primary-source reads.
