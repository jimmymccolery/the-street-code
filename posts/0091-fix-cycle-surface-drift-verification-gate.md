# When the Drift-Fix Drifts: A Counterintuitive Verification-Gate Pattern at n=2

**Author:** Jimmy McColery
**Date:** 2026-06-10

The cross-section reference drift discipline in my framework operates at four workflow surfaces: post-landing (when canonical state has just been written and downstream artifacts haven't yet caught up), pre-deployment self-audit (when an artifact is about to ship and the operator runs a final scan), pre-deployment canonical verification (when an automated check compares the artifact against the canonical record), and fix-cycle surface (when an iteration is amending a prior version's drift and the amendment itself introduces new drift). The first three surfaces are well-documented in the discipline's enumeration; the fourth surface is the subject of this post because the n=2 reproduction shape is counterintuitive against the naive expectation that drift-fixes settle the drift.

The fourth surface fired twice across architectural-lock close cycles in the framework's recent history. Each fire is small in scope and would be invisible without the explicit verification discipline that caught it; the cumulative pattern is what makes the surface worth naming.

## The two fires

The first fire happened at the Q14 architectural-lock close cycle. The orchestrator had amended iteration 3 of the lock content to fix prior drift; the amendment introduced new drift in the same operation. Specifically, the amendment substituted one non-canonical symbol name for another non-canonical symbol name. The original drift had been the use of a symbol that did not match the canonical reference; the fix-cycle drift was substituting a different symbol that also did not match the canonical reference. Both the original drift and the fix-cycle drift were equally non-canonical; the amendment did not converge toward the canonical name, it lateral-moved to a different non-canonical name.

The second fire happened at the Q11 architectural-lock close cycle. The iteration 3 framing of the architectural-section introduced two drift items while fixing prior framing. One drift was a schema-version assumption about how the architecture tracked its own state; the assumption did not match the canonical record's actual schema-versioning practice. The other drift was a location-confusion about which subsection of the document contained a mandatory persistence list; the iteration 3 framing referenced the wrong subsection number when redirecting downstream consumers to the list. Both drifts were introduced in the same amendment-cycle that was trying to fix prior framing drift; neither was present in the iteration 2 framing the amendment was correcting.

The two fires share a structural shape that is worth naming directly. The amendment cycle is an iteration that takes a prior version's known drift as input and produces a corrected version as output. The amendment cycle is not free of drift; the amendment cycle is an iteration like any other iteration, and iterations can introduce drift at their own output. The naive expectation is that an amendment cycle's discipline is high enough to prevent its own drift because the cycle's whole purpose is to fix drift; the empirical observation is that this expectation does not hold reliably.

## Why the naive expectation fails

The naive expectation is that an amendment cycle, because it is explicitly tasked with fixing drift, will hold the discipline higher than a normal iteration would. This expectation has the structural shape of "the doctor takes better care of their own health than the average patient does." Both the doctor expectation and the amendment cycle expectation are intuitive; both are wrong empirically.

The mechanism behind the failure is specific. The amendment cycle's attention is on the prior version's drift. The cycle reads the prior version, identifies the drift items, drafts corrections, and produces the amendment. The cycle's discipline is concentrated on the drift items it is correcting; the cycle's discipline is comparatively relaxed on the rest of the amendment, because the rest of the amendment is treated as "the fix" rather than as "new content that might itself drift."

This concentration-of-attention is the failure mode. Attention is a finite resource; when attention is concentrated on the drift items being corrected, attention is not available for the rest of the amendment. The rest of the amendment is where new drift creeps in, because the rest of the amendment is processed under the relaxed discipline that the concentration-of-attention produces.

The Q14 fire illustrates the mechanism cleanly. The orchestrator's attention was on the symbol-substitution correction. The correction itself was performed with high discipline; the symbol-substitution was tracked, the new symbol was selected, the substitution was applied. What was not tracked under high discipline was whether the new symbol matched the canonical reference. The substitution-as-correction was the focus; the canonical-match check was the silent assumption. The silent assumption did not hold, and the new symbol drifted from the canonical reference in a different direction than the original symbol had.

The Q11 fire illustrates a variant of the mechanism. The orchestrator's attention was on the framing correction. The correction itself was performed; the framing was rewritten; the framing's new content was the focus. What was not tracked under high discipline was whether the framing's new content introduced new claims that needed verification against the canonical record. The framing-as-correction was the focus; the new-content verification was the silent assumption. The silent assumption did not hold; the framing introduced two specific new claims (a schema-versioning assumption and a section-number reference) that did not match the canonical record.

The structural lesson is that the amendment cycle's attention has the same concentration-vs-distribution tradeoff that any iteration has. The amendment cycle is not a discipline-preserving operation by virtue of being an amendment; the amendment cycle is an iteration that produces output, and the output can drift like any other iteration's output. The verification gate that catches original-draft drift is also the verification gate that catches amendment-cycle drift. There is no special verification gate for amendment cycles, and there should not be; the verification gate runs at the amendment cycle's output the same way it runs at any iteration's output.

## What this changed in my workflow

The discipline I have committed to since the second fire is to run the verification gate at amendment-cycle output with the same intensity as I run it at original-draft output. The amendment cycle is not exempt from verification; the amendment cycle is a candidate for verification because the cycle's attention concentration produces a predictable surface where new drift creeps in.

The operational pattern is straightforward. After the amendment cycle produces the corrected version, I run the verification gate against the corrected version's canonical claims. The gate checks the corrected version against the canonical record at every reference point, not just at the points the amendment was correcting. The gate does not assume that the rest of the amendment is "the fix" and therefore drift-free; the gate treats the entire amendment as a new artifact that needs verification at every reference point.

The verification gate costs the same minute-or-two it costs at original-draft output. The benefit is catching the fix-cycle drift before it propagates into canonical state. Without the verification gate at amendment-cycle output, the fix-cycle drift becomes canonical and downstream consumers absorb it; with the verification gate, the drift is caught and the amendment cycle iterates once more before commit.

I also changed how I frame amendment cycles in my own mental model. The naive framing was that amendment cycles "fix drift" and therefore produce drift-free output. The corrected framing is that amendment cycles iterate on drift, and iterations can introduce new drift at their output. The framing matters because the discipline I bring to the amendment cycle depends on how I think the cycle relates to drift. The corrected framing produces verification-gate-at-output discipline; the naive framing produces verification-gate-skipping discipline because the amendment is treated as the verification itself.

## Where the pattern might fire beyond architectural-lock close cycles

The pattern's structural claim is that the verification gate at amendment-cycle output is load-bearing. The claim is workflow-specific to architectural-lock close cycles in my framework; the structural argument generalizes to other workflows where amendment cycles produce corrected versions of prior artifacts.

Other surfaces where the pattern is likely to fire include: code review with reviewer-requested changes (the iteration that addresses reviewer feedback can introduce new bugs in addressing the requested fixes); document revision under editorial feedback (the revision can introduce new errors while addressing the editorial corrections); legal contract amendment (the amendment language can introduce new ambiguities while fixing the prior ambiguities); architecture decision record (ADR) updates (the updated ADR can introduce new implicit assumptions while updating the prior decision); test failure remediation (the fix can introduce new test failures while addressing the failing test).

The structural shape is the same across these surfaces: the iteration's attention is on the items being corrected; the rest of the iteration is processed under relaxed discipline; new drift creeps in at the relaxed-discipline surface. The mitigation is the same: the verification gate runs at the iteration's output, not at the items the iteration was correcting.

The transferability has one specific limit. In workflows where the amendment cycle is itself audited externally (e.g., code review of a fix commit; legal review of a contract amendment; editorial review of a revision), the external audit can serve as the verification gate at amendment-cycle output. The workflows where the pattern fires hardest are workflows where the amendment cycle is internal-only and there is no external audit between the amendment and the canonical commit. In those workflows, the verification gate has to be operator-side discipline because no external check exists.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the n=2 reproduction is sufficient evidence for the pattern's full generalization. Two fires in one workflow context are evidence the pattern is not random noise; two fires are not evidence the pattern reproduces across workflows with different structural characteristics. The methodology candidacy is open until further fires accumulate across other architectural-lock close cycles and across other amendment-cycle workflows. The structural argument (concentration-of-attention produces a predictable surface for new drift) is consistent with the two fires but is not validated as a generalizable mechanism by them alone.

I am not claiming the verification gate at amendment-cycle output is the only discipline that catches the fix-cycle drift. Other disciplines could close the failure mode: pre-commit hooks that run drift checks against canonical state; automated tooling that diffs the amendment against the prior version and flags new references for verification; pair-programming-style review where a second person reads the amendment with fresh eyes. The verification gate is the discipline I have committed to because it fits my single-operator workflow and runs at minute-scale; other workflows with different structural constraints would close the failure mode through different mechanisms.

I am not claiming amendment cycles are inherently low-discipline. The amendment cycle's concentration-of-attention is a structural property of iteration, not a deficiency of the operator running the cycle. The mechanism produces predictable new-drift creep at any operator's amendment cycles; the discipline I have committed to is what offsets the mechanism, not what proves the operator is more careful than other operators. Reading the pattern as operator-deficiency rather than as structural-mechanism would lead to the wrong mitigation.

I am not claiming the fix-cycle-surface variant is qualitatively different from the other three drift surfaces. The post-landing, pre-deployment self-audit, and pre-deployment canonical verification surfaces are structurally related; they each occur at different points in the artifact's lifecycle and they each have characteristic failure modes. The fix-cycle surface is one more drift surface in the same family; the family's structural property (iteration produces output; output can drift) is what generalizes. The fix-cycle surface is worth naming separately because the naive expectation (drift-fix produces drift-free output) is widespread enough that the surface is silently overlooked.

I am not claiming the two fires were equally consequential. The Q14 fire's symbol substitution would have produced minor canonical-state drift; the Q11 fire's framing introduced two drift items including a section-number reference that would have produced downstream-consumer routing errors. The fires are similar in mechanism but different in consequence; lumping them together risks treating the pattern as uniformly low-stakes when one of the two fires had higher-stakes downstream implications. The discipline that catches both fires is the same; the consequences of missing each fire would have been different.

## What I am claiming

The fix-cycle surface is a real and reproducible drift surface that deserves its own verification gate. Across two architectural-lock close cycles in my framework, amendment cycles introduced new drift at the same time they were correcting prior drift. The structural mechanism is concentration-of-attention: the amendment cycle focuses on the drift items being corrected; the rest of the amendment is processed under relaxed discipline; new drift creeps in at the relaxed-discipline surface.

If you are running iterative work where amendment cycles produce corrected versions of prior artifacts, the surface I have described is the surface you are exposed to. The naive expectation that drift-fixes produce drift-free output does not hold reliably; the verification gate that catches original-draft drift is also the verification gate that catches amendment-cycle drift, and skipping the verification gate at amendment-cycle output is the discipline gap where new drift propagates into canonical state.

The discipline I have committed to is to run the verification gate at amendment-cycle output with the same intensity as I run it at original-draft output. The cost is small (a minute or two per amendment); the benefit is catching the fix-cycle drift before it propagates. The discipline does not require new tooling or new infrastructure; the discipline requires the operator-side framing that amendment cycles are iterations that produce output, not discipline-preserving operations that exempt their output from verification.

The pattern's n=2 reproduction is sufficient to argue the surface is real and the verification gate is load-bearing; the pattern's broader generalization to workflows beyond architectural-lock close cycles is open work that further reproductions would clarify. The structural argument (concentration-of-attention produces a predictable surface for new drift) is the methodology contribution; the two fires are the evidence the contribution is grounded in real workflow experience rather than in abstract structural reasoning.
