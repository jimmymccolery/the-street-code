**Author:** Jimmy McColery

---

This post leads a sequence about a specific discipline I had to develop the harder way. I built a design-theory framework for an indie tycoon sim's procedural-character architecture. The framework went through eight rounds of multi-substrate research between mid-May and mid-June 2026. Every round produced corrections that improved the framework's grounding. Some corrections were small (wrong venue, wrong page number, an over-included author in a citation chain). Some were structural (a collapsed emotion variable that needed to split into three; a wrong trait source for an encoding pathway; an uncorroborated universalist claim that was later corroborated when an orchestrator-class substrate re-read the primary text; a canonical record that was carrying stale wrong flags for four rounds before a primary-text re-read closed them). All of them mattered because each one changed implementation, not just bibliography.

The discipline this post publishes is the discipline that earned the corrections. Multi-substrate verification (the discipline of running the same research question across multiple AI substrates in parallel and adjudicating divergences) is necessary but not sufficient. Cross-substrate convergence is not proof. Primary-text grounding catches what substrate paraphrase chains miss when the chains converge on a misreading. Canonical records carry stale wrong flags that periodic primary-text re-reads must check. The orchestrator-class substrate (the substrate whose role is to read the other substrates' work for structural integrity) is where the catches happen most reliably when it actually opens the primary sources rather than reasoning over the substrate-produced citations.

The post that follows this one each take one correction from the retraction table below and walks through the implementation consequence. The retraction table is the substantive contribution of this lead post. The discipline that produced the table is the methodology I am committing to publicly because publishing it openly is what makes the framework accountable to the discipline.

## Why these errors mattered

The corrections were not bibliographic housekeeping. Each one changed implementation. Four examples are worth walking through in enough depth to show the shape.

The DC1 split (the row about splitting the single mood scalar into three) changed the schema. A single scalar produces a single equation for accumulator updates. Three separate scalars produce three equations and a different consolidation-phase summary form. The downstream implementation of the encoding pathway, the retrieval pathway, and the trait-modulation pathway all change shape under the split. The split was not a documentation update; the split was a re-specification of the architecture.

The HEXACO-to-attachment re-source (the row about trait source for relational encoding) changed which character attributes drive which encoding filters. Under the original (wrong) spec, a high-HEXACO-H character would produce richer relational encoding than a low-HEXACO-H character. Under the corrected spec, relational encoding richness is driven by attachment-avoidance and attachment-anxiety scalars; HEXACO-H drives cooperation and exploitation behaviors independently. The two pathways now use different trait sources because the literature is clear that they measure different things. The implementation of either pathway in isolation would have been the same under the wrong and right specs, but the cross-pathway interactions differ in ways that produce visibly different character behaviors at scale.

The Wiredu mapping drop (the row about Wiredu's sympathetic impartiality not being a HEXACO H trait coordinate) saved substantial V1 BUILD-side work. The estimate at the time of the drop was somewhere between twenty-five and seventy hours of code work that would have implemented the mapping; the drop eliminated that work. The drop also avoided an ornamental-deployment risk (the risk that V1 would cite Wiredu philosophically while implementing none of his framework's communal-accountability substance; the gap between citation and implementation is the harm). The drop is documented in a subsequent post as the load-bearing example of why procedural moral tests are not trait coordinates; the implementation consequence here is that the trait pipeline is now narrower and cleaner.

The position-(a) boundary discriminator change (the row about state-space content versus temporal extent) changed the operational discriminator at the audit layer. Under the original spec, the audit layer tested horizon depth as the primary check on whether a candidate architecture satisfied the framework's commitments. Under the corrected spec, the audit layer tests state-space content as the primary check (does the generative model contain a cross-era self-identity variable; if yes, the architecture fails the check regardless of horizon depth). Horizon depth is now a secondary defense-in-depth, not the discriminator. The mandatory test surface changed from a horizon-bound test to a state-space-tensor enumeration test, which is a different category of audit work and produces different failure modes.

The pattern across these four examples is consistent. The corrections changed implementation. The framework would have shipped differently if the corrections had not surfaced. The framework's empirical content is the implementation; bibliographic accuracy without implementation-level consequence would be a different kind of post.

## The methodology that caught these

Multi-substrate verification, the discipline the framework calls Rule 11, runs the same research question across multiple AI substrates in parallel and adjudicates divergences. The discipline was canonized in late May 2026 after enough cycles had reproduced the pattern that the discipline could be named. Rule 11 is necessary because single-substrate research routinely produces the substrate-paraphrase errors that show up in the citation-chain rows of the retraction table; running the same question across multiple substrates surfaces the divergences that single-substrate work cannot.

Rule 11 is not sufficient because cross-substrate convergence is not proof. Multiple substrates can converge on a wrong reading when the wrong reading is sticky enough to reproduce across substrate-paraphrase chains. The F-17 reversal (the Wiredu universalist row) is the cleanest example: across the prior arc, multiple substrates converged on "uncorroborated"; majority-vote synthesis would have ratified the wrong reading; the orchestrator-class substrate's primary-text re-read of the actual chapter was what reversed the canonical state.

The orchestrator-class substrate is the substrate whose role is to read the other substrates' work for structural integrity. In my framework's enumeration, the orchestrator-class substrate runs at a different reading depth than the per-question research substrates. The per-question substrates produce substantive research output against specific prompts; the orchestrator-class substrate reads the per-question output as a structural-integrity reviewer and is empowered to override the per-question consensus when primary-text evidence supports the override. The orchestrator-class substrate at the most recent round did exactly that: it opened the actual Wiredu chapter, read pages 193 and 199 directly, and identified what the prior arc's substrates had collectively missed.

Primary-text grounding is the discipline that closes the substrate-paraphrase-chain failure mode. The F-14 catch (the Friston 2017 Appendix F row) was a four-round canonical-record drift; multiple rounds of substrates had reasoned over a flagged-as-uncorroborated citation without anyone opening the primary text to actually verify; the verification when finally run took under thirty minutes and resolved the flag definitively. The drift had cost four rounds of architectural reasoning that proceeded under the wrong canonical state. The verification cost was small; the drift cost was substantial. The discipline I have committed to since the F-14 close is that flags older than two rounds get a mandatory primary-text re-read before they continue to constrain architectural reasoning.

The methodology lesson, sharper than "use multiple substrates," is this: even with cross-substrate convergence, primary-text re-reads at different reading depths can reverse canonical state. The discipline that catches the reversal is the orchestrator-class re-read; the discipline that prevents long-running drift is the periodic re-read of stale flags; the discipline that enables both is keeping the canonical record explicit enough that the re-read has a definite question to answer.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the retraction table is exhaustive. The eight-round arc produced more corrections than the sixteen rows above; the rows are the corrections that changed implementation or citation venue at the framework-canonical level. The corrections that surfaced and were ratified at the substrate-output level (e.g., a substrate's research-report draft contained a wrong reading that the orchestrator caught before the report consolidated into canonical state) are not in the table because they were caught before they entered the canonical record. The discipline that caught them is the same discipline; the table is the subset where the canonical record itself needed retraction.

I am not claiming the methodology is novel in its components. Multi-substrate verification has prior art across adversarial-review practices in industry and academia; primary-text grounding has prior art across the citation-discipline tradition in scholarly work; orchestrator-class re-read has prior art in editorial review workflows. The components are not novel. The specific operational shape (Rule 11 plus orchestrator-class primary-text re-reads plus periodic stale-flag re-checks as a co-operating discipline for solo-operator framework work) is the operational claim, and the operational claim is supported by the empirical reproduction at sixteen rows across eight rounds rather than by structural-novelty argument.

I am not claiming the framework would not have shipped without the corrections. The framework would have shipped; it would have shipped with the wrong specs. Some of the wrong specs would have produced visible game-feel issues at the implementation layer; some would have produced silently-wrong character behavior that a player might never notice but that an architecturally-aware critic could surface; some would have produced citations that an external academic critic could falsify in minutes. The cost of shipping without the corrections is not "the game does not work"; the cost is the visible-or-silent gap between the framework's claims and what the implementation actually does. The corrections close the gap.

I am not claiming the discipline scales without operator-attention overhead. Multi-substrate verification costs more than single-substrate work; primary-text grounding costs more than substrate-paraphrase reading; orchestrator-class re-read costs more than per-question reading. The cumulative operator-attention cost is real and is the constraint that makes the discipline operationally interesting. The discipline I have committed to includes the cost; I am not claiming the cost is small.

I am not claiming the canonical-record drift problem is solved. The discipline I have committed to (periodic stale-flag re-reads; orchestrator-class primary-text grounding; canonical-citations file as the working source-of-truth) reduces the drift's frequency and the drift's persistence but does not eliminate either. Future arcs will produce future drift; the question is whether the drift is caught before it propagates downstream. The discipline is what makes the catching more likely; the discipline is not a guarantee.
