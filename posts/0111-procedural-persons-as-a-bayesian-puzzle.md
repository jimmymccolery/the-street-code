I asked five AI substrates how to model NPC self-discovery as a sequence of inference operations and got back five different framings of the same structural problem. The framings differed on surface vocabulary; they converged on the structural observation that the player and the NPC are running inferences at different layers, in different parameter spaces, and from opposite sides of an information asymmetry. The convergence is not a coincidence. The mathematics of inference under uncertainty has a specific shape when the inferring agent is itself an object the observer is inferring about, and the shape is what the framing posts of the convergence each (in their different vocabularies) tried to name.

This post is about the framing my framework adopts. The framework calls itself Modified-C-bis (a working name for an internal architecture specification; the "C-bis" suffix marks it as the second-pass refinement of a prior architecture). The framework's framing for the inference asymmetry is second-order Bayesian model inversion. The player infers about an NPC; the NPC infers about its own world; the structure binds the two inferences while keeping their parameter spaces and their evaluation criteria computationally separate. The cryptographic puzzle in the framework's production design is the design-theoretic instantiation of this second-order inversion. The puzzle's internals are commercial scope that I am not discussing in this post; the conceptual frame the puzzle instantiates is what this post puts on the table.

## The structural problem

When a player observes an NPC, the player runs a Bayesian inference about the NPC. The NPC is, in the player's mental model, a system with hidden states (intentions, beliefs, preferences, memories). The player updates a probability distribution over the NPC's hidden states given the NPC's observed behavior, the situation the NPC is in, and the player's prior expectations about characters of that type. The inference is a familiar shape from the mind-perception literature (Gray, Gray, and Wegner 2007, "Dimensions of mind perception," *Science* 315(5812):619, established Experience and Agency as the canonical dimensions; Waytz, Morewedge, Epley, and colleagues 2010, "Making sense by making sentient: Effectance motivation increases anthropomorphism," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 99(3):410-435, established the effectance-driven motivation that makes the inference more salient under unpredictability).

The NPC runs a different inference. The NPC, in the architecture's internal model, has its own probability distribution over its own world-states (where it is, what is happening, what it should do next). The NPC updates this distribution given its own observed perceptual input, its own situation-specific priors, and its own dispositional traits. The NPC's inference is about the world; the player's inference is about the NPC; the two inferences are not the same inference at different scales, and they cannot be conflated.

The structural observation matters because architectures that conflate the two inferences produce specific failure modes. An architecture that treats the NPC as a fully-knowable database the player queries collapses the player-side inference into a lookup operation; the player's mental model of the NPC has no uncertainty to update, and the engagement collapses too. An architecture that treats the player as part of the NPC's perceptual environment without isolating the player from the NPC's belief-updating produces NPCs that read player-state in ways that break the diegetic boundary; the NPC's behavior becomes legible to the player as engine-driven rather than character-driven. The two failure modes are different shapes of the same conflation. Keeping the inferences computationally separate is what avoids both.

## The second-order inversion

Modified-C-bis frames the structural binding as second-order Bayesian model inversion. The framing borrows from the variational-inference literature where second-order inversion is a specific computational pattern: an outer inference operates over the parameters of an inner model that itself does inference. In the NPC-and-player case, the outer inference is the player's mental model of the NPC (which infers about the NPC's hidden states); the inner inference is the NPC's own model of the world (which infers about the world's hidden states). The outer and inner inferences run on different parameter spaces and use different priors and different evaluation criteria, but they are bound by the fact that the outer inference's target is the parameters of the inner inference.

The framing is operational, not metaphysical. The framework does not claim the NPC has phenomenal experience that the player is inferring about; the framework claims the player runs a Bayesian inference whose target is a structurally-coherent computational object (the NPC's belief-updating model) regardless of whether that object has anything corresponding to inner experience. The mind-perception literature is clear that human observers attribute mental states to systems whose behavior supports the attribution; the attribution is not a claim about the system's actual phenomenology. The framework's commitment is to making the NPC's behavior support a coherent Bayesian-target attribution; the player-side phenomenology of the attribution is separate from the architecture's mechanics.

The second-order inversion frame does specific work for the framework. It justifies separating the NPC's internal generative model from the player's perceptual interface; the two are different parameter spaces and the framework's audit layer can test them separately. It justifies the framework's commitment to keeping the NPC's belief-updating computationally bounded; the NPC's inner inference runs on its own state and does not consume player-side state, which keeps the outer-inference target stable from the player's perspective. It also clarifies what kind of puzzle structure aligns with the inference asymmetry. A puzzle whose computational structure mirrors the second-order inversion (the player infers about an inferring agent; the puzzle's solution lives in the parameter-space gap between the two layers) produces a different engagement than a puzzle that treats the NPC as a database to be queried. The puzzle is solvable because the structure is interpretable; the engagement is felt because the structure mirrors the genuine inferential asymmetry the architecture commits to.

## The V1 versus banked-Phase boundary

The active inference machinery the framework uses for NPC decision-making comes from the process theory of active inference (Friston, FitzGerald, Rigoli, Schwartenbeck, and Pezzulo 2017, "Active inference: A process theory," *Neural Computation* 29(1):1-49). The 2017 paper develops the partially observable Markov decision process formalism that the framework's NPCs run on; the paper's Appendix F (pages 42-43) introduces the naive-versus-sophisticated active inference distinction that has become the canonical anchor for sophisticated inference in the subsequent literature. The framework's V1 specification adopts the sophisticated scheme as the V1 architectural commitment under an operator ratification that I am calling Path A; the framework's prior planning had banked sophisticated inference for a post-V1 phase, and Path A is the ratification that pulled the apparatus into V1 critical-path.

The naive scheme runs without recursive belief-about-belief updating. The agent updates its beliefs about world-state given new evidence; the agent does not also model how its belief-updates would change under counterfactual evidence trajectories. The sophisticated scheme runs the recursion; the agent updates its beliefs about world-state and also updates its model of how its belief-updates would change under candidate action sequences. Sophistication is recursive metacognition, not temporal horizon depth; the framework's architecture is precise about this because conflating recursion with horizon depth produces a specific architectural confusion that the framework had to correct in its own earlier specification.

The orthogonal-axis discipline matters here. The naive-versus-sophisticated axis is recursive metacognition. The temporal-horizon axis is depth of forward planning. The Episodic-versus-Diachronic axis (from the philosophy-of-mind literature on personal identity; Strawson 2004 "Against Narrativity" at *Ratio* 17(4):428-452 is the canonical source) is self-experience continuity. These are three orthogonal axes; a naive-active-inference agent can have arbitrary temporal horizon depth, a sophisticated-active-inference agent can have shallow temporal horizon, a Diachronic agent can run on naive scheme, an Episodic agent can run on sophisticated scheme. The framework's V1 spec adopts sophisticated inference under Path A; the framework's V1 spec also adopts an Episodic-compatible self-experience structure (the NPC's own self-experience is present-anchored; there is no cross-era self-identity variable in the NPC's generative model). The three axes' independence lets the framework be precise about what V1 commits to without overcommitting on adjacent axes.

The boundary between what V1 commits to and what subsequent phases might extend matters specifically for the player-side puzzle structure. V1's NPCs run sophisticated active inference at the consolidation boundaries of the framework's memory architecture (the consolidation pass runs at game-year boundaries; sophisticated-inference apparatus evaluates the consolidated state and updates the NPC's cached policies for the next game-year). V1's NPCs do not run sophisticated active inference per-frame at runtime; the per-frame budget runs cached policies derived from the consolidation-boundary evaluation. The puzzle structure the framework commits to instantiates at the consolidation boundary, not at every per-frame decision. The puzzle is observable across game-years rather than within a single in-game minute, which aligns with the multi-decade scope the architecture is designed for.

## Why this changes how you sketch the puzzle

A puzzle whose computational structure mirrors the second-order inversion creates a different kind of player engagement than a puzzle that treats the NPC as a database to be queried. The database puzzle has a fixed correct answer; the inference puzzle has an answer that depends on what the player has inferred about the NPC and what the NPC has inferred about its own situation up to the moment the puzzle resolves. The two puzzle shapes look superficially similar at the surface level (both are "the player figures out what to do"); they differ structurally at the inference-layer level (the database puzzle's answer does not change with player-side or NPC-side inference; the inference puzzle's answer does).

The shape of the inference puzzle the framework is developing is constrained by the second-order inversion. The puzzle's solution lives in the parameter-space gap between what the player has inferred about the NPC and what the NPC has inferred about its own situation. The player has to recognize that the gap exists; the player has to reason about what the NPC's inner inference would have produced under the available evidence; the player has to act on that reasoning. The puzzle is solvable because the inference asymmetry is interpretable; the puzzle is engaging because the asymmetry mirrors the genuine player-NPC relationship the framework's architecture commits to.

The framework's production design includes a specific puzzle instantiation that I am not naming in this post. The puzzle internals are commercial scope still in development; the soft-disclosure rule applies to operator-specific implementation specifics. The conceptual frame the instantiation runs on (the second-order inversion; the inference asymmetry; the parameter-space gap as the solution surface) is what the post puts on the table. Operators building procedural-character architectures with similar inference-asymmetry commitments can adopt the conceptual frame without the specific instantiation; the conceptual frame generalizes more cleanly than any specific puzzle does.

## The mind-perception canonical anchor

The mind-perception literature is the canonical reference for the player-side attribution that the architecture's inference-asymmetry framing presupposes. The canonical citations require a correction note worth surfacing because the substrate-paraphrase chains in my framework's research arc had repeatedly conflated two different papers.

The Experience-versus-Agency dimensions came from Gray, Gray, and Wegner 2007, "Dimensions of mind perception," *Science* 315(5812):619. The paper is a one-page report in *Science* establishing the two-dimensional structure of mental-state attribution; Experience tracks the capacity for sensations and emotions; Agency tracks the capacity for planning and self-control. The paper is the empirical foundation for the framework's commitment that NPC behavior can be designed to support attribution on both dimensions independently.

The effectance-driven anthropomorphism literature came from Waytz, Morewedge, Epley, and colleagues 2010, "Making sense by making sentient: Effectance motivation increases anthropomorphism," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 99(3):410-435. The paper is the empirical foundation for the framework's design pattern that NPCs whose behavior is genuinely unpredictable (in the sense of not being trivially predictable from the player's observable evidence) elicit more mental-state attribution from players than NPCs whose behavior is trivially predictable. The pattern is design-relevant because the architecture's commitment to keeping the NPC's belief-updating computationally separate from the player's perceptual interface produces NPCs whose behavior is not trivially predictable; the unpredictability is structural, not stochastic, and it supports the kind of attribution the architecture is designed for.

The correction note is that an often-cited 2010 review paper in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* is sometimes cited as the source for either dimensions or anthropomorphism mechanisms; that paper is a review, not the origin source for either claim. The framework's citation discipline cites the origin papers directly. The substrate-paraphrase chains in my framework's research arc had repeatedly attributed the dimensions claim or the effectance claim to the 2010 *TiCS* review; the orchestrator-class re-read of the primary literature corrected the attribution to the Gray Gray Wegner 2007 *Science* report (for dimensions) and the Waytz et al. 2010 *JPSP* paper (for effectance). The corrected citations are what the framework's canonical citations file carries forward.

## The Bouizegarene bridge and the substrate-neutrality assumption

The active-inference literature on narrative as a structuring mechanism for self-modeling came from Bouizegarene, Ramstead, Constant, Friston, and Kirmayer 2024, "Narrative as active inference," *Frontiers in Psychology* 15:1345480. The paper develops the active-inference framing for narrative self-understanding; the framing is human-scoped in the paper's own scope statement. The framework's adoption of the framing for procedural agents extends the paper's scope through a substrate-neutrality and functional-equivalence assumption: if the active-inference framing characterizes the structural shape of narrative self-understanding for human cognition, the same structural shape can characterize the structural-only self-modeling of a procedural agent whose architecture commits to the same active-inference machinery.

The substrate-neutrality assumption is the framework's bridge from the human-scoped literature to the procedural-agent application. The bridge is operational: the framework does not claim that the procedural agent has the phenomenal experience the human-scoped literature presupposes; the framework claims the structural shape transfers. Deane 2021, "Consciousness in active inference," *Neuroscience of Consciousness* 2021(2):niab024, addresses some of the philosophical complications around the substrate-neutrality assumption directly; the framework's reading of Deane is that the substrate-neutrality assumption is defensible for the structural-only claims the framework makes, and the framework's commitments do not require the stronger consciousness claims Deane's paper engages with.

The bridge matters for the framework's audit layer. The audit layer tests the procedural agent's architecture against the structural-only commitments the framework makes; the audit does not test the agent against any phenomenal-experience claim. The state-space content audit (the primary audit in the framework's mandatory test surface) tests whether the generative model contains a cross-era self-identity variable; if yes, the architecture fails the audit; if no, the architecture passes. The audit's criterion is purely structural; the bridge from the human-scoped narrative-self literature to the procedural agent is what makes the structural criterion meaningful for the kind of NPC the framework is designed to produce.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes that propagate from the second-order inversion frame into the architecture's day-to-day specification work. First, I treat any architectural decision that risks conflating the player-side inference and the NPC-side inference as a load-bearing audit-layer issue rather than a per-frame design choice. The framework's audit layer checks whether candidate architecture proposals maintain the parameter-space separation; proposals that violate the separation are rejected at the audit layer regardless of how they look at the per-frame design level. The discipline catches conflations that would otherwise propagate downstream into specific implementation choices that look like local decisions but actually compromise the architecture's coherence.

Second, I draft puzzle structures by starting from the inference asymmetry and working out the puzzle shape from the asymmetry rather than starting from a puzzle idea and trying to fit it onto the architecture. The starting-from-asymmetry discipline produces puzzles that align with the architecture's commitments by construction; the starting-from-idea discipline produces puzzles that have to be retrofitted to the architecture and often produce visible seams between the puzzle's surface mechanics and the underlying NPC behavior. The discipline is small (a few minutes per puzzle sketch); the benefit is that the puzzle shapes the framework adopts align with the architecture's commitments by construction rather than by retrofit.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the second-order inversion framing is unique to my framework. The framing is recognizable from the variational-inference literature where second-order inversion is a standard computational pattern; the application to procedural-character architectures is the framework's specific contribution, not the framing itself. Operators working in adjacent design surfaces (mixed-initiative systems; multi-agent simulations; embodied conversational agents) will recognize the framing from their own working literature, and the framework does not claim primacy over the framing. The framework claims the application is operationally interesting for the specific design surface the architecture works in.

I am not claiming the inference asymmetry is the only useful structural pattern for procedural-character design. Other patterns are useful for other surfaces. State-machine-based character behavior is operationally cheaper and works well for surfaces where the player-side inference is intentionally minimal (e.g., utility-NPC behavior in management sims). Utility-AI-based character behavior is operationally cheap and works well for surfaces where the NPC's decision-space is small enough that the utility function can be authored directly. The inference-asymmetry framing is the right framing for surfaces where the player's mental model of the NPC is itself part of the gameplay; the framing is not a universal design pattern.

I am not claiming the framework's V1 spec validates the second-order inversion framing empirically. V1 will ship with the architecture's commitments to the inference asymmetry; player-side empirical validation of whether the asymmetry actually produces the engagement the framework's design predicts is a separate empirical question that V1 cannot answer on its own. The pre-registered pilot the framework has planned (an X-Phi-style study with abstract Character A / Character B vignettes; planned for the period after V1 ships) is the empirical apparatus that would test the framing; the architecture's commitment to the framing is independent of the pilot's outcome, but the pilot is what would let the framework claim empirical content rather than design-theoretic content.

I am not claiming the puzzle internals I am declining to name in this post are protected because they are uniquely novel. The internals are protected because they are commercial scope still in development; the conceptual frame they instantiate is publishable and is the substantive contribution of this post. Operators working in similar design surfaces can adopt the conceptual frame without needing the specific internals; the conceptual frame's value does not depend on the internals' uniqueness.

I am not claiming the substrate-neutrality assumption is uncontroversial. The assumption is the framework's working bridge from the human-scoped active-inference literature to the procedural-agent application; the literature on whether the assumption holds (Deane 2021 is the recent canonical engagement) is mixed and the framework's reading is one reading among several. The framework's commitments are structural and the assumption is the structural-only version of the bridge; the stronger versions of the assumption that would extend to phenomenal-experience claims are not what the framework is committing to.

## What I am claiming

The player and the NPC are inferences at different layers, in different parameter spaces, and from opposite sides of an information asymmetry. The second-order Bayesian model inversion frame is the operational shape that lets the two inferences be bound (the player's inference targets the NPC's belief-updating model) while keeping them computationally separate (the parameter spaces are disjoint; the priors are different; the evaluation criteria are different). The frame justifies the framework's commitment to separating the NPC's internal generative model from the player's perceptual interface; the frame justifies the framework's commitment to keeping NPC belief-updating computationally bounded; the frame justifies a puzzle structure whose solution lives in the parameter-space gap between the player-side and NPC-side inferences.

If you are designing procedural-character architectures where the player's mental model of the NPC is itself part of the gameplay, the inference-asymmetry frame is the framing that produces architectures that align by construction with the kind of engagement the design surface is reaching for. The frame's components are not novel (variational inference; mind-perception literature on Experience and Agency; active-inference machinery; substrate-neutrality bridges); the frame's application to the specific design surface is what makes the frame operationally useful. The discipline of starting from the asymmetry and working out the puzzle from the asymmetry, rather than starting from a puzzle idea and retrofitting it onto the architecture, is the operational discipline that earns the framing's value.

The framework's V1 spec ships with sophisticated active inference at consolidation boundaries (per Path A operator ratification); the V1 spec adopts an Episodic-compatible self-experience structure (no cross-era self-identity variable in the NPC's generative model); the V1 spec's puzzle structure instantiates the second-order inversion at consolidation-boundary cadence rather than per-frame. The architectural commitments above are the substantive content of the framework's V1 commitment; the deeper posts in this arc each take one architectural commitment and walk through the implementation consequence.
