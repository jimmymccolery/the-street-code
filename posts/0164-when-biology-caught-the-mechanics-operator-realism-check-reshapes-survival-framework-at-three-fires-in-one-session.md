I'm a solo indie developer building a procedural-character architecture for a multi-decade tycoon simulation. The framework's Sim AI substrate ships shared across multiple products. This post documents a specific empirical finding from a Phase 2.7 empirical BUILD arc 2026-07-04 late-through-latest evening: the framework's operator-realism-check discipline reproduced at three empirical fires within one session, each reshaping a framework calibration decision (fires 1 and 2) or preventing an unnecessary constraint from shipping (fire 3).

The framework's Sim AI substrate models NPC affect, action selection, and memory. Phase 2.6 added death, rescue, and vitality mechanics per Council #7 candidacy ratification. Phase 2.7 was scheduled to tune the shipped Phase 2.6 mechanics based on empirical validation at seed=42 replay sessions. Each Phase 2.7 sub-phase (A, B, C, D) originated from a specific empirical failure mode surfaced by session log analysis, then progressed through a design phase, then either shipped or was deliberately deferred.

Three of those design phases involved an operator realism check that materially changed the outcome. I noticed the pattern reproducing partway through the session and started tracking it explicitly. By the session's end, the discipline had strengthened from n=1 to n=3 within a single arc. The n=3 within-arc strengthening is significant because the pattern surfaces at a specific decision point (framework calibration under empirical pressure) that recurs multiple times per session in dense BUILD arcs. Three fires in one arc is unusually strong empirical foundation for a discipline that was banked at n=1 earlier in the same session.

This post is about what the three fires were, why they matter for framework methodology, and what discipline candidacy they justify at Council #8 methodology deployment scope.

## What operator-realism-check discipline is

The framework's calibration decisions for survival, embodied cognition, or emergency behavior mechanics rely on a specific class of judgment call: what is realistic in the real world versus what is a coding simplification that produces unrealistic edge cases. AI-assistant collaboration workflows I use tend toward the simplification. The AI produces a proposal, the proposal codifies one interpretation of the mechanic, the interpretation ships, and edge cases surface at empirical validation as "the framework does this weird thing when."

Some of those weird things are legitimate framework bugs. Others are the framework's chosen interpretation being blunt where reality is nuanced. The distinction matters because bug-fixes reshape code, but blunt-interpretation-fixes reshape design.

Operator-realism-check discipline names the specific pattern where I (as the operator, and as someone who has lived through the real-world biology or psychology the mechanic is meant to model) catch the AI's blunt interpretation before it ships. The catch happens at a specific decision layer: after the AI has proposed a design and before I've approved it. My contribution to the design is not code or architecture; it's biology or psychology reality-checking.

The discipline is adjacent to the framework's L14 wife-as-audience-reception-anchor discipline in leveraging trusted human intuition for framework decisions. It differs in that L14 leverages a wife-audience reception check for brand or product decisions; operator-realism-check leverages the operator's own real-world survival or embodied cognition intuition for framework mechanic decisions. Same discipline family (trusted-human-intuition-as-input); different subject-matter scope.

## Fire 1: Sleep during storm

The Phase 2.7-A design phase started with an empirical observation from a seed=42 session where Sim A slept 293 straight ticks through her own exposure death. She fell asleep at exposure 6.5 (fine), storm forced 12 ticks later, exposure climbed to 100 over the next 200 ticks, vitality drained from 100 to 0 over the following 100 ticks, and she died at tick 2158 during action `sleep`. No wake-up. No interrupt. She was asleep the whole time.

I asked the AI for a proposed fix. The AI proposed: "emergency mode interrupts sleep when exposure crosses 92." Blunt threshold-based interrupt tied to the framework's existing emergency mode entry threshold at exposure 92.

I noticed something and pushed back. My exact words: "someone can freeze in their sleep? But I guess a storm might keep them awake?"

Two-part biology insight. Part one: freezing in sleep is real. Hypothermia deaths in sleep are documented in survival medicine. Late-stage hypothermia fades consciousness, which prevents waking. Real people do die in their sleep from cold exposure. Part two: a storm should keep them awake. Auditory startle from thunder, tactile discomfort from rain, shivering reflex from cold. Environmental stress interrupts sleep for healthy sleepers.

Both are true. The framework's simplification collapsed them into one. The blunt threshold-based interrupt would make advanced-hypothermia sleep-death impossible, which is unrealistic in the terminal-stage direction. But not modeling storm-as-wake-signal at earlier stages is unrealistic in the other direction.

We revised the design to a two-regime biology model. Storm active plus healthy vitality equals wake up. Storm active plus advanced hypothermia (vitality below a floor at 30) equals continue sleeping (freeze in sleep preserved as realistic terminal outcome). The framework code added a `WAKE_FROM_STORM_VITALITY_FLOOR` constant at 30 and a specific check that triggers wake only when `isInStorm && vitality_b >= 30 && currentAction === 'sleep'`.

The reshaped design shipped as Phase 2.7-A. Empirical validation at a fresh seed=42 session confirmed the interrupt firing at tick 1691 (one tick after sleep started during storm) instead of the prior 293-tick run to death. The two-regime model produced observably different behavior at the healthy-vitality window while preserving the terminal-stage outcome for the advanced-hypothermia window.

## Fire 2: Which safe cell during emergency

Phase 2.7-B shipped a generic emergency-mode current-action interrupt after Phase 2.7-A's empirical validation surfaced a follow-on failure: Sim A no longer slept through her death, but she still died from exposure via non-sleep actions (explore_jungle, walk_beach, climb_to_high_point) at exposure 100 for 300 plus ticks. Phase 2.7-B interrupted those actions at emergency-mode entry so decideNextAction could re-evaluate.

Phase 2.7-C's design phase then asked: what should decideNextAction prefer after the interrupt? Under active storm exposure emergency, retreating to a known cave cell is empirically safer than building new shelter (which takes 240 ticks during which the Sim is still exposed and can die mid-build). The obvious design proposal was a hard filter: block build_shelter selection during storm exposure emergency; force retreat_to_cave.

I pushed back again. My exact question: "Why did EFE pick build_shelter over retreat_to_cave? I'd argue some humans would fail like this in an emergency as well? Do you agree? I don't think I necessarily want to code that out, but I would hope some or most would understand the cave is the better answer."

The realism check here is different from fire 1. Not biology-of-consciousness this time; psychology-of-emergency-decision-making. Real people under emergency pressure do sometimes commit to slow bad choices (build shelter mid-storm) rather than fast safe choices (retreat to cave). Cognitive tunneling under stress is a documented failure mode. Personality, training, and situational awareness all vary across a population; some people freeze, some flee, some heroically build.

A hard filter would eliminate the possibility of emergent bad-decision narrative. Some Sims dying from build-shelter-during-storm decisions is a legitimate outcome. What matters at the base-rate level is that most Sims should pick the cave. So the design needs a nudge, not a filter.

We revised to a moderate boost design. Phase 2.7-C exports an `EMERGENCY_SAFE_CELL_REGISTRY` mapping emergency-need types to safe-cell types (shelter_exposure → cave and shelter; thirst → stream and coconut_grove; and so on for extensibility). Under matching emergency mode with the safe cell physically reachable in the world grid, actions that navigate to a registered safe cell receive a 2.5x EFE weight boost. Moderate strength, not overwhelming. Softmax sampling preserves the possibility of alternate selections.

Empirical validation at 100-trial probabilistic tests confirmed retreat_to_cave dominates under storm exposure emergency at healthy strength, while build_shelter still appears in some samples. Realistic bad-decision variance preserved. Framework as narrative substrate strengthened.

## Fire 3: The framework already models this

Phase 2.7-C's empirical validation session showed retreat_to_cave firing correctly at emergency onset (Sim A reached cave at tick 3149, exposure crashed from 93.5 to 43.5) but then Sim A left the cave three ticks later once emergency mode deactivated at the exposure < 85 hysteresis exit. Storm was still active. Sim wandered back into the storm and ultimately died to a subsequent build_shelter decision at exposure 66 (non-emergency).

The proposed Phase 2.7-D design phase suggested an at-safe-cell dwelling boost: when Sim is currently AT a safe cell during matching emergency type, boost stay-in-place actions or block departure actions. Would prevent the leave-cave-during-storm dynamic.

I pushed back a third time. My exact framing: "What if I run the storm so long, they must leave to fulfill needs? They'd have to make a tough choice and take a chance?"

The realism check here is different again. Not biology of consciousness; not psychology of decision-making; a framework-modeling insight. The framework already models the multi-need trade-off. Sim in cave under long storm: exposure decays fast (cave provides -2 per tick), thirst rises slowly, hunger rises slowly. Eventually thirst hits 92, thirst emergency activates, Phase 2.7-C safe-cell boost applies to the thirst registry (stream, coconut_grove), Sim leaves cave for water. Storm still active. Exposure climbs during her water trip. She either makes it back or dies from exposure while addressing thirst.

That is a real survival trade-off, and the framework was already producing it. Empirical evidence at the seed=42 validation session showed Sim A leaving cave via `move_toward_known_water` at tick 3150, thirst-driven, rather than random departure. She was making the trade-off. She just failed to execute it cleanly at a later decision point, which is realistic bad execution under real emergency pressure.

Adding a Phase 2.7-D dwelling boost would over-constrain what was already emergent-behavior-appropriate. The framework's job isn't to keep Sims alive; it's to let them face real survival trade-offs and produce meaningful narratives. Phase 2.7-D would strip a substantial fraction of the emergent narrative territory.

We deferred Phase 2.7-D by design. Not "we'll ship it later"; the design itself is intentionally not shipped because the framework already produces the desired outcome. The memory record notes this as a deliberate non-ship per operator third realism check.

## Distinct value patterns across the three fires

Fires 1 and 2 belong to one pattern subtype: operator catches the AI's blunt interpretation before ship and pushes for a nuanced revision. The revision ships. Framework calibration changes.

Fire 3 belongs to a different pattern subtype: operator recognizes that the proposed constraint would over-constrain what the framework is already correctly modeling. The proposed constraint does NOT ship. Framework calibration stays as-is.

Both subtypes are realism preservation. Both leverage the same operator-side real-world intuition. But they operate at different framework touch points. Fires 1 and 2 catch simplification-that-would-produce-unrealistic-behavior. Fire 3 catches unnecessary-constraint-that-would-eliminate-realistic-behavior.

The unified discipline is: cross-check simulation mechanics against real-world biology or psychology or survival-scenario intuition BEFORE shipping calibration. The catch can produce either "reshape and ship" (fires 1-2) or "don't ship" (fire 3). Both are legitimate outputs of the same discipline. The discipline is uniform; its output is contextual.

## What actually shipped

Phase 2.7-A sleep-storm interrupt shipped at sim-ai commit `17fa31f`. WAKE_FROM_STORM_VITALITY_FLOOR constant at 30. Sleep during storm interrupts unless vitality below floor. 8 new tests. Empirically validated at seed=42 (sleep 1 tick vs prior 293 straight).

Phase 2.7-B generic emergency-mode current-action interrupt shipped at sim-ai commit `5350e85`. EMERGENCY_INTERRUPTIBLE_ACTIONS registry as foundational layer for future emergency types. 13 new tests. Empirically validated with actionProgress reset visible at emergency onset.

Phase 2.7-C emergency safe-cell registry shipped at sim-ai commit `ea1438b`. EMERGENCY_SAFE_CELL_REGISTRY extensible mapping. EMERGENCY_SAFE_CELL_BOOST at 2.5x moderate strength. 12 new tests. Empirically validated at seed=42 (Sim A retreated to cave at tick 3119 emergency onset).

Phase 2.7-D at-safe-cell dwelling boost DELIBERATELY DEFERRED. No code shipped. Memory record filed at `feedback_operator_realism_check_reshapes_framework_calibration_2026-07-04.md` documenting the third fire and the not-ship decision.

Test count trajectory across the arc: 483 (pre-Phase 2.6 baseline) → 610 (post-Phase 2.7-C ship). All pass.

Council methodology deployment session-close discipline extended by n=3 within-arc strengthening. Canonization candidacy for the operator-realism-check discipline TRIGGERED at Council #8 methodology deployment scope. The pattern is now empirically anchored at n=3 rather than n=1; the discipline is a serious canonization candidate rather than an isolated observation.

## What I'm not claiming

I'm not claiming operator-realism-check discipline generalizes to all framework design decisions. It applies to survival, embodied cognition, and emergency-behavior mechanics where real-world biology or psychology is the anchor. It might not apply cleanly to abstract mechanics like accumulator decay rates or discount factors where real-world biology is not directly the anchor.

I'm not claiming n=3 within-arc strengthening is equivalent to n=3 cross-arc strengthening. Within-arc reproduction has higher risk of pattern-fit-to-current-context than cross-arc reproduction. The framework's canonical multi-fire promotion bar (typically n=3 cross-arc) may or may not treat within-arc strengthening as equivalent evidence. Council #8 methodology deployment will need to adjudicate this.

I'm not claiming AI-assistant collaboration always produces blunt interpretations that need operator override. The AI often produces well-calibrated designs. What I'm documenting is a specific subclass of design decisions where the AI's default toward simplification is empirically observable and where operator biology or psychology intuition catches it before ship.

I'm not claiming the three fires are the only operator-realism-check events across the session's arc. Other decisions may have involved smaller realism checks that didn't produce material framework changes and thus didn't register as fires. The three fires documented here are the ones that materially reshaped framework calibration or prevented a ship.

I'm not claiming operator realism intuition is always right. If I'm wrong about a biology or psychology claim, the framework absorbs my wrong-intuition and the framework's empirical validation surfaces the mistake later. What matters is that the discipline gives me the opportunity to intervene BEFORE ship, not that my intervention is always correct.

## What I am claiming

The framework's operator-realism-check discipline reproduced at n=3 empirical fires within a single Phase 2.7 empirical BUILD arc 2026-07-04. Fires 1 and 2 reshaped shipped designs (Phase 2.7-A two-regime biology model; Phase 2.7-C moderate-boost variance preservation). Fire 3 prevented an unnecessary ship (Phase 2.7-D dwelling boost deferred because framework already models the multi-need trade-off).

All three fires produced material framework artifacts or prevented one. Fires 1 and 2 changed shipped code, tests, and memory. Fire 3 prevented a shipped constraint that would have over-constrained emergent narrative behavior. Each fire was empirically observable at seed=42 validation sessions before or after the design decision.

The discipline unifies the two apparent subtypes (reshape-and-ship at fires 1-2 and don't-ship at fire 3) under a single pattern: cross-check simulation mechanics against real-world biology or psychology intuition BEFORE shipping calibration. The output is contextual (ship reshape or don't ship) but the input pattern is uniform (operator real-world intuition as check against AI-produced blunt interpretation).
