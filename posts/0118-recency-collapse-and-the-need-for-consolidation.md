The framework I am developing lets procedural characters live through thirty game-years of weekly interactions with a multi-NPC population. Naively storing every interaction event would crash the save file at iOS V1 budget; naively summarizing them would flatten the texture that makes characters feel like they have lived through time. The framework's job is to thread the needle: bounded memory at production scale; preserved texture across multi-decade arcs; deterministic save-load behavior; per-frame performance within tight budgets. This post is about how the framework threads the needle.

The needle-threading mechanism the framework adopts is a tiered memory architecture with a consolidation pass that runs at game-year boundaries. Raw event memory lives in Layer 1 with a bounded recency window; consolidation promotes core events to Layer 2 (encoded-and-reconstructed perspective memory); everyday events decay via a 0.995-per-game-hour anchor; the accumulator (Layer 3) integrates Layer 1 and Layer 2 contributions into a running affective state per the framework's DC1 split (valence, arousal, stress as independent scalars). The 0.995 anchor is the load-bearing technical commitment that makes the architecture computationally tractable at multi-decade scale; the consolidation pass is what preserves the texture without exploding the storage cost.

The post walks through the failure modes the architecture is designed to prevent (naive recency collapse; premature compression that flattens texture), the tiered architecture's specific mechanics, the 0.995 anchor's empirical inspiration and design-choice rationale, the decision rule for what gets consolidated versus what decays, and the integration with the framework's broader hybrid-path commitments (cached policies at runtime; sophisticated active inference at consolidation boundaries; multi-graph memory typed indexes for fast retrieval; LLM-backed observation/reflection only at consolidation triggers, not per-interaction). The integration is what makes the tiered architecture V1-shippable; the integration is not novel in any individual element; the integration's coherence is what the framework contributes.

## The recency-collapse problem

The arithmetic at the framework's design surface is sharp. A multi-decade tycoon sim with tens-of-NPCs scale interactions across thirty game-years produces approximately one to two million interaction events at the framework's target interaction density (the specific multiplication depends on per-NPC interaction frequency and game-week duration; the order-of-magnitude is what matters for the architecture's scaling considerations). At a hundred bytes per event (a conservative estimate for the framework's event-record schema), the raw storage cost is approximately one hundred to two hundred megabytes per character; the cost multiplied across the NPC population produces storage requirements that exceed the iOS V1 budget envelope by an order of magnitude or more.

The retrieval cost compounds the storage problem. Naive retrieval against the raw event store is O(N) over the event count; mood-congruent retrieval (Eich 1995 "Searching for mood dependent memory," *Psychological Science* 6(2):67-75, is the canonical anchor for the mood-congruence pattern the framework adopts at the retrieval layer) requires scanning the event store for events matching the current affective state, and the scan cost makes the retrieval impractical at the framework's interaction density. The framework's per-frame performance budget (approximately 33 milliseconds per frame at the iOS V1 target framerate) cannot absorb O(N) retrieval costs at scale; the per-frame budget would be exhausted by the retrieval pathway alone, leaving nothing for the rest of the per-frame pipeline.

The naive growth makes the architecture fail to deliver the multi-decade promise. The framework's design surface commits to characters whose accumulated experience is observable to the player across decades; the design surface requires the architecture to preserve enough event content that the accumulation is meaningful, and to scale that preservation across the multi-decade scope without crashing the storage budget or the retrieval budget. The naive raw-event-store approach does neither; the architecture has to do better.

## The premature-compression failure mode

The alternative failure mode is aggressive compression that throws away the texture. The framework could replace all 1950s-decade interactions with a single per-character summary entry ("character had X positive interactions and Y negative interactions; net sentiment Z") and free the storage cost. The summary would be storage-cheap and retrieval-cheap; the summary would also be experientially flat. A player observing a character whose 1950s history is a single summary entry would find the character flat in a specific way the framework's design surface explicitly avoids; the character would feel like a database entry rather than a person with accumulated experience.

The premature-compression failure mode is the framework's risk if the consolidation pass over-summarizes. The framework's broader design commits to characters whose accumulated experience produces visible texture differences across decades; the texture differences require the architecture to preserve enough event-specific content that the differences are meaningful at the player-facing layer; over-summarization removes the event-specific content and flattens the differences. The compression has to be calibrated; uncalibrated compression solves the storage problem at the cost of the design surface's substantive commitments.

The texture matters specifically because the texture is what distinguishes the framework's pair-relational mechanism from the Sims-style relationship-meter approach. A Sims-style relationship meter compresses pair-relational history to a single sentiment scalar (or a small set of sentiment entries with cap-per-pair limits); the compression is operationally proven at consumer scale (Sims 4 Sentiments documented in EA's November 2020 update notes; RimWorld opinions documented in the RimWorld wiki); the compression also flattens the per-event texture that makes specific historical interactions feel meaningful. The framework's design surface commits to more texture than the Sims-style approach provides; the framework's tiered consolidation has to preserve the additional texture without paying the naive-raw-event storage cost.

## The tiered consolidation model

The framework's three-layer memory architecture decomposes the character's accumulated state into three layers with different storage costs, retrieval costs, and content types. The decomposition is structurally similar to the Dwarf Fortress emotional memory tiers (DF wiki Memory thought page; recent memories stored verbatim, consolidated memories preserve affective content but compress specifics, older memories summarized further or forgotten); the framework's adoption of the tiered pattern is informed by the DF precedent and extended for the framework's multi-decade scope.

Layer 1 stores raw events verbatim within a bounded recency window. The window is approximately the last six to twelve game-months per character (the specific window length is operator-judgment tuning; the order-of-magnitude is what matters for the architecture); events older than the window roll out of Layer 1 either by promotion to Layer 2 (if the consolidation pass marks them for promotion) or by decay (if the consolidation pass marks them as everyday events whose specific content does not warrant Layer 2 storage). The Layer 1 bounded-recency-window keeps the verbatim storage cost bounded per character; the boundedness is what makes Layer 1 tractable at the framework's NPC population scale.

Layer 2 stores consolidated events that promoted from Layer 1 during the consolidation pass. The Layer 2 entries are compressed representations of the original Layer 1 events; the compression preserves the event's affective content (the DC1-split valence/arousal/stress contributions; the pair-relational sentiment contributions; the trait-modulated encoding tags) and the event's narrative-significant content (the specific interaction partners; the specific situation; the specific outcome) while compressing the event's lower-priority content (incidental detail; routine context; non-narrative-significant features). The Layer 2 storage cost per entry is substantially lower than the Layer 1 storage cost per event; the per-character Layer 2 storage cost grows with the number of promoted events across the character's lifetime, but the growth rate is bounded by the consolidation-pass promotion criteria.

Layer 3 is the running accumulator. The accumulator integrates Layer 1 and Layer 2 contributions into the character's current affective state (the DC1-split valence/arousal/stress scalars; the framework's broader trait-state context). The accumulator does not store specific events; the accumulator stores the integrated state that the events have produced. The accumulator's storage cost is constant per character (a fixed-size state vector regardless of event count); the constancy is what makes the accumulator tractable at any NPC population scale.

The yearly consolidation pass is the mechanism that moves content between layers. The pass triggers at game-year boundaries; the pass evaluates each Layer 1 event against the promotion criteria (high-affect events; high-pair-history-impact events; trait-modulated-significant events; first-occurrence events); promotion-eligible events move to Layer 2 with consolidated representations; non-promotion events decay via the 0.995-per-game-hour anchor that has been applied since the event's original encoding (the decay is mathematically equivalent to applying the 0.995 multiplier once per game-hour since encoding; the cumulative decay over a game-year reduces the event's contribution to the accumulator to a small fraction of the original encoding-time value). The pass's runtime cost is bounded by the number of Layer 1 events per character; the cost is amortized over a game-year of player interaction; the per-frame cost of the consolidation pass is negligible because the pass runs at game-year cadence rather than per-frame.

## The 0.995/game-hour decay anchor

The 0.995-per-game-hour decay anchor is the framework's load-bearing technical commitment for the everyday-event decay. The anchor's specific value produces a half-life of approximately 138 game-hours (the half-life is the time interval over which an event's contribution decays to half its encoding-time value; mathematically, the half-life is ln(0.5)/ln(0.995), which is approximately 138). At twenty-four game-hours per game-day, the 138 game-hour half-life is approximately 5.8 game-days; the half-life is approximately one game-week.

The anchor's value is empirically inspired but not directly derived from a specific forgetting-curve study. The forgetting-curve literature (Ebbinghaus 1885 *Über das Gedächtnis: Untersuchungen zur experimentellen Psychologie* is the canonical historical anchor; the modern re-analysis literature includes Murre and Dros 2015, "Replication and analysis of Ebbinghaus' forgetting curve," *PLOS ONE* 10(7):e0120644) documents forgetting patterns that are approximately exponential in time elapsed since encoding; the specific decay constants in the literature vary across measurement paradigms and across content categories, and the framework's 0.995/game-hour value is not a direct translation of any specific literature value. The framework's value is a design choice informed by the literature's general pattern and tuned to the framework's game-feel and computational-budget constraints.

The design-choice rationale is specific. A half-life of approximately one game-week is short enough that everyday events from a typical game-week have already decayed substantially by the end of the next game-week; the decay produces the felt-recency pattern the framework's design surface commits to (the character's recent week of interactions is salient; the character's interactions from two weeks ago are softened; the character's interactions from a month ago are largely decayed unless they promoted to Layer 2 during the consolidation pass). The half-life is also long enough that the player's specific recent interactions with the NPC are retained for long enough to feel like continuous engagement (the player's interactions from the last several game-sessions remain salient for the NPC's accumulator; the player does not have to repeatedly re-introduce themselves because the NPC has forgotten them).

The anchor is tuneable per character via trait modulation. High-stress characters retain events longer (the decay rate slows in the presence of high-stress accumulator state; the slower decay produces the stress-related rumination pattern the literature documents); secure-attachment characters consolidate events faster into Layer 2 (the consolidation-pass promotion rate is elevated for secure characters; the elevation produces the consolidation pattern the attachment literature documents); the trait-modulation parameters are part of the framework's operator-side canonical record and are not enumerated in this post per the framework's soft-disclosure discipline. The trait-modulation is the operational expression of the framework's commitment that characters' memory dynamics differ across trait profiles rather than being uniform.

## What gets consolidated versus decayed

The consolidation pass's decision rule operates per Layer 1 event. The rule evaluates the event against four promotion criteria; events meeting any of the criteria are promoted to Layer 2 with consolidated representations; events meeting none of the criteria decay via the 0.995 anchor and roll out of Layer 1 at the recency-window boundary.

Affective-spike events promote to Layer 2. The criterion is high-arousal-moment events; the framework's encoding pathway tags events with arousal contributions at encoding time; events whose arousal contributions exceed a per-character threshold (the threshold is trait-modulated and is part of the framework's canonical operator-side specification) promote to Layer 2. The criterion captures the empirically-documented pattern that high-arousal events are encoded more durably and remembered more vividly across multiple memory-research paradigms; the framework's adoption of the criterion is grounded in the literature's findings and tuned to the framework's affective-modulation parameters.

Pair-history-significant events promote to Layer 2. The criterion is large-sentiment-shift events at the pair-relational level; the framework's pair-relational mechanism documents the sentiment-scalar update formula; events whose update produces a sentiment-shift exceeding a per-pair threshold promote to Layer 2. The criterion captures the empirically-documented pattern that relationship-defining events (Hawkins, Carrère, and Gottman 2002 *Journal of Marriage and Family* 64(1):193-201 documents the related sentiment-override pattern at the marital-quality scale; the framework's adoption extends the pattern to procedural-character pair-relational interactions) are encoded more durably and shape subsequent relationship dynamics more strongly.

Trait-modulated-significant events promote to Layer 2. The criterion is events whose trait-modulated encoding weight exceeds a per-character threshold; the encoding pathway's trait-modulation (HEXACO H for cooperation/exploitation events; attachment style for relational events; the specific modulation parameters live in the framework's operator-side specification) produces per-event weighting that varies across trait profiles. Anxious-attachment characters' high-weight events for threat-relevant relational signals promote at a higher rate than the same events would for secure-attachment characters; the differential promotion is the operational expression of the framework's commitment to trait-differentiated memory dynamics.

First-occurrence events promote to Layer 2. The criterion is events whose specific category has not previously occurred in the character's pair-relational history with the specific interaction partner; the first time a player introduces themselves to an NPC, the first time the NPC observes a specific kind of player action, the first time a specific interaction pattern emerges; the first-occurrence pattern is empirically-documented as more memorable than subsequent occurrences. The framework's adoption of the criterion produces NPCs whose first-meeting memories are durably encoded; the player's first interactions with each NPC are remembered across decades because they promote to Layer 2 immediately.

Everyday events that do not meet any of the four criteria decay via the 0.995 anchor and roll out of Layer 1 at the recency-window boundary. The everyday events are routine interactions (regular tipping; regular ordering; regular small talk) whose specific content does not warrant Layer 2 storage; the events' contributions to the accumulator are preserved through the 0.995 decay over the recency window, and the events themselves do not preserve as specific Layer 2 entries. The everyday-event decay is what bounds the per-character Layer 2 storage cost; the boundedness is what makes the architecture scalable to multi-decade scope without storage-cost explosion.

## Why this links to the hybrid path

The tiered consolidation pattern is necessary but not sufficient for V1 performance. The pattern bounds the storage cost; the pattern does not bound the runtime cost of NPC decision-scoring at scale. Naive sophisticated active inference scoring even on consolidated memory layers may still be too slow at the framework's NPC population scale; the framework's decision-scoring layer needs additional architectural commitments that the broader hybrid-path post sequence (posts 0121 through 0123 in this arc) addresses.

The cached-policies-plus-slow-consolidation pattern is what the framework adopts for the runtime-cost question. Cheap cached-policy lookups drive per-frame visible behavior; slow sophisticated-inference apparatus runs at consolidation-pass boundaries to update the cached policies for the upcoming game-year. The pattern is grounded in the Project Zomboid offscreen-storylet architecture (a 2022 Indie Stone development-blog post documents the architecture; subsequent post 0123 in this arc walks through the pattern in detail); the framework's adoption extends the pattern with active-inference machinery at the slow layer.

The multi-graph memory typed-indexes pattern is what the framework adopts for the retrieval-cost question. Typed indexes (semantic; temporal; causal; entity) over the three-layer memory store enable fast retrieval at runtime without paying the O(N) scan cost; the pattern is grounded in the MAGMA research literature on multi-graph agent memory architectures (a 2026 research paper that subsequent post 0122 in this arc walks through); the framework's adoption simplifies the research-system overhead to typed indexes plus pair summaries that fit within the framework's V1 budget.

The LLM-backed observation/reflection pattern is what the framework adopts for the cognitive-processing question (specifically, the question of how the consolidation pass's promotion decisions are made and how the consolidated Layer 2 representations are produced). The pattern is grounded in the Park 2023 Generative Agents research (arXiv:2304.03442; subsequent post 0121 in this arc walks through the pattern); the framework's adoption restricts the LLM-backed processing to consolidation-pass triggers rather than per-frame decisions, which keeps the per-frame budget within the iOS V1 envelope.

The four patterns together (tiered consolidation; cached policies; multi-graph memory; LLM-at-consolidation-boundary) compose the framework's hybrid path. The composition is what makes the V1 implementation tractable; the composition is documented across the subsequent posts in this arc; this post focuses on the tiered consolidation element that the broader composition depends on.

## V1 implementation cost

The schema-level work for tiered storage is bounded. The implementation adds Layer 2 as a separate persistent storage form distinct from Layer 1; the consolidation-pass scheduler runs at game-year boundaries and evaluates Layer 1 against the four promotion criteria; the decay-anchor application runs as a Layer 1 maintenance step during the consolidation pass. The implementation cost estimate at the framework's planning phase was roughly 20 to 36 hours of BUILD-side work; the estimate includes the schema addition, the consolidation-pass implementation, the decay-anchor maintenance, the audit-layer extension to verify the consolidation determinism, and the test-run validation across multi-game-year scope.

The save-load determinism requirement matters specifically. The consolidation-pass timing must be deterministic across save and load boundaries; a player who saves at one game-month boundary and loads at the same boundary must produce the same consolidation-pass outcome; the determinism is required for save-game compatibility and for replay-based testing. The framework's implementation commits to deterministic consolidation through specific schema-versioning and timestamp-fixing patterns that the framework's operator-side specification documents in detail; the patterns are commercial-scope implementation tuning that the framework's soft-disclosure discipline keeps out of public posts.

## Honest scope: what consolidation does not solve

The tiered consolidation pattern does not solve all of the framework's memory-architecture failure modes; three specific failure modes that the pattern does not address are worth naming directly.

The retraction-condition-Trigger-1 risk (the framework's first retraction-condition trigger; the trigger that fires if the architecture accidentally encodes a state-space self-variable through consolidated Layer 2 summary entries) is not eliminated by the consolidation pattern; the pattern can still produce Layer 2 entries that the audit layer flags as state-space-content violations. The framework's audit-layer mandatory test surface includes the state-space content audit as the primary check; the audit catches the violations at the architecture-evaluation layer regardless of the consolidation pattern's specific decisions. The consolidation pattern is one architectural element; the audit layer is another; the two operate at different layers and the failure mode requires the audit layer to address.

The cross-cultural-tags problem is not addressed by the consolidation pattern. The framework's character-generation pipeline includes cultural-context-tag metadata (the schema-placeholder fields the broader cross-cultural-disclosure sequence commits to); the consolidation pass may erase the cultural-context tags if the tag-preservation logic is not explicitly part of the pass's implementation. The framework's implementation specifies tag-preservation as part of the Layer 2 consolidated representation; the specification is required because the consolidation pass would otherwise produce Layer 2 entries whose cultural-context tags are missing, and the missing tags would compromise the broader cross-cultural-disclosure discipline.

The save-load determinism requirement adds implementation complexity. The deterministic-consolidation specification produces specific patterns that the implementation has to honor (timestamp-fixing; deterministic-random-seed propagation; schema-versioning that allows future framework versions to apply the same consolidation pattern across older save-games). The complexity is operationally manageable but is not zero; the V1 budget includes the complexity in the 20-36 hour cost estimate, and the complexity is one of the reasons the cost estimate carries a buffer.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes. First, when the framework adds an architectural commitment that scales with game-time, I now check the scaling against the framework's multi-decade-scope constraint before the commitment is ratified. The discipline catches scaling-violation failure modes at the architectural-evaluation layer; the discipline produces commitments whose scaling is operationally tractable rather than commitments that would crash at multi-decade scope. The recency-collapse arithmetic above is the load-bearing example; the discipline would have caught the violation at the planning phase if the framework had applied the discipline before the first-pass spec.

Second, when the framework adopts a decay-anchor or consolidation-cadence parameter, I now check both the empirical-literature inspiration and the design-choice rationale before the parameter is ratified. The discipline produces parameter choices whose grounding is explicit at both the literature and the design layers; the alternative is parameters whose grounding is implicit and whose justification is post-hoc. The 0.995-per-game-hour anchor's rationale is the operational example; the rationale is documented in the framework's specification, and the documentation is what makes the parameter accountable to subsequent scrutiny.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the 0.995/game-hour anchor is empirically derived from a specific forgetting-curve study. The anchor is empirically inspired by the forgetting-curve literature's general pattern (approximately exponential decay; modern re-analyses by Murre and Dros 2015 confirm the pattern; specific decay constants vary across paradigms and content categories); the anchor's specific value is a design choice tuned to the framework's game-feel and computational-budget constraints. The choice is operationally defensible; the choice is not literature-mandated.

I am not claiming the four promotion criteria (affective spike; pair-history significance; trait-modulated significance; first occurrence) are exhaustive. The criteria capture the most empirically-supported promotion patterns; additional criteria may surface as the framework's implementation work produces additional patterns the criteria would miss. The framework's V1 implementation commits to the four criteria; subsequent versions may extend the criteria based on the empirical evidence from the framework's player-facing pilot.

I am not claiming the tiered consolidation pattern eliminates the storage-versus-texture trade-off. The pattern shifts the trade-off; the pattern does not eliminate it. The framework's Layer 2 storage cost grows with the number of promoted events; the growth rate is bounded by the consolidation-pass promotion criteria; the boundedness keeps the multi-decade scope tractable but does not eliminate the underlying trade-off between storage cost and preserved texture. The trade-off is operational; the framework's specific choice is operationally defensible but is not the only defensible choice for the same design surface.

I am not claiming the save-load determinism specification handles all save-load corner cases. The specification handles the consolidation-pass timing and the Layer 2 representation generation; the specification does not handle all of the broader save-load complications (e.g., version-migration when the framework's schema evolves between V1 and V1.x; corruption-recovery when save-files are partially corrupted; cross-platform compatibility when save-files transfer between iOS and other platforms the framework's commercial product may eventually ship to). The broader save-load discipline is a separate framework-engineering scope; the consolidation-pass specification is one element of the broader discipline.

I am not claiming the framework's V1 implementation will handle the consolidation-pass at the framework's full target NPC population scale without performance issues. The arithmetic above suggests the implementation should be tractable; the empirical performance at the full target scale is the V1-implementation-validation question. The framework's V1 budget includes the implementation work; the validation is part of the budget; the post-V1 work that would optimize the implementation further is a separate scope.

## What I am claiming

The framework's tiered memory architecture (Layer 1 raw events with bounded recency window; Layer 2 consolidated entries with structured representation; Layer 3 running accumulator with constant per-character storage cost; yearly consolidation pass with four promotion criteria; 0.995-per-game-hour decay anchor for everyday-event decay) is the framework's operational response to the recency-collapse-versus-premature-compression failure modes. The architecture preserves enough event-specific content to support the framework's design surface's texture commitments; the architecture bounds the per-character storage cost to keep the multi-decade scope tractable at iOS V1 budget; the architecture is deterministic across save-load boundaries.

If you are building procedural-character architectures at multi-decade game-time scope, the recency-collapse arithmetic is sharp and the premature-compression failure mode is equally consequential; the tiered consolidation pattern is one operational shape that threads the needle. The architecture's specific elements are grounded in the empirical literature (forgetting-curve inspiration; mood-congruent retrieval at Layer 3; trait-modulated encoding at Layer 1-to-Layer 2 promotion) and in commercial-precedent analogs (Dwarf Fortress emotional memory tiers; Project Zomboid offscreen-storylet pattern; Sims 4 Sentiments cap-per-pair); the architecture's coherence within the framework's broader commitments is what makes the elements work together.
