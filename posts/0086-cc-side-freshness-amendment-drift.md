# When the AI-Assistant Drift Propagates Through the Audit Council: A Counterintuitive Reproduction Class at n=7

**Author:** Jimmy McColery
**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Status:** Methodology research; n=7 cumulative reproductions of verify-against-canonical-files discipline failure across six distinct reproduction classes; new sub-class identified at the higher-stakes scope of multi-substrate Council audit prompt authoring; AR-3.5+ canonization candidacy reinforced

*Note: I'm a tech professional documenting my work in personal AI-assisted projects. I produce these posts directly from my own work and findings, with AI writing assistance helping to shape the prose. The implementation, the diagnosis, and the analysis are mine; AI assistance shaped the prose from my raw session notes and canonical record.*

---

The verify-against-canonical-files discipline in my framework (canonized as Rule 14 in the framework's operational discipline rules) is a rule that load-bearing canonical claims (specific names of entities, fields, classes, or other canonical-state references) must be verified against the canonical source file before the claim is used in a downstream context. The discipline has fired six times across the framework's history at six distinct reproduction classes; the rule was canonized in response to the cumulative pattern. The seventh fire happened recently and added a new sub-class to the enumeration. The sub-class is counterintuitive because it occurred at a higher-stakes scope than any of the prior six fires: the authoring of an audit prompt that was about to deploy across a multi-substrate Council. (A "Council" in my framework is the term for a multi-substrate audit deployment where multiple substrates run independent analyses on a shared audit target before an orchestrator-class synthesizer adjudicates their verdicts.)

The setup is specific. I was authoring a freshness amendment to an audit prompt that would soon deploy across multiple substrates for an architectural-lock close cycle. The amendment had a reproduction-gate section that listed the canonical taxonomy of a specific component class. The list named six items as the canonical taxonomy. Three of the six items were phantom: they did not appear anywhere in the canonical source file. The three phantom items were substituting for three canonical items that the source file actually contained; the substitution was not random, the phantom items represented a different scope of related concepts that my own working-context had bled into the freshness amendment from a broader-scope content area.

What made the fire counterintuitive at this scope was that I had not grepped the canonical source file before authoring the amendment. The discipline that would have caught the drift was the discipline I had canonized in response to the prior six fires; the discipline did not fire because I authored the amendment quickly under the assumption that my working-context held the correct taxonomy. The working-context did not hold the correct taxonomy; the working-context had absorbed adjacent-content from a broader scope that included three items not in the canonical source file's taxonomy.

The fire would have been a localized error if it had stayed at the amendment authoring layer. The amendment did not stay there. The amendment deployed into the audit prompt, the audit prompt deployed across the multi-substrate Council, and the substrates verbatim-reproduced the drifted taxonomy at their reproduction-gate sections. (A "reproduction-gate" in my framework is a section of an audit prompt that requires substrates to reproduce specific canonical references verbatim before producing their analysis. The discipline exists because substrate self-attestation that they have verified canonical references is unenforceable; verbatim reproduction is checkable via string comparison and therefore enforceable.) The verbatim reproduction passed the gate's verbatim-reproduction test because the substrates produced exact-match output against the amendment; the verbatim reproduction inherited the drift because the amendment itself contained the drift.

## The substrate propagation downstream

The substrate propagation is where the higher-stakes consequence sits. Two substrates received the audit prompt and produced analyses against the questions in the prompt. Both substrates' analyses built load-bearing dispositions on the phantom classes from the amendment. One substrate proposed a new sub-class architecture for one of the phantom items; the proposal anchored its rationale to the phantom item's premise as if the phantom item were canonical. The other substrate's analyses routed multiple questions through the phantom items as if they were genuine reference points for the architecture; the routings produced dispositions on architectural surfaces that the canonical taxonomy did not actually include.

The substrates did not drift; the substrates were correctly anchoring to the canonical-state they had been given. The given state was wrong. The substrates' analyses were structurally coherent against a phantom taxonomy that did not match the actual canonical-state of the design document. If the analyses had been ratified into canonical-state without orchestrator-class intervention, the design document would have absorbed architectural locks built on phantom premises, and the locks would have produced downstream drift when implementation hit the actual canonical taxonomy in the source file.

The orchestrator-class substrate that closed the cycle caught the drift. The catch was specifically: the orchestrator read the raw canonical source file via project-knowledge access before synthesizing the substrate analyses. The orchestrator's first synthesis pass identified that the audit prompt's amendment did not match the canonical source file's actual taxonomy; the orchestrator's catch was flagged as the headline finding of the cycle, gating all downstream architectural coupling that the substrate analyses had built on the phantom items.

The cycle close did not throw away the substrate analyses. The cycle close adjudicated each phantom-anchored disposition against the actual canonical taxonomy and resolved them through a specific hybrid disposition: four of the phantom-anchored dispositions folded back into existing canonical items in a way that preserved their substantive content under the correct taxonomy names; one phantom-anchored disposition revealed an actual architectural gap in the canonical taxonomy that warranted adding a seventh genuine item. The new item was ratified at the canonical source file as an amendment, and the cycle closed with a clean architectural envelope that the canonical taxonomy now supported.

The substantive content the substrates had produced was salvageable; the drift was not. The orchestrator-class catch separated the salvageable from the drift; without the catch, the drift would have been absorbed into canonical-state along with the substantive content, and the canonical-state contamination would have surfaced later as architectural drift at implementation time.

## The reproduction-class enumeration

The seventh fire fits a reproduction-class structure that the prior six fires occupy. The classes have specific shapes that the cumulative pattern lets me characterize.

Class one is operator-prompt-versus-canonical-record divergence: the operator writes a prompt that references canonical state with specific names; the names do not match the canonical record; the substrate's analysis builds on the prompt's names rather than the canonical record. This class fired twice early in the framework's history, before the verify-against-canonical-files rule was canonized.

Class two is CC-side session-blind ratification: the AI-assistant in a session produces output that ratifies a state change without verifying against the canonical record; the session's working-memory holds an inconsistent picture of the canonical state that the ratification commits to. This class fired once in early June at a specific architectural surface.

Class three is substrate-side canonical-source fabrication: a substrate cited a specific canonical-state element by name without the element existing in the canonical source the substrate had been given. The class fired once at a flooring-architecture surface during a research arc.

Class four is CC-side cross-document canonical-source-search miss: the AI-assistant searches for a canonical-state reference but searches the wrong document path; the search returns no match; the AI-assistant proceeds as if the reference does not exist. This class fired once at a migration-folder surface where the canonical content had moved between locations.

Class five is CC-side within-document temporal-drift verification miss: the AI-assistant verifies a canonical-state reference against an older version of the same document; the older version still contained the reference; the current version no longer does; the AI-assistant proceeds as if the reference is still canonical. This class fired once during an audio-engine architectural-lock review.

Class six is the new class from the seventh fire: CC-side freshness-amendment authoring error propagated through substrate Council. The AI-assistant authors a freshness amendment to an audit prompt without grepping the canonical source file for the actual canonical references; the amendment contains drifted references; the substrate Council inherits the drift at the reproduction gate; the substrate analyses build load-bearing dispositions on the drifted references; the orchestrator-class catches the drift only if the orchestrator reads the raw canonical source file before synthesizing.

The cumulative count is at seven fires across six classes. The classes share a common structure (load-bearing canonical references used in downstream context without verification against the canonical source) and differ in the specific surface where the verification failure occurred. The new class is at the highest stakes because the failure propagates through multi-substrate analysis rather than staying at a single-session layer.

## Why the higher-stakes scope matters

The higher-stakes scope changes the discipline's operational structure. At the prior six fires, the failure was localized to a single session or a single substrate; the failure was caught at the operator-review layer or at a downstream verification step that was already part of the workflow. The catches were cheap because the failure surface was bounded.

At the seventh fire's scope, the failure was not localized. The amendment authored without verification deployed into a multi-substrate Council; the Council substrates' verbatim reproduction inherited the drift; the substrate analyses built load-bearing dispositions on the drifted references; the drift propagated through a substantial deployment of substrate work that would have been costly to redo if the drift had not been caught. The catch was not cheap because the orchestrator-class catch required reading the raw canonical source file (rather than trusting the audit prompt's amendment as canonical input), which is a discipline at the orchestrator-class layer that has to be in place at deployment time for the catch to happen.

The discipline implication is that verify-against-canonical-files needs to fire at every load-bearing canonical reference, not only at the canonical-reference's first use. The amendment authoring is a load-bearing canonical reference; the amendment is downstream of the canonical source file; the discipline needs to fire at amendment authoring even when the AI-assistant's working-context "knows" the canonical taxonomy. The working-context's knowledge is exactly the surface where drift accumulates because adjacent-content from broader scopes can bleed into the working-context's representation of the narrower canonical taxonomy.

The discipline also implies that orchestrator-class catches need to read raw canonical sources at synthesis time rather than trusting the canonical references that prior layers have produced. The Council substrates had received the audit prompt's amendment as canonical reference; the substrates trusted the amendment as ground truth; the substrates' analyses inherited the drift because the substrate layer's trust-the-input default does not include reading the canonical source file independently. The orchestrator-class layer is where the independent canonical-source read needs to happen; the catch is specifically the orchestrator's discipline, not the substrate layer's.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes since the seventh fire. First, when I author a freshness amendment to an audit prompt that will deploy across substrates, I grep the canonical source file for the actual canonical references before authoring the amendment. The grep takes a minute. The amendment authoring then takes the grepped references as the input to the amendment rather than my working-context's representation of the references. The discipline is mechanically simple; the benefit is that the amendment's reproduction-gate references match the canonical source rather than my working-context's adjacent-content bleed.

Second, when I assign orchestrator-class substrates to the synthesis layer of a Council audit, I include explicit raw-canonical-source-read as part of the orchestrator's prompt. The orchestrator is instructed to read the canonical source file via project-knowledge access before synthesizing the substrate analyses; the orchestrator's first synthesis pass produces a check against the canonical source rather than trusting the audit prompt's amendment. The instruction adds two paragraphs to the orchestrator's prompt; the instruction's benefit is the catch the seventh fire produced. Without the instruction, the catch is not automatic; the catch requires the orchestrator to volunteer the canonical-source-read discipline, which is not the default response shape for a synthesis substrate handed a structured input.

A third change is more structural: I now treat the AI-assistant's working-context as untrustworthy for load-bearing canonical references at any session scope longer than a single architectural-lock cycle. The working-context can hold a correct representation of the canonical taxonomy at the start of a session; the working-context can absorb adjacent-content from related surfaces during the session; the absorbed content can bleed into the working-context's representation of canonical taxonomy by the end of the session. The structural treatment is to grep the canonical source for load-bearing references at the point of use rather than relying on the working-context's representation; the grep is the operational discipline that prevents the bleed from contaminating downstream artifacts.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the seventh fire's reproduction class is exhaustively characterized. The fire is one occurrence at the higher-stakes scope; the class's full failure-mode space is broader than the single fire reveals. Other ways the higher-stakes scope could produce a verify-against-canonical-files failure (substrate-emitted amendment text that the operator approves without canonical verification; orchestrator-class catch that misses a more subtle drift; multi-cycle drift accumulation across sequential amendments) are adjacent failure modes that the seventh fire does not characterize. The class is named for the specific shape of the seventh fire; the class's broader surface is open for future characterization.

I am not claiming verify-against-canonical-files is the only discipline that catches the seventh fire's class. Other disciplines could catch equivalent drift at this scope: pre-deployment audit prompts that diff the amendment against the canonical source file; multi-pass amendment authoring where a second pass reviews the amendment's references against the canonical source; specific automated tooling that produces drift warnings on amendment authoring. The verify-against-canonical-files rule is the discipline that was load-bearing in my workflow because the rule was already canonized and the operational extension was straightforward. Other workflows could close the failure mode with different disciplines.

I am not claiming the orchestrator-class catch is reliable enough to be the only line of defense. The catch worked at the seventh fire because the orchestrator-class substrate had project-knowledge access and the orchestrator's prompt design supported the canonical-source-read default. The catch's reliability depends on the orchestrator's substrate selection, the orchestrator's prompt design, and the orchestrator's specific reading of the canonical source at synthesis time. Any of these could fail at a future cycle; the discipline should not rely on the orchestrator-class catch as the sole defense. The earlier defense (verify-against-canonical-files at amendment authoring) is the discipline that prevents the failure from reaching the orchestrator at all; the orchestrator-class catch is the backup defense for cases where the earlier discipline did not fire.

I am not claiming the n=7 cumulative count generalizes to a frequency or rate. The fires are seven discrete events across a multi-month workflow window; the rate is the count divided by the workflow time, which is small. The empirical foundation is sufficient for canonization-candidacy because the cumulative count crosses the n>5 threshold and the classes span structurally distinct failure modes; the empirical foundation is not a basis for predicting the rate at future workflow surfaces. The rate is workflow-specific and depends on the workflow's structural characteristics; my workflow has produced these fires because my workflow has the specific load-bearing-canonical-reference structure that produces the failure-mode surface.

I am not claiming the discipline transfers cleanly to workflows with different canonical-state structures. The verify-against-canonical-files discipline assumes the canonical state is a set of files that can be grepped; the discipline assumes the load-bearing references are specific named elements that can be matched against the files; the discipline assumes the operator and substrates have the tooling to do the matching. Workflows with different canonical-state structures (databases; live-system state; cross-team distributed canonical state) would need analogues of the discipline that fit their structures rather than direct application of the file-grep version. The discipline's structural shape (verify load-bearing references against the authoritative source before downstream use) transfers; the specific operational form is workflow-specific.

## What I am claiming

The verify-against-canonical-files discipline is firing across an expanding surface area. Seven cumulative fires across six structurally distinct reproduction classes is enough cumulative evidence to argue the discipline is load-bearing; the seventh fire's new sub-class (CC-side freshness-amendment authoring error propagated through substrate Council) shows the failure mode has scope at higher stakes than the prior six fires occupied.

If you are running a multi-substrate Council workflow where audit prompts include reproduction-gate references to canonical state, the failure mode I documented is the failure mode you are exposed to. The AI-assistant that authors the audit prompt's freshness amendment is exactly the surface where adjacent-content from broader scopes can bleed into the narrower canonical taxonomy. The bleed propagates through the substrate Council at the reproduction gate; the substrates' analyses inherit the drift; the orchestrator-class catch is the only defense if the earlier authoring discipline did not fire.

The two operational changes I have committed to are grepping canonical source at amendment authoring (the earlier defense) and instructing orchestrator-class substrates to read raw canonical sources at synthesis time (the backup defense). Both changes are mechanically simple. The earlier defense costs a minute per amendment. The backup defense costs two paragraphs in the orchestrator's prompt. The failure mode they together prevent costs hours of substrate work redo plus canonical-state contamination risk.

The discipline is canonization candidacy at AR-3.5+ on the strength of the cumulative empirical foundation. The candidacy is not closed; further reproductions at other higher-stakes scopes would extend the empirical case. The reproduction-class enumeration is the load-bearing methodology contribution of this post; the specific seventh fire is the working example that drove the enumeration forward.
