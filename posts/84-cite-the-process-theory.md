The recurring objection to using Friston's framework as the architectural ground for NPC design is that the Free Energy Principle is metaphysics, not science. The objection has been made by capable philosophers of science (Colombo and Wright in *Synthese* 2021; Williams in *Philosophical Studies* 2021; Bowers and Davis in *Psychological Bulletin* 2012 if we go back to the falsifiability-criterion source). The objection is not a strawman; the principle has the structural shape of a metaphysical claim, and the response to the objection cannot be to wave the criticism away.

This post is about the response I adopted in a design framework I am calling Modified-C-bis. The response is specific. The framework cites the process theory of active inference, which is the mathematical machinery of partially observable Markov decision processes that the principle generated, and explicitly does not adopt the principle's metaphysical commitments. The process theory has empirical traction at the design layer; the principle does not need to be empirically tractable for the design layer to do its work. The move accepts Colombo and Wright's premise (the principle-as-principle is not falsifiable in the way the broader Bayesian-just-so-stories critique requires) and routes around the premise's consequences (the process theory IS empirically tractable, because it makes specific claims about how belief updates against evidence under specific inference schemes).

The move is not original to me. It is visible in the structure of Friston's own 2017 paper. The post documents the move, names the citations precisely, and reports the primary-text verification that closed an open flag in the framework's research arc about which sections of the 2017 paper carry the relevant content.

## The objection's accurate part

Colombo and Wright (2021) wrote a substantive philosophy-of-science treatment of the Free Energy Principle that rejects strong-program FEP claims while preserving a specific weaker reading. Their paper's section 4.1 (preprint page 28) endorses an epistemic-pluralism position: the FEP "defines a class of process models" rather than a single empirical hypothesis about brain function. The class-of-process-models reading is what the principle's mathematical content actually delivers; the stronger reading (the FEP as a unified theory of cognition that explains-everything-the-brain-does) is what they reject.

The Bowers and Davis 2012 critique sits behind the Colombo and Wright treatment. Bowers and Davis argued that Bayesian models in psychology and neuroscience function as just-so-stories when they are constructed post hoc to fit any observed data without independent constraint on the priors and likelihoods used in the fit. The just-so-story diagnosis is sharp: a Bayesian model that can be tuned to match any data is not empirically informative because the tuning absorbs whatever the data would have falsified.

Williams (2021) is the most philosophically precise recent FEP critique. The paper argues that predictive coding accounts of thought face specific structural problems that the FEP's broadest readings cannot resolve. Williams is not arguing the FEP has no scientific value; the paper is arguing that the FEP's broadest reading cannot do the work the broadest claims require, and the FEP literature has not adequately distinguished the broad claim from the narrow claim.

The accurate part of these critiques is structural. The FEP as broadest claim (a unified principle that explains brain function from first principles of variational free energy minimization) has the shape of a metaphysical commitment because it claims explanatory closure rather than empirical content. Metaphysical commitments are not falsifiable in the way empirical claims are; the falsifiability criterion applies to empirical claims and the FEP-as-broadest-claim does not pass the criterion. Acknowledging this is not capitulation to the critics; it is reading the literature precisely.

## The 2017 paper's own disclaimer

Friston, FitzGerald, Rigoli, Schwartenbeck, and Pezzulo (2017), "Active inference: A process theory," published in *Neural Computation* 29(1):1-49, contains the move that exits the falsifiability critique. The paper has two sections (the Summary at §4.4, around page 34, and the Conclusion at §5, pages 36 through 37) where Friston disclaims the principle's prescriptive force. Specifically, the paper distinguishes the process theory (the POMDP mathematical machinery the paper develops in §2 and §3) from the broader Free Energy Principle (the unified framing the paper situates the process theory within). The distinction is not a footnote; the paper's structure is the disclaimer. The title says "process theory" because the paper's substantive content is the process theory, and the broader principle is the context within which the process theory makes sense.

The 2017 paper is the canonical citation for the active inference process theory. The paper develops the POMDP machinery in formal detail, derives the variational-message-passing scheme that the process theory runs on, and demonstrates the scheme's empirical content through specific simulation work. The empirical content is checkable: the scheme produces specific predictions about how belief states update under specific evidence configurations, and the predictions can be empirically tested against animal or human behavior or against simulation work that the scheme generates.

The paper also contains Appendix F, "Sophisticated Schemes," at pages 42 through 43. Appendix F introduces the naive-versus-sophisticated active inference distinction that becomes the canonical anchor for sophisticated inference in the subsequent literature. The direct quote from Appendix F (page 43) is: "Clearly, this scheme is computationally more involved than the naive scheme and calls on recursive variational updating. This means that sophisticated agents are metacognitive in some sense because they perform belief updating (based on fictive outcomes) to optimize their belief updating." The naive scheme runs without recursive belief-about-belief updating; the sophisticated scheme runs the recursion. The distinction is about inference scheme, not about temporal horizon depth.

A flag in my framework's research arc went open about this. The Round 7 closing-round flagged the Appendix F citation as potentially conflated with another paper. The Round 8 closing-round ratified by R4 closed the flag definitively. Both R2 and R4 ran independent primary-text reads of Friston 2017 Appendix F and confirmed the naive-versus-sophisticated distinction is in the paper at pages 42 through 43 as cited. The flag's prior-arc status (uncorroborated; probable conflation with another paper) is retracted. Both Friston 2017 (Appendix F, first formal introduction) and Friston, Da Costa, Hafner, Hesp, and Parr (2021) "Sophisticated Inference" in *Neural Computation* 33(3):713-763 (the full formal treatment) are valid co-citations for the sophisticated-active-inference distinction.

A specific disambiguation matters here. The naive-versus-sophisticated axis is recursive metacognition. The temporal-horizon axis is depth of forward planning. The Episodic-versus-Diachronic axis (from the philosophy-of-mind literature on personal identity) is self-experience continuity. These are three orthogonal axes. A naive-active-inference agent can have arbitrary temporal horizon depth; a sophisticated-active-inference agent can have shallow temporal horizon; a Diachronic agent can run on naive scheme; an Episodic agent can run on sophisticated scheme. Conflating any two of these three axes produces architectural confusion that I had to correct in my own framework's specification.

## The move that exits the critique

The framework decision Modified-C-bis adopts is to cite the process theory as the architectural anchor and explicitly not adopt the FEP-as-broadest-claim as a metaphysical commitment. The decision sounds small. In practice it is large because the citation chain throughout the framework changes shape under the decision.

Under the decision, every framework citation to active inference cites Friston 2017 specifically for the POMDP machinery. The citation is paired with one of the falsifiability-critique anchors (Colombo and Wright 2021, primarily) to acknowledge the broader-claim's limits in the same citation environment. The framework does not cite the FEP as a unifying principle; the framework cites the process theory as a specific mathematical scheme. When the framework needs to discuss the broader context (because some specific design choice references it), the discussion is explicit about the principle-versus-process-theory distinction and routes the load-bearing claim through the process-theory anchor rather than the principle anchor.

This is the move that exits the critique. The critique is that the broadest principle is not falsifiable in the way empirical claims are. The move is to cite the process theory, which is empirically tractable, and not commit to the broadest principle as a metaphysical claim. The critique's premise is accepted; the critique's consequence is routed around because the framework is not committed to the broadest principle in the first place.

The disambiguation about the naive-versus-sophisticated axis matters under the move. Modified-C-bis adopts the naive scheme as the V1 architectural commitment for cost reasons (recursive metacognition is computationally more expensive than non-recursive belief updating, and V1 deployment budget is constrained). The naive scheme is not adopted because sophistication implies Diachronicity (it does not; the three axes are orthogonal), and the framework's later forms (Phase 8.x post-V1) may adopt sophisticated inference for specific design surfaces without changing the framework's commitments on the other two axes. The orthogonal-axis discipline is what lets the framework be precise about what V1 commits to without overcommitting on adjacent axes.

## What this costs the framework

The framework gives up the metaphysical-foundation claim. It does not get to argue that NPC design is grounded in a unified theory of cognition that explains-everything-the-brain-does; the framework explicitly disclaims that the broadest principle is the foundation. The framework gives up the rhetorical reach the broadest principle would have provided.

The framework gains operational testability. The process theory makes specific claims about how belief states update under specific evidence configurations; the design layer can run the claims as a specific NPC architecture and observe whether the architecture produces the design behaviors it predicts. The architecture is not the test of the broadest principle; the architecture is the deployment of the process theory in a specific design surface where the process theory's empirical content carries the design load. The empirical-content claims are checkable at the design layer because the design layer produces observable NPC behavior.

The trade matters specifically for the design surface I was working in. Procedural-generation work for indie games is design work, not cognitive science. The design layer needs running code that produces specific NPC behavior under specific design conditions. The broadest principle's metaphysical reach is not load-bearing at the design layer; the process theory's specific machinery is. Citing the process theory and not the principle is the move that aligns the framework's foundational claims with the design layer's actual needs.

The trade may not work for every design surface. A cognitive-science contribution that wants to make a unified-theory claim cannot exit the critique by citing only the process theory; the unified-theory claim is exactly what the critics are rejecting, and the framework's commitments would have to carry the load directly. A design framework that is content to be design infrastructure rather than cognitive science can make the trade cleanly because the design layer does not require the unified-theory claim to do its work. The trade is design-surface-specific, and the framework's clarity about which surface it operates on is what makes the trade defensible.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the Free Energy Principle is invalid as metaphysics. The principle's broadest reading has structural problems the literature has identified; the broadest reading also has defenders who have responded to the critiques in substantive ways. The principle's metaphysical status is a live philosophy-of-science debate that I am not adjudicating in this post. The post adjudicates only the framework's decision to cite the process theory rather than the principle, which is a narrower decision than ruling on the principle's metaphysical merits.

I am not claiming the process theory is empirically validated as a complete account of belief updating in biological cognition. The process theory's empirical content is checkable; the empirical work that would close the validation question is substantial and ongoing in the cognitive-science literature; the framework's design layer is not the venue for that validation. The framework's claim is that the process theory's machinery is sufficient for the design layer's needs, not that the process theory is the complete account of biological belief updating.

I am not claiming the framework's design layer commitments transfer to cognitive science. The design layer commits to specific architectural choices (naive active inference; present-state-anchored state-space content; specific Marcus Reed audit-layer separations) that are appropriate for the design layer's V1 budget. The architectural choices are not cognitive-science claims about how biological cognition actually works; the choices are design decisions about what running architecture is feasible at V1 deployment scope. A cognitive-science paper would have to make different choices because the cognitive-science layer's success criteria are different from the design layer's.

I am not claiming the naive-active-inference choice is permanent. The framework adopts naive scheme at V1 for cost reasons; the framework's Modification 6 amendment explicitly allows Phase 8.x post-V1 expansion to sophisticated scheme for specific design surfaces if the engagement target justifies the expansion. The naive choice is a V1 commitment that is honest about being V1; the framework's later forms are not promised by the V1 architecture and are not foreclosed by it either. The architectural discipline is to be precise about V1 commitments without overcommitting on Phase 8.x outcomes.

I am not claiming the move (cite the process theory, not the principle) is the only response to the falsifiability critique. The literature includes other responses: defenders of the broadest principle have argued the principle is empirically tractable when read with appropriate care; reformulators have argued the principle should be read as a normative framework rather than an empirical claim; pluralists have argued the principle's epistemic status varies across the contexts in which it is applied. The framework's decision is one response among several; the post does not adjudicate whether the framework's response is the best response, only that it is the framework's response and that the response is defensible on the structural grounds I have laid out.

## What I am claiming

I am claiming that the framework Modified-C-bis cites the process theory of active inference, specifically Friston et al. 2017 in *Neural Computation* 29(1):1-49, and explicitly does not commit to the broader Free Energy Principle as a metaphysical foundation.
