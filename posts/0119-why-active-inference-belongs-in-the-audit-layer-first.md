The framework I am developing uses active inference as the mathematical apparatus that grounds its NPC decision-making mechanism. Active inference is too theoretically powerful to ship as runtime by default; the apparatus's recursive metacognition machinery makes per-frame deployment expensive at the framework's NPC population scale, and the empirical question of whether per-frame sophisticated inference is necessary for the framework's design surface is open. The disciplined response is to ship the apparatus in the audit layer first and earn the runtime promotion through empirical evidence; the tempting response is to ship the apparatus as runtime directly and hope the empirical evidence supports the deployment after the ship. The disciplined response is what this post is about.

The framework's earlier planning had banked sophisticated active inference for a post-V1 phase under a more conservative deployment plan. An operator ratification (the framework calls it Path A) at the close of an earlier research-arc round promoted sophisticated active inference and structural-only self-reflection to V1 critical-path; the promotion is the architectural commitment, not a sequencing change. The audit-first-versus-runtime-first sequencing question is independent of Path A; the audit-first sequencing is compatible with Path A and is what the framework adopts as the operational discipline for the V1 deployment.

The post walks through the tractability arithmetic at the framework's target NPC scale, the audit-layer-versus-runtime-layer distinction for active-inference apparatus, the safety reasons audit-first sequencing is the right discipline, the Path A ratification context, the four-audit mandatory test surface that runs as audit-layer apparatus, and the Round 9 generative-model formalization arc that the audit-first sequencing depends on. The arc is the framework's response to the architectural-commitment question of "what specifically does the V1 implementation commit to under Path A"; the formalization is what the V1 BUILD-side work consumes; the post documents the sequencing discipline that the BUILD-side work runs against.

## What active inference does for NPCs

A brief recap of the active-inference apparatus the framework adopts. The apparatus is the process theory developed by Friston, FitzGerald, Rigoli, Schwartenbeck, and Pezzulo 2017, "Active inference: A process theory," published in *Neural Computation* 29(1):1-49. The paper develops the partially-observable Markov decision process formalism that the framework's NPCs run on; the formalism scores candidate actions by expected free energy (the EFE; a scalar that measures the expected cost of taking the candidate action in terms of the agent's variational free energy across the action's anticipated outcome distribution); the action with the lowest EFE is selected.

The 2017 paper's Appendix F (pages 42 through 43, primary-text verified per the framework's Round 8 R2 and R4 closing-round re-reads) introduces the naive-versus-sophisticated active inference distinction. The naive scheme scores candidate actions through a single-step expected-free-energy calculation; the sophisticated scheme adds recursive belief-about-belief updating (the agent updates its beliefs about world-state given candidate evidence; the agent then updates its model of how its belief-updates would change under counterfactual evidence trajectories; the recursion is what makes the scheme sophisticated). Friston, Da Costa, Hafner, Hesp, and Parr 2021, "Sophisticated Inference," published in *Neural Computation* 33(3):713-763, develops the sophisticated scheme's formal treatment in detail; both papers are co-canonical anchors for the framework's sophisticated active inference adoption under Path A.

The Modification 6 Amendment under Round 8 Revision (the framework's working name for the architectural commitment that resolves a specific boundary-discriminator question; the broader retraction-table post 0110 in this arc documents the boundary-discriminator-corrected framing) ratifies the framework's position-(a) discipline: sophisticated active inference and structural-only self-reflection at V1; state-space-content boundary (the generative model contains no cross-era self-identity variable); horizon depth and Episodic-versus-Diachronic axes treated as orthogonal-to-sophistication axes that the framework's commitments do not couple. The naive-versus-sophisticated axis is recursive metacognition; the temporal-horizon axis is forward-planning depth; the Episodic-versus-Diachronic axis is self-experience continuity; the three axes are independent; the framework's V1 commits to sophisticated scheme without overcommitting on horizon depth or self-experience continuity.

## The tractability risk at the framework's NPC scale

The arithmetic at the framework's target NPC scale is sharp. The framework's design surface targets tens of NPCs in the player's perceptual window at any moment; the NPC population is in the order of approximately 80 to 100 characters for the V1 specification; each NPC makes roughly ten decisions per game-minute at the framework's interaction density; the total per-game-hour decision count is in the order of tens of thousands. Each decision requires an EFE evaluation; sophisticated inference adds recursive belief-about-belief evaluation per Friston 2017 Appendix F and Friston 2021; the per-decision compute cost grows with the recursion depth and with the policy-search breadth.

At the iOS V1 target framerate of approximately 30 frames per second, the per-frame budget is approximately 33 milliseconds. The per-frame budget has to absorb rendering, input processing, physics, audio, and the framework's NPC-decision pipeline simultaneously; the NPC-decision pipeline's share of the per-frame budget is in the order of single-digit milliseconds in production-tuned implementations. The per-frame budget cannot absorb tens-of-thousands-per-hour EFE evaluations at sophisticated-scheme cost; the budget would be exhausted by the decision pipeline alone, leaving nothing for the rest of the per-frame pipeline.

The horizon-bound discipline (the framework's secondary defense-in-depth that constrains policy-search depth to τ less than or equal to 3) reduces the per-decision compute cost; the discipline does not eliminate the cost growth; the per-frame budget still cannot absorb the full sophisticated-scheme cost at the framework's interaction density. The framework needs an architectural commitment that decouples the per-frame decision pipeline from the sophisticated-scheme apparatus; the commitment is what the audit-first sequencing produces.

## The audit-layer use of active inference

Define the audit-layer use clearly. NPCs make decisions via cached or heuristic policies during runtime; the policies are cheap per-decision and produce predictable per-frame budgets; the cached-policy approach is what most consumer-scale procedural-character architectures use because the alternative is performance-prohibitive. At consolidation boundaries (the framework's yearly consolidation pass; documented in post 0118 in this arc) or at developer-triggered audit points, the framework's active-inference apparatus runs offline to evaluate whether the NPC's cached-policy choices align with the position-(a)-compatible EFE-minimization that the framework's discipline commits to.

The audit-layer use is structurally different from the runtime-layer use. The audit-layer use runs at consolidation cadence (yearly per the framework's specification) rather than per-frame; the audit-layer use evaluates accumulated NPC decisions across the year rather than scoring individual decisions in real-time; the audit-layer use produces audit-trail artifacts that document whether the cached-policy decisions align with the framework's discipline rather than producing runtime decisions; the audit-layer use's failures do not crash gameplay because the audit runs offline rather than in the per-frame pipeline.

The audit-layer use catches specific failure modes the cached-policy approach can produce. A cached policy that was baked under one set of accumulator-state assumptions may produce decisions that violate the framework's discipline once the accumulator state drifts beyond the assumption boundary; the audit detects the violation by re-evaluating the cached-policy decisions against the current accumulator state under sophisticated inference; the audit flags the violation for developer review or triggers a cached-policy regeneration at the next consolidation boundary. The catch is the audit-layer use's substantive contribution; the catch is what makes the cached-policy approach safe to ship.

The audit-layer use is also where the active-inference apparatus earns its theoretical complexity. The apparatus's recursive metacognition machinery is overkill for per-frame decision scoring (the per-frame decisions can run on cached policies without paying the recursion cost); the machinery is exactly the right tool for the offline-evaluation question of whether the cached-policy decisions are aligned with the framework's discipline. The audit-layer use is the apparatus operating in its empirically-justified role; the runtime-layer use is the apparatus operating in a role whose empirical justification is open.

## Why audit-first is the safer sequencing

Three specific reasons audit-first sequencing is the safer discipline. First, the audit layer ships with fewer operational risks than the runtime layer. The audit layer runs offline (no per-frame budget impact; no real-time failure-mode exposure); the audit layer's failures do not crash gameplay (a failed audit produces a flag in the audit log rather than producing a runtime exception that the engine has to handle); the audit layer can ship with provisional cached-policy approaches and refine the cached-policy generation based on the audit's findings over time. The runtime layer has none of these affordances; the runtime layer's failures crash gameplay; the runtime layer has to ship with the apparatus fully working at V1; the V1 ship is more brittle if the runtime layer is the deployment target.

Second, the audit layer produces empirical evidence on whether NPCs actually need sophisticated-inference scoring at the runtime layer at all. The empirical question is open: maybe cached policies suffice for the V1 1950s baseline design surface; maybe the cached policies' decisions align with sophisticated-inference decisions closely enough that the runtime-layer deployment would produce no observable improvement in NPC behavior; maybe the V1 player-facing evidence supports continuing the audit-layer-only approach indefinitely. The audit-layer deployment produces the evidence; the runtime-layer deployment assumes the evidence rather than producing it.

Third, the audit-first sequencing buys time for the four-audit mandatory test surface to mature before sophisticated inference is in the player-facing path. The four audits (state-space content audit as primary; amnesia re-initialization test as mandatory; Markov-blanket isolation test; horizon bound τ ≤ 3 as secondary defense-in-depth) are the framework's commitments to architectural discipline; the audits' implementations need empirical refinement based on the framework's actual NPC architecture; the audit-first deployment produces the refinement evidence without exposing the player-facing path to architectural-discipline failures that the audits would catch. The runtime-first deployment would expose the player-facing path to the discipline-failures before the audits are mature enough to catch them reliably.

Fourth (a bonus reason), the audit-first sequencing lets the framework publish posts about active inference (this arc; subsequent posts on the formalization arc and the hybrid path) without claiming runtime-deployed sophisticated inference until empirically warranted. The publication discipline matters because claims propagate; a claim of runtime-deployed sophisticated inference that the framework later has to retract because the runtime deployment was not yet ready would damage the framework's claim-credibility; the audit-first claim is empirically defensible at the V1 ship moment, and the runtime claim can be added later when the empirical evidence supports the deployment.

## The four-audit mandatory test surface

The framework's mandatory test surface includes four specific audits. The state-space content audit is the primary check (the audit enumerates the generative-model tensor's state variables; the audit verifies no state variable indexes character identity across eras; the audit's failure flags the architecture as not satisfying position-(a) discipline). The amnesia re-initialization test is mandatory (the test resets the NPC's accumulated state to era-baseline; the test verifies the NPC returns to era-baseline behavior; the test's failure flags the architecture as having state-space-content violations that the primary audit missed). The Markov-blanket isolation test is structural-discipline (the test verifies that the NPC's recursive belief-updating does not read player-side narrative state; the test's failure flags the architecture as having boundary-isolation violations that would compromise the player-NPC inference asymmetry the framework commits to). The horizon-bound test (τ ≤ 3) is secondary defense-in-depth (the test verifies that the NPC's forward-planning policy-search does not exceed the horizon depth the framework's V1 spec allows; the test is secondary because the boundary discriminator is state-space content, not horizon depth, per the F-18 correction; the test still operates as defense-in-depth to bound the computational cost).

The four audits are themselves audit-layer apparatus. Running the audits is exactly the audit-layer use case the post advocates for; the audits run at consolidation cadence or at developer-triggered audit points; the audits do not run per-frame; the audits produce audit-trail artifacts; the audits do not affect runtime decisions directly. The audit-first sequencing for active inference is therefore not a separate architectural choice from the mandatory test surface; the audit-first sequencing IS the mandatory test surface plus the sophisticated-inference apparatus running at audit-layer cadence.

The mandatory test surface's implementation cost is in the order of approximately 40 to 80 hours of BUILD-side work within the Path A budget; the cost includes the audit implementations, the audit-trail format, the audit-integration with the framework's logging, and the developer-facing audit-review interface. The cost is operationally tractable; the audits are V1-critical-path under Path A because the audits are the apparatus that earns the framework's discipline claims at the production-deployment layer.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes. First, when the framework adopts an architectural element whose runtime-layer deployment carries unresolved empirical-evidence questions, I now default to audit-first sequencing for the element's V1 deployment. The discipline catches the runtime-first failure mode (architectural elements deployed to runtime layer at V1 without sufficient empirical evidence; subsequent retraction or partial-rollback work to address the unresolved evidence; reputation cost to the framework's claim-credibility); the discipline produces V1 deployments whose empirical foundation is established at the audit layer before the runtime promotion is considered.

Second, when the framework commits to a four-audit mandatory test surface or any analogous test-surface specification, I now check whether the surface's specific audits operate at audit-layer cadence rather than at per-frame cadence. The discipline catches surface specifications that conflate audit-layer and runtime-layer evaluations; the discipline produces surface specifications whose implementation cost and integration patterns align with the framework's broader per-frame-budget constraints. The four-audit mandatory test surface in the framework's V1 spec is the operational example; the surface's audits all operate at audit-layer cadence, and the cadence alignment is what makes the surface tractable within the Path A budget.

## What I am not claiming

The discipline applies specifically to elements whose runtime-layer deployment carries unresolved empirical-evidence questions; elements whose runtime-layer deployment is empirically well-supported can deploy to runtime layer at V1 directly. The HEXACO-trait modulation pathway (documented in post 0115 in this arc) is an example of a runtime-deployed element; the modulation runs at runtime and the empirical evidence supports the deployment. I am not claiming the audit-first sequencing eliminates the runtime-deployment risk. The discipline produces audit-layer evidence that the runtime deployment can subsequently rely on; the discipline does not produce certainty that the runtime deployment will succeed when it eventually happens. The empirical evidence from the audit layer may support runtime deployment; the empirical evidence may not; the discipline is a structured response to the open question, not a resolution of it.
