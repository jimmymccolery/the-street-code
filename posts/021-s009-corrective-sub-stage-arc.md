## The pattern named

A corrective sub-stage is a targeted mutation arc triggered by a higher-level audit catching drift the within-arc verification missed. Three structural properties distinguish it from re-running closure or treating the original arc as failed.

First, it doesn't re-fire the same internal verification logic that missed the drift. The original arc's verification gates ran what they were designed to run; if drift slipped past those gates, re-running them produces the same result. The corrective sub-stage operates against the audit findings directly, with mutation scope sized to what the audit surfaced and verification appropriate to the corrective scope. Different mechanism than re-closure.

Second, the corrective sub-stage is itself a Type 5 verify-then-deploy mutation arc. It has its own pre-mutation state read, its own per-stage residual greps, its own post-mutation verification. The discipline that ran the original arc runs the corrective sub-stage at smaller scale. A corrective sub-stage that abandons verify-then-deploy in favor of "just fix it quickly" reintroduces the same risk class that produced the original drift.

Third, corrective sub-stages can themselves spawn further corrective sub-stages when their own audits surface residual drift. The recursion is not a defect; it's the expected shape when scope gaps in the original arc compound across multiple substrates and file formats. What the recursion requires is that each corrective sub-stage close its own audit gates before declaring complete, and that any new audit findings get scoped into the next corrective sub-stage rather than absorbed informally.

The lessons-learned entry at line 474 captures the canonical principle directly: "Corrective sub-stages (e.g., Stage 1.5) are first-class and should be added as needed without abandoning the broader stage architecture." The discipline canonization at bootstrap_manifest.md v2.2 §Type 5 verify-then-deploy taxonomy frames the pattern as scale-validation evidence; this post is the empirical case the canonization references.

## What the discipline costs and what it buys

Cost is real and named. Corrective sub-stage 1 took roughly five to seven hours of mutation work (the initial scope estimate was sixty to ninety minutes; honest re-estimate during prompt-quality audit was five to seven hours; actual execution cleared the honest estimate). Corrective sub-stage 2 added a further mutation cycle on top. Step 12 cleanup added a smaller pass. Audit cycles cost subscription time on Gemini Deep Think plus operator attention on each round's findings. The total opportunity cost runs into a working day's worth of focused operator time across cross-substrate audit, mutation work, and verification.

Benefit is the alternative. Without corrective sub-stage discipline, the closure paths available are: accept canonical state with drift (which propagates to runtime behavior across the seventeen-project deployment), re-run the entire main arc (which re-fires the same internal verification logic that missed the drift the first time), or treat the arc as failed and roll back (which loses the work that closed clean). The corrective sub-stage path is the only one that preserves the closed-clean work while addressing what the closed-clean verification couldn't catch.

The discipline also generates structural lessons that wouldn't surface from clean closure. Three earned lessons canonized today rest specifically on corrective work: the Stage 2 scope gap pattern (line 523), the bidirectional scope-gap recursive fire correction (line 539), and the multi-format extension correction (line 541). Two more lessons were canonized at corrective sub-stage 2 close: Council of Three triangulation as operationally required (line 545) and the verification-query precision class (covered in Post 23). One more at Step 12 close: third-leg fallibility (line 571, covered in Post 20). None of these lessons would have been visible from inside the main arc; the corrective discipline is also the surface where structural patterns become observable.

## Connection to existing canonical patterns

The corrective sub-stage discipline operates one structural level above the within-cycle Phase 1 / Phase 3 / Phase 5 architecture. The phase architecture catches drift inside a single mutation cycle: Phase 5 verifies what Phase 3 produced against canonical files in a separate substrate execution. A corrective sub-stage operates against a higher-level audit catching drift the within-cycle architecture's verification gates structurally couldn't reach. The two operate complementarily; neither substitutes for the other.

Council of Three triangulation is the audit mechanism that surfaces what within-arc verification can't catch. The architecture decision document (canonized 2026-05-07) frames Council of Three as substrate-class-agnostic at architectural level: three structurally-distinct substrates verifying the same canonical state, where structural distance between substrates is the criterion for verification independence. Corrective sub-stages are how the framework responds when Council of Three triangulation surfaces drift; they're the operational vocabulary for "the audit caught something; here's the targeted mutation arc that addresses it."

The multi-fire empirical bar (the discipline that requires at least two independent fires before promoting a pattern from operator-side discipline to canonical anti-pattern) is distinct from the corrective sub-stage discipline but related at the level of pattern recognition. Corrective sub-stages produce empirical fires; the multi-fire bar adjudicates when fires accumulate to the point where the pattern earns canonical promotion. The recursive scope-gap pattern that fired three times within S009's corrective work meets that bar within a single arc; covered in Post 24.

Corrective sub-stages are themselves Type 5 mutation arcs at smaller scale, inheriting the per-stage-residual-grep discipline. The lessons-learned entry at line 470 frames S009 as scale-validation evidence for Type 5: the workflow held under multi-stage canonical-record discipline at large scope, and held again under corrective sub-stage application.

## What I'm not claiming

I'm not claiming corrective sub-stages are costless. The discipline absorbs operator time and attention beyond what clean closure would have required, and the cost compounds when corrective work spawns further corrective work. The five-to-seven-hour estimate for corrective sub-stage 1 plus subsequent rounds plus cleanup represents real opportunity cost against other framework work that didn't happen during that window.

I'm not claiming three corrective sub-stages plus cleanup was the right number. The arc could have closed at corrective sub-stage 1 if Gemini Audit A had caught everything in one pass; sub-stage 2 was necessary because audit Round 1 didn't surface the Step 12 dimensions; cleanup was necessary because audit Round 2 surfaced more findings. The number is descriptive of what closure actually required for this specific arc, not normative for arcs in general.

I'm not claiming the discipline is universally applicable. Corrective sub-stage discipline applies to high-stakes canonical-state work where drift propagates downstream and Council of Three triangulation is available. Lower-stakes work where same-substrate verification suffices doesn't need the same discipline; the cost-benefit calculation tips the other way.

I'm not claiming this is novel as a class. Corrective rounds in software deployment are well-established (think hotfix releases, post-deploy verification cycles, follow-up patches). What's named here is the specific structural shape: a Type 5 mutation arc triggered by a higher-level audit, run with the same verify-then-deploy discipline as the original arc, capable of spawning further corrective sub-stages when its own audit surfaces residual drift.

## What I am claiming

Corrective sub-stage as a discipline pattern, not just as one-shot recovery, is real and operationally clear. The S009 arc empirically demonstrates the pattern at scale: three corrective sub-stages plus cleanup, all running as Type 5 mutation arcs at smaller scale, all closing their own audit gates before declaring complete. The canonical record reflects what the arc actually required to close clean, not what within-arc verification reported as closed clean on the first pass.

The discipline catches drift the within-arc verification can't catch. The empirical case is direct: main-arc verification reported clean; Council of Three Round 1 caught five real drift dimensions; corrective sub-stage 1 verification reported clean twice; Round 2 caught more real drift; corrective sub-stage 2 closed; Round 3 produced false positives that confirmed the cross-leg consensus rather than triggering further work. The arc closed in three audit rounds across roughly twenty-four hours, with each corrective sub-stage adding empirical material the within-arc gates would not have surfaced.

What corrective sub-stage discipline buys is the only path that preserves closed-clean work while addressing what closed-clean verification couldn't catch. The alternative paths (accept drift in canonical state, re-run the main arc, treat the arc as failed) are all structurally worse. Naming the discipline as a class is what makes it operational vocabulary instead of one-off improvisation.
