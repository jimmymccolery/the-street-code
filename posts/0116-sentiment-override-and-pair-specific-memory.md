A regular bowler tips a pinboy a quarter. A different bowler tips the same pinboy a quarter. Five years of game-time later, the two regulars are very different to the pinboy. One has accumulated thousands of weekly quarter-tips into a pattern of expected gratitude; one has occasionally appeared with much larger tips that the pinboy remembers as moments rather than as patterns. The framework's job is to make that difference legible mathematically; the difference is what the pair-specific memory mechanism the framework adopts is designed to produce.

The framework calls the underlying mechanism pair-relational history with sentiment override. The canonical psychological-literature anchor is Hawkins, Carrère, and Gottman 2002, "Marital quality: Modeling the dynamics of the relationship over the lifespan," *Journal of Marriage and Family* 64(1):193-201; the paper formalizes what marital-quality research had observed empirically: pair-relational behavior is biased by an accumulated cumulative-affective sentiment toward the partner, not just by the current interaction's content. Once a positive sentiment-override regime engages in a relationship, partners interpret ambiguous behavior charitably; once a negative regime engages, the same ambiguous behavior is interpreted as confirmation of partner bad-intent. The override is the mechanism the framework adopts; the framework extends the mechanism beyond marriage research's scope to pair-relational interactions in a multi-decade procedural-character architecture.

This post walks through the architecture. The walkthrough uses the bowling-alley tipping scenario as a worked example so the implementation is legible at the per-event level; the walkthrough also compares the framework's pair-history architecture against commercial precedents (the Sims, RimWorld, Crusader Kings 3, Dwarf Fortress) so that the framework's positioning relative to existing production-quality patterns is honest about what the framework borrows and what the framework extends.

## The bowling-alley tipping scenario

Three bowlers and one pinboy. Bowler A is a regular; the bowler arrives at the alley weekly across ten game-years; the bowler tips the pinboy a standard quarter each week. The pinboy's pair-relational memory of Bowler A accumulates ten years of consistent small tips. The accumulated sentiment is mildly positive; the regime is established; the pinboy expects the quarter and feels mild weekly gratitude when the tip arrives. The interactions are not memorable in themselves (a quarter tip is a small event); the pattern of interactions is memorable as a sentiment regime.

Bowler B is irregular; the bowler appears at the alley occasionally over the same ten-year period; on one specific busy night, the bowler tipped the pinboy fifty cents instead of the standard quarter. The pinboy's pair-relational memory of Bowler B does not contain ten years of accumulated weekly tips; the memory contains the specific fifty-cent tip as a moment that stands out against the otherwise-sparse interaction history. The moment is memorable as a discrete event rather than as a pattern; the moment's sentiment contribution is elevated because it stands out against the sparse history.

Bowler C is a no-tip-history character; the character has never previously tipped the pinboy; the character one night arrives and tips fifty cents. The pinboy's pair-relational memory of Bowler C has no prior tipping pattern to set expectations against; the fifty-cent tip surprises the pinboy because no tipping was expected; the moment is encoded as an event whose sentiment regime is uncertain because the prior history does not establish the regime.

The same fifty-cent tip event becomes three different things in the framework's encoding: gratitude (Bowler B), reputation-building (Bowler C; the moment might shift the pinboy's expectation toward future tipping from Bowler C, but the shift is conditional on subsequent interactions confirming the pattern), pair-trust (the cumulative tipping pattern that Bowler A has built; the pair-trust regime is what makes the weekly quarter feel like part of an established relationship rather than as a transactional moment), public norm signal (if other pinboys in the alley observe Bowler A's tipping pattern, the pattern becomes information about the alley's norm structure that other pinboys factor into their own pair-relational expectations), or nothing (if the encoding conditions do not register the tip event as significant enough to encode at all, the event decays out of memory through the framework's consolidation pass without being preserved).

The encoding conditions matter operationally. The framework's architecture commits to encoding decisions that are conditional on the pair-history-accumulated context; the same event in different contexts produces different encoding outcomes; the encoding produces different downstream pair-relational behavior; the downstream behavior is what the player observes as character variation across the multi-decade scope. The scenario above is what the architecture is designed to produce; the rest of the post walks through the mechanism that produces it.

## What sentiment override is

Hawkins, Carrère, and Gottman 2002 documents the empirical finding that married couples' perception of partner behavior is biased by an accumulated positive-or-negative sentiment regime. The regime acts like a Bayesian prior over partner-behavior interpretation; positive-regime partners read ambiguous partner-behavior as supporting the positive regime (e.g., partner is late from work; positive regime reads as "something must have come up"; the late-arrival event is encoded charitably); negative-regime partners read the same ambiguous partner-behavior as supporting the negative regime (e.g., partner is late from work; negative regime reads as "partner doesn't care about my time"; the late-arrival event is encoded as confirmation of the negative regime).

The paper's empirical methodology is the marital-quality longitudinal study that the Gottman lab developed across multiple decades of marital-interaction research; the paper synthesizes the longitudinal evidence into the sentiment-override formalization. The sentiment-override regime is not just a tendency; the regime is operational across multiple specific cognitive-and-behavioral patterns: the regime biases interpretation; the regime biases recall; the regime biases anticipation of future interaction; the regime predicts subsequent interaction behavior with measurable validity. The framework adopts the sentiment-override mechanism because the empirical foundation is broad enough to support the adoption beyond the marriage-research scope the paper is set in.

The cross-cultural caveat is important and worth flagging directly. The Hawkins-Carrère-Gottman 2002 empirical base is U.S. married-couple sample (the Gottman lab's primary research population); broader cross-cultural marriage-research literature has produced mixed findings on whether the same sentiment-override dynamics hold universally across cultures (some patterns reproduce in non-U.S. populations; some patterns differ in ways that suggest culture-specific moderation; the literature is unsettled at the cross-cultural-generalization level). The framework's adoption of sentiment-override for procedural characters extends the mechanism beyond the empirical base in two ways simultaneously (the population scope extends beyond U.S. married couples; the relationship scope extends beyond marital relationships to pair-relational interactions broadly). The framework's extension is operator-judgment supported by the structural shape of the mechanism rather than by direct cross-cultural empirical validation; the broader cross-cultural-disclosure sequence (subsequent posts in this arc) addresses the WEIRD-defaults problem the extension implicates.

## How it maps to game NPCs

The framework's sentiment-override implementation for procedural characters is operationally specific. Each ordered pair of characters (the pair (A, B) is distinct from the pair (B, A); the directional ordering matters because A's sentiment toward B may differ from B's sentiment toward A) maintains a sentiment scalar in the range from negative one to positive one. The scalar represents the accumulated sentiment regime; positive values denote positive regime; negative values denote negative regime; zero represents a neutral or undefined regime.

The scalar updates with each interaction event according to a decay-toward-current-plus-drift-from-history formula. The decay-toward-current term updates the scalar in the direction of the current event's affective valence (a positive event drifts the scalar toward positive; a negative event drifts it toward negative; the update magnitude scales with the event's affective weight). The drift-from-history term resists rapid scalar changes when the accumulated history is strong (the scalar's update magnitude is dampened by the cumulative pair-history; a single positive event does not flip a long-running negative regime; a single negative event does not flip a long-running positive regime). The formula's specific parameters live in the framework's operator-side canonical record; the framework's soft-disclosure discipline keeps the specific parameters out of public posts because the parameters are commercial-scope tuning. The structural shape of the formula (decay-toward-current plus drift-from-history) is the substantive content of this post; the structural shape is publishable because it grounds the architecture in the literature.

The sentiment scalar biases event-interpretation in the pair-history-accumulator at subsequent interactions. Positive-regime pair-context causes subsequent positive-tagged events to be over-encoded (the encoding pathway reads the positive regime as supporting amplified encoding of positive events; the amplification is bounded by the encoding pathway's bandwidth); negative-regime pair-context causes subsequent threat-tagged events to be over-encoded (the encoding pathway reads the negative regime as supporting amplified encoding of threat-relevant content); ambiguous events are encoded with the regime-aligned interpretation (positive regime interprets ambiguous events charitably; negative regime interprets ambiguous events as threat-confirming). The bias is the operational expression of the sentiment-override mechanism; the bias is what produces the per-event encoding outcomes the bowling-alley scenario above walks through.

The framework caps the number of active pair-sentiments per character at three to five active sentiments per ordered pair. The cap is grounded in the commercial-precedent literature (the Sims 4 Sentiments feature uses four active sentiments per Sim-pair as the cap; the framework's choice of three-to-five is informed by the Sims precedent and tuned to the framework's multi-decade scope); the cap is what bounds the per-character memory load at scale. Older sentiments either decay (their scalar magnitudes drift toward zero over game-year intervals and the sentiments drop out of the active set when they fall below an activation threshold), merge into summaries (multiple low-magnitude sentiments may consolidate into a single summary entry that preserves the cumulative magnitude without preserving each individual sentiment), or promote to core pair events (specific high-magnitude pair events promote to Layer 2 storage during the consolidation pass; the consolidation-pass mechanism is documented in a subsequent post in this arc).

## Commercial precedent comparison

The framework's pair-relational mechanism is not the first attempt to operationalize accumulated pair-history in production game architectures. Four commercial precedents inform the framework's positioning; the comparison clarifies what the framework borrows and what the framework extends.

The Sims 4 Sentiments feature (added in The Sims 4's November 2020 update; documented in EA's update notes and the broader Sims community wiki) is the closest commercial precedent for pair-specific affective memory at consumer scale. Sentiments in Sims 4 are formed by shared memorable experiences between Sim-pairs; sentiments can be one-sided (Sim A has a sentiment about Sim B that Sim B does not reciprocate); each Sim-pair can have up to four active sentiments; the weakest sentiment is replaced when a fifth sentiment would otherwise be added. The Sims 4 architecture is the framework's most direct commercial-precedent anchor for the cap-per-pair pattern and for the one-sided-sentiment pattern; the framework's adoption of the three-to-five active sentiments cap is informed by the Sims 4 precedent's empirical track record at production scale.

RimWorld's opinion system (documented in the RimWorld wiki's Social mechanics pages) operates a bounded mood-and-opinion modifier per pair; opinions update under interaction events; opinions decay over time; opinions trigger specific behaviors when they cross specific thresholds. The RimWorld architecture is simpler than the Sims 4 Sentiments architecture (RimWorld opinions are scalar modifiers without the structured-sentiment-entry decomposition that Sims 4 Sentiments provides); the RimWorld architecture is operationally proven at consumer scale across thousands of hours of player engagement. The framework adopts the threshold-triggered behavior pattern from RimWorld (specific sentiment-scalar thresholds trigger specific NPC-behavior responses) while extending the architecture beyond RimWorld's single-scalar-per-pair simplicity.

Crusader Kings 3's memory system (documented in the CK3 wiki at the Memory page; the system was substantially revised in Patch 1.7) treats memories as chronological event records that subsequent content can reference (event triggers reference past pair-history memories; the memories are content-triggers rather than affective substrates per se). The CK3 architecture is memory-as-content-trigger rather than memory-as-affective-substrate; the architecture is different in kind from the Sims 4 + RimWorld pattern; the framework borrows the chronological-event-record element from CK3 (the framework's Layer 1 raw event memory is chronologically ordered and supports content-trigger lookup) while combining the chronological record with the affective-substrate elements that the Sims 4 + RimWorld + Gottman literature inform.

Dwarf Fortress emotional memory tiers (documented in the Dwarf Fortress wiki's Memory thought page) implement a tiered memory architecture where recent memories are stored verbatim, consolidated memories preserve the affective content but compress the specifics, and older memories are summarized further or forgotten. The DF architecture is the closest commercial-precedent anchor for the tiered consolidation pattern; the framework adopts the tiered consolidation pattern (described in a subsequent post) with cross-references to the DF precedent at the commercial-precedent disclosure layer.

## What the framework adds beyond commercial precedents

The framework's pair-relational architecture borrows specific elements from each commercial precedent (cap-per-pair from Sims 4; threshold-triggered behavior from RimWorld; chronological event records from CK3; tiered consolidation from Dwarf Fortress); the framework extends the borrowed elements in specific ways that no commercial precedent the framework's research arc surveyed combines.

The multi-decade NPC memory scope is the first extension. None of the commercial precedents the framework's research arc surveyed operates at multi-decade game-time scale per character; the precedents typically operate at game-session timescales or at multi-year timescales but not at the multi-decade scope the framework's tycoon-sim design surface requires. The framework's extension to multi-decade scope requires the tiered consolidation pattern to scale beyond what the commercial precedents have demonstrated; the framework's architecture commits to the scale as an empirically-open question that the framework's implementation will test.

The emotion-conditioned encoding is the second extension. The framework's Layer 1-to-Layer 2 encoding filter modulates the encoding based on the event's affective content (the framework's DC1 split separates valence, arousal, and stress as independent affective scalars; the encoding filter reads the affective scalars to modulate the encoding bandwidth); the commercial precedents the framework's research arc surveyed do not implement the same level of affective-modulated encoding (the precedents typically encode pair events with uniform fidelity regardless of affective content, or they tag events with simple affective labels without modulating the encoding bandwidth). The framework's emotion-conditioned encoding is the architectural element that produces the bowling-alley scenario's per-event variance; the variance is what makes characters' relational histories feel like genuinely accumulated experience rather than as uniform-fidelity event logs.

The retrieval-time affect modulation is the third extension. The framework's retrieval pathway modulates the retrieved content based on the retrieving character's current affective state (a character in a negative affective state retrieves negative pair-history content with elevated weight; a character in a positive affective state retrieves positive pair-history content with elevated weight); the commercial precedents the framework's research arc surveyed do not implement the same depth of state-dependent retrieval modulation. The retrieval modulation is grounded in Eich 1995 mood-congruent retrieval literature ("Searching for mood dependent memory," *Psychological Science* 6(2):67-75 is the canonical anchor); the framework's retrieval modulation extends the literature's empirical findings into the procedural-character architecture context.

The active-inference-inspired decision scoring under position-(a) discipline is the fourth extension. The framework's NPC decision-scoring layer reads the pair-relational sentiment scalars as inputs to the expected-free-energy decomposition that scores candidate actions; the scoring is grounded in active-inference machinery rather than in utility-function aggregation; the position-(a) discipline (the framework's commitment to structural-only sophisticated active inference without cross-era self-identity variables in the generative model) is documented in subsequent posts in this arc. The active-inference-grounded scoring is what distinguishes the framework's pair-relational mechanism from utility-AI-grounded pair-relational mechanisms; the distinction is structural and produces different downstream NPC-behavior patterns.

The combination is the novelty, not the components. None of the four extensions above is novel in isolation; each extension has prior art in the academic or commercial literature; the combination of all four extensions under the framework's broader architecture commitments is what the framework contributes. The honesty framing (novel in combination, not in components) is the framework's commercial-precedent-respect discipline; the discipline matters because the framework's claims need to be precise about what is borrowed versus what is extended, and precise claims survive scrutiny in ways that exaggerated novelty-claims do not.

## What the framework does NOT do better than commercial precedents

The framework's pair-relational architecture is not better than commercial precedents at every dimension. Specific dimensions where commercial precedents are better are worth naming directly because the honesty matters for the framework's positioning.

Production engineering is the first dimension. The Sims 4, RimWorld, Crusader Kings 3, and Dwarf Fortress all ship at consumer scale across millions of player hours; the production engineering each precedent has accumulated includes specific QA infrastructure, performance-optimization investments, and content-tooling that the framework's research-stage architecture has not built. The framework is a research-framework specification, not yet a production-tested system; the production-engineering gap is real and will be substantial work to close.

UI surfacing is the second dimension. The player-facing rendering of relationships in each commercial precedent has gone through multiple iterations of player-facing refinement; the framework's architecture commits to specific player-facing rendering decisions but the rendering is not the framework's primary research focus. Commercial-precedent UI surfacing is informed by years of player-feedback iteration that the framework's research-stage work has not had access to; the UI gap is real and will require specific design-and-iteration work that is downstream of the framework's architecture commitments.

Modding extensibility is the third dimension. Each commercial precedent has a mod scene that extends the architecture's capabilities through community-contributed content; the framework is operator-internal at the present moment and does not have a mod-extensibility surface. The mod-extensibility gap may close in subsequent versions of the framework's commercial-product expression; the gap exists at V1.

## Cross-cultural caveat for pair-sentiment

The Hawkins-Carrère-Gottman 2002 sentiment-override empirical anchor is WEIRD-sample-derived (the Gottman lab's primary research population is U.S. married couples; the broader Gottman literature is mostly U.S.-based with some cross-cultural extensions). The framework's adoption of sentiment-override for multi-decade multi-cultural NPC populations extends the mechanism beyond the empirical base in two simultaneous dimensions (population scope; relationship scope); the extension is operator-judgment supported by the structural shape of the mechanism rather than by direct cross-cultural empirical validation.

The framework's broader cross-cultural-disclosure sequence (subsequent posts in this arc) addresses the WEIRD-defaults problem in detail. The handling at the sentiment-override layer specifically is: the framework's character-generation pipeline includes cultural-context-tag metadata as schema-placeholder fields (per the broader sequence's commitments); the V1 spec does not branch on the tags for sentiment-override operationalization (the V1 spec uses the Hawkins-Carrère-Gottman-informed operationalization uniformly); the post-V1 work that would add cultural-context-aware sentiment-override dynamics is a separate research-program scope that the framework has not committed to at V1.
