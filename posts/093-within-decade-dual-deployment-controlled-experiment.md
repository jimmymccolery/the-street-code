The two-axis substrate-style preservation model in [Post 82](082-two-axis-substrate-style-preservation.md) decomposed substrate behavior into a framework-adherence axis and a content-emphasis axis. The decomposition rested on cross-decade reproductions of substrate behavior across a seven-decade research arc. Cross-decade reproductions are evidence the axes hold across decade-scope variation, but cross-decade reproductions cannot isolate which substrate properties are substrate-intrinsic versus which are prompt-responsive, because cross-decade reproductions vary both the decade content and the prompt template at the same time.

This post documents the operational shape that closes the isolation question: within-decade dual deployment, where the same substrate runs against the same decade twice under different prompt-template scaffolding. The controlled comparison isolates the prompt-dimension as the variable while holding substrate identity and decade content constant. The within-decade dual-deployment configuration is the cheapest controlled-experiment design for substrate behavior I have found.

## The dual-deployment configuration

The configuration is specific. Deploy a substrate against a research topic at a specific decade scope (say, the 1950s for the bowling-industry research arc). Record the substrate's output. Later in the same arc, deploy the same substrate against the same decade with a different prompt-template scaffolding. Record the second output. Compare the two outputs for what is preserved (substrate-intrinsic) and what differs (prompt-responsive).

The configuration's discipline is that the two deployments hold everything constant except the prompt scaffolding. The substrate is the same (Grok in the example I am about to describe). The decade content is the same (1950s bowling industry). The research topic is the same (staffing and operational patterns). The only variable is the prompt-template scaffolding: the first deployment used the framework's foundational research-prompt template, the second deployment used an alternative prompt-template the operator had drafted in parallel for a different purpose.

The first deployment in my arc was Grok at the 1950s research decade in the morning. The deployment used the framework's research-prompt template; the output produced the substrate's research-rigor analysis with the substrate's native Gang-framework structural decomposition (the five-role decomposition the substrate's training has internalized). The second deployment was Grok at the same 1950s research decade in the afternoon. The deployment used a different prompt-template the operator had drafted earlier in parallel; the output produced the substrate's research-rigor analysis with the same native Gang-framework structural decomposition.

The framework-adherence axis was preserved across the two deployments. The substrate composed its native style under the operator's framework in both cases; the substrate did not substitute one structure for the other. The framework-adherence assignment is substrate-intrinsic; the assignment held under different prompt-template scaffolding.

The content-emphasis axis was responsive to the prompt scaffolding. The first deployment's content emphasized the substrate's default narrative-arc decomposition across the decade. The second deployment's content emphasized substantially deeper primary-source anchoring (specific 1958 industry-survey citation, named-individual archival quotes, multiple quantitative anchors at league count, growth rate, and center count). The same substrate at the same decade produced different empirical depth based on how explicit the prompt-template scaffolding was about primary-source surfacing.

The dual-deployment isolated the two axes empirically. Cross-decade reproductions had shown the axes held across decade variation; the within-decade dual-deployment showed which axis was substrate-intrinsic and which was prompt-responsive. The framework-adherence axis is substrate-intrinsic (held under different prompts at same decade). The content-emphasis axis is prompt-responsive (varied under different prompts at same decade).

## Why the dual-deployment costs less than cross-substrate verification

Cross-substrate verification (running different substrates against the same research topic to compare outputs) is the standard methodology for substrate-behavior characterization in multi-substrate research workflows. Cross-substrate verification is informative but costly: each substrate is a separate deployment with separate operator-attention overhead, and the cumulative cost of running multiple substrates against the same topic scales linearly with the number of substrates.

Within-substrate same-decade dual-deployment costs significantly less than cross-substrate verification for the same isolation question. The dual-deployment is two deployments of the same substrate; the deployment cost is two units rather than N units. The operator-attention cost is comparable to two deployments of any substrate. The output integration is simpler because the two outputs come from the same substrate's processing rather than from different substrates' processing.

The cost differential matters because most operators running multi-substrate research workflows do not have the operator-attention budget to run full cross-substrate verification at every research scope. The dual-deployment configuration is a cheaper alternative that produces a specific kind of isolation evidence (substrate-intrinsic versus prompt-responsive) that cross-substrate verification cannot produce directly.

Cross-substrate verification produces convergence-or-divergence evidence at the cross-substrate level: do multiple substrates produce similar or different outputs? Within-substrate dual-deployment produces stability-or-responsiveness evidence at the within-substrate level: which properties of one substrate's output are preserved across prompt variation, and which vary?

The two evidence types are complementary, not substitutes. Cross-substrate verification answers "do substrates differ on this topic?" Within-substrate dual-deployment answers "which properties of one substrate's behavior are substrate-intrinsic versus which are prompt-responsive?" Operators running characterization work for their substrate set should use both configurations as resources permit; the within-substrate dual-deployment is the cheaper of the two and produces the isolation evidence that cross-substrate verification cannot produce.

## What the dual-deployment configuration requires

The configuration is methodologically simple but requires three operational disciplines that are easy to skip and produce uninformative results when skipped.

The first discipline is that the two deployments must hold everything constant except the prompt scaffolding. If the operator inadvertently changes the research topic between deployments (e.g., asks slightly different questions in the two prompts), the comparison loses the controlled-experiment property. The substrate-output differences could be attributable to the topic variation rather than to the prompt-scaffolding variation. The discipline is to specify the research topic verbatim across the two prompts; only the scaffolding around the topic should differ.

The second discipline is that the two prompt-template scaffoldings must differ in a structured way that the operator can characterize. A naive dual-deployment with random prompt variation produces output differences that cannot be attributed to specific scaffolding properties. The structured variation could be: prompt A is content-permissive while prompt B specifies content focus; prompt A provides a structured role framework while prompt B leaves the substrate to organize its own response; prompt A provides primary-source guidance while prompt B does not. The structure of the variation is what lets the operator interpret the output differences.

The third discipline is that the two outputs must be analyzed side-by-side for preserved-versus-varied properties. The analysis is not a "which output is better" comparison; the analysis is a "which properties are preserved and which are varied across the prompt variation" characterization. The properties that are preserved are substrate-intrinsic; the properties that are varied are prompt-responsive. The analysis discipline is what extracts the controlled-experiment evidence from the two outputs.

When the three disciplines are in place, the dual-deployment produces interpretable evidence. When any of the three is skipped, the dual-deployment produces output differences that cannot be reliably attributed to the prompt-scaffolding variation. The discipline cost is small; the discipline benefit is the controlled-experiment design property that makes the dual-deployment uniquely informative.

## What this changed in my workflow

The discipline I have committed to since the dual-deployment surfaced is to run a same-decade dual deployment whenever I am about to commit to a substrate's axis-behavior characterization for a research arc. The dual-deployment provides the controlled-experiment evidence that the axis assignments are reliable; without it, the axis assignments are inferences from cross-decade reproductions that could be confounded with prompt-template variation.

The operational pattern is straightforward. At the start of a research arc, after the first decade's substrate deployments produce their outputs, deploy at least one substrate at the same decade a second time with a different prompt-template scaffolding. Analyze the two outputs for preserved-versus-varied properties. The analysis takes 30 minutes to an hour; the analysis produces the axis-assignment evidence that the rest of the arc's substrate deployments will rest on.

If the dual-deployment shows that a substrate's axis assignment is responsive to prompt scaffolding when I expected it to be substrate-intrinsic, the axis assignment needs to be reconsidered. The dual-deployment is the falsifiability check on the axis assignments; without it, the axis assignments are running on inference from cross-decade reproductions that are evidentially weaker than controlled comparison.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the n=1 same-decade dual-deployment in my arc is sufficient evidence for the methodology's full generalization. One same-decade dual-deployment at one substrate at one decade is a single observation; the methodology candidacy depends on reproductions across other decades and other substrates. The structural argument (within-substrate dual-deployment isolates prompt-responsive properties from substrate-intrinsic properties) is consistent with the single observation but is not validated across substrate-set or topic-set variation by it alone. Further reproductions would close the validation question.

I am not claiming the within-decade dual-deployment is the only controlled-experiment design for substrate behavior. Other configurations produce different kinds of evidence: same-substrate different-decade dual-deployment with the same prompt-template (isolates decade-content as the variable); cross-substrate same-decade same-prompt dual-deployment (isolates substrate identity as the variable); same-substrate same-decade same-prompt dual-deployment at different times (isolates temporal-state as the variable). The within-decade different-prompt configuration is the configuration that surfaces in my arc; the methodology generalizes to other configurations with their own discipline requirements.

I am not claiming the dual-deployment substitutes for cross-substrate verification. The two configurations produce different evidence types; using one to substitute for the other under-equips the research arc with the wrong evidence type for the question at hand. Operators running multi-substrate workflows should use the dual-deployment as a cheap supplement to cross-substrate verification, not as a replacement.

I am not claiming the three operational disciplines are exhaustive. Other disciplines could improve the dual-deployment's evidence quality: pre-registration of the expected substrate behavior before the second deployment runs (to reduce post-hoc rationalization of the output differences); blind analysis of the two outputs before knowing which prompt-template produced which (to reduce operator bias in the interpretation); third-party review of the analysis (to catch operator-side confirmation bias). The three disciplines I named are what I have committed to so far; other operators running the dual-deployment may add disciplines that fit their workflow constraints.

I am not claiming the dual-deployment can characterize all substrate axes. The within-decade dual-deployment isolates prompt-responsiveness from substrate-intrinsicness for the axes the dual-deployment surfaces. Substrate behaviors that vary across factors the dual-deployment does not vary (e.g., behaviors that depend on training-data updates rather than on prompt-template choices) are not characterized by this configuration. Other configurations would surface those axes; the dual-deployment is one tool in the characterization toolkit, not the complete toolkit.

## What I am claiming

Within-decade same-substrate dual deployment is the cheapest controlled-experiment design for substrate behavior I have found. The configuration holds substrate identity and topic-content constant while varying prompt-template scaffolding; the comparison isolates substrate-intrinsic properties from prompt-responsive properties at within-substrate level. The single observation in my arc showed framework-adherence is substrate-intrinsic and content-emphasis is prompt-responsive for the substrate I characterized, which complements the cross-decade reproduction evidence in [Post 82](082-two-axis-substrate-style-preservation.md) by isolating which axis is which.

If you are running a multi-substrate research workflow and you need axis-assignment evidence for a substrate's behavior, the within-decade dual-deployment is a configuration that produces controlled-experiment evidence at low cost. The three operational disciplines (hold topic constant; structure the prompt variation; analyze side-by-side for preserved-versus-varied properties) are what make the configuration informative. Skipping any of the three produces output differences that cannot be reliably attributed to prompt-scaffolding variation; running all three produces axis-assignment evidence that the rest of the arc's substrate work can rest on.

The methodology candidacy is open until further reproductions surface across other substrate-set and topic-set variations. The single observation is sufficient to argue the configuration produces interpretable evidence; the configuration's full generalization to other research arcs is the open work that further reproductions would clarify. The discipline cost is small enough that operators running characterization work for their substrate set should default to running the configuration at the start of each research arc, not as a special-case methodology when something seems off.
