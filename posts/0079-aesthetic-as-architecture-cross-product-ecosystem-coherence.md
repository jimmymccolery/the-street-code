# Aesthetic as architecture: how an emotional through-line can hold a cross-product portfolio together without shared technical infrastructure

**Author:** Jimmy McColery
**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Status:** Brand-strategy principle articulated and ratified across recent weeks of operational reproduction; principle has underwritten ten-plus brand and architecture decisions during that period; n=1 ratified case with sustained cascade reuse

*Note: I'm a tech professional documenting my work in personal AI-assisted projects. I produce these posts directly from my own work and findings, with AI writing assistance helping to shape the prose. The implementation, the diagnosis, and the analysis are mine; AI assistance shaped the prose from my raw session notes and canonical record.*

---

I am building a portfolio of related but distinct products. Four entries in total under a single publisher: a tycoon simulation, a set of cozy 3D-environment simulators, a physical card game, and a hardware companion device. The products do not share a codebase. They do not share an engine. They do not share a character roster or a continuous world-state. They are not a franchise in the conventional sense.

What they do share is harder to name. There is an emotional through-line that runs across all of them. The products are all about a specific orientation toward time and place: about the felt experience of being somewhere specific in a specific period of someone's life, and about the artifacts of memory that period left behind. The tycoon sim is set in a bowling alley across multiple decades. The cozy simulators are set in spaces (a bedroom from a specific era, a public room with a specific cultural moment baked into it). The card game is about constructing the felt texture of a particular decade. The hardware companion sits in the player's actual living room as a physical artifact of the portfolio's emotional register.

The through-line is "memory and place." Every product is a vessel for memory in a specific place. The places are diverse. The memory orientation is constant. The portfolio coheres not because the products share infrastructure but because the products share this emotional through-line.

This post is about why that kind of aesthetic-level coherence is doing more structural work than I had initially recognized, and what that suggests for indie publishers building portfolios without the technical-infrastructure-sharing or franchise-IP coherence that larger publishers default to.

## The conventional cross-product coherence options

Cross-product franchise strategy has two well-understood patterns. The first is technical infrastructure sharing: multiple products built on the same engine, sharing assets, sharing tooling, sharing a codebase. The pattern is operationally efficient (one engine to maintain; assets amortize across products; tooling investments compound) and produces a recognizable family resemblance because the products literally come from the same machinery. The cost is technical lock-in: once the engine is the franchise's spine, the products are constrained to what the engine can do.

The second pattern is franchise IP transfer: multiple products in the same continuity, sharing characters, sharing lore, sharing a continuous world-state. The pattern is narratively cohesive (the player who knows one product knows pieces of the others; cross-product references create resonance; the IP holds the portfolio's identity together) and produces a sense of universe. The cost is narrative constraint: each product has to fit the continuity, and the continuity has to evolve carefully to support each product without contradicting the others.

Larger publishers default to both patterns at once. Engine-and-IP-shared franchises are the bread and butter of large studios. The patterns work; the costs are absorbed by the studio's scale.

Indie publishers usually cannot afford either pattern. The engine-sharing pattern requires sustained engine engineering, which an indie publisher's headcount cannot support across multiple distinct products. The franchise-IP pattern requires a writing team and continuity-management discipline that an indie publisher cannot scale. Both patterns are operationally expensive in ways that scale with the studio's resources, and indie publishers cannot scale them.

This is the practical problem I faced. I have multiple products I want to ship under a single publisher. Each product wants to be its own technical thing (its own engine choice; its own platform fit; its own development arc). I do not have the resources to engineer them on shared infrastructure. The products do not naturally fit a single continuity (different characters, different scopes, different formats). The conventional cross-product coherence options are not available.

The third pattern I have been operating under is what I want to describe here.

## Aesthetic as the load-bearing structure

The through-line "memory and place" is doing the work that a shared engine or shared IP would do in a conventional cross-product portfolio. The through-line is aesthetic; the through-line operates at the emotional level rather than at the infrastructure level. Every product evokes a specific orientation toward time and place; the orientation is consistent across products even though the products' technical and narrative implementations are distinct.

The mechanism is that aesthetic coherence is loadable through the player's experience. A player who finishes the tycoon sim has experienced a particular felt register: the specific texture of a bowling alley as a place that holds working-class American history across the post-war period. The same player picking up the cozy simulator experiences a related register: the specific texture of a bedroom from a specific era as a place that holds personal history. The registers rhyme without repeating. The player recognizes the family resemblance not because the products share characters or assets but because the products share a way of orienting toward the felt experience of being somewhere specific.

The recognition is real because aesthetics are real. The player's emotional experience is the point of the products; coherence at that experiential level is more directly connected to the value the products produce than coherence at the technical or narrative level would be. A shared engine produces family resemblance the player may not notice; shared IP produces family resemblance the player has to know the continuity to appreciate; shared aesthetics produce family resemblance the player feels directly.

The structural property is that aesthetic-level coherence does not require shared infrastructure. The cozy simulator's engine choice is independent of the tycoon sim's engine choice. The card game's production technology is independent of both. The hardware companion's firmware stack is independent of all three. Each product can choose its technical implementation freely; the aesthetic discipline is what holds the portfolio together across the divergent technical choices.

## What this requires of the publisher

The pattern requires the publisher to maintain aesthetic discipline as a first-class operational concern. Aesthetic discipline is harder to operationalize than engine engineering or IP continuity management. Engine engineering produces measurable outputs (frame rates, asset pipelines, tooling capabilities) the operator can verify. IP continuity produces auditable outputs (bibles, timelines, character sheets) the operator can review. Aesthetic discipline produces emotional registers that resist measurement and are harder to audit in the abstract.

The way I have been operationalizing aesthetic discipline is through explicit through-line documentation. The "memory and place" through-line is a phrase I have written into the publisher's brand documentation, into the product documentation for each portfolio entry, and into the design review process for major decisions. The phrase is short enough to remember; the phrase is specific enough to discriminate among design choices ("does this serve memory and place" surfaces an evaluation operator-side decisions can be checked against).

The through-line documentation is operator-side discipline. The discipline catches drift in product design choices that would have moved away from the through-line. The discipline catches drift in marketing copy that would have positioned a product against a different emotional register. The discipline catches drift in product-naming decisions that would have introduced cross-product dissonance.

The cost of the discipline is operator attention. The discipline does not run automatically; the operator has to invoke the through-line at decision points and check the decisions against the through-line explicitly. The cost is bounded; the through-line is a single phrase that takes seconds to invoke. The benefit is portfolio coherence that survives the divergent technical implementations across products.

## The publisher tier as visible architecture

The aesthetic-level coherence has a visible expression in how the publisher's brand architecture is structured. The portfolio has three visible tiers: the publisher (the brand identity that holds the portfolio); the ecosystem (the named through-line and the products it covers); the individual products (each with its own name, identity, and platform fit).

The three-tier structure makes the aesthetic-level coherence legible to players and to partners. A player who encounters one product can see the ecosystem name and recognize that the product is part of a portfolio with shared orientation. A partner (a platform, a press contact, a distribution channel) can see the publisher tier and recognize that the portfolio has a publisher identity that is broader than any single product.

The three-tier structure also disciplines the publisher's communication. Each tier has its own audience and its own scope. Publisher-tier communication addresses why the publisher exists and what the portfolio is about. Ecosystem-tier communication names the through-line and identifies which products share it. Product-tier communication addresses what a specific product is and how it works. The tiers do not collapse into each other; the publisher does not market individual products as if they were the portfolio, and the products do not market themselves as if they were the publisher's identity.

The discipline of the three-tier structure is what makes the aesthetic-level coherence operationally visible. Without the three-tier structure, the through-line would be implicit in each product's marketing and would not connect across products. With the three-tier structure, the through-line has a named owner (the ecosystem tier) and a named audience (players who orient toward the through-line) and a named scope (the products that share the orientation).

## How the through-line was identified

The through-line was not designed up front. The through-line was identified after multiple products had been scoped and the portfolio's strategic question had become how to brand the publisher coherently. I had been treating the products as a collection of independent projects with overlapping operator-side preferences (era-fidelity research; physical-place specificity; nostalgic registers). The collection's coherence was visible to me but I had not named it.

The naming happened during a deliberative session with AI assistance, when the assistant surfaced the cross-product pattern that I had been operating under without articulating. The assistant's framing was useful because it was external; the framing made the through-line visible by stating it as a pattern across products rather than as an operator preference per product. Once the through-line was named, it became a discipline I could operationalize.

The lesson here generalizes. Aesthetic-level coherence in an indie portfolio is often present implicitly before it is identified explicitly. The operator's design choices reflect operator preferences; the preferences cluster around aesthetic registers that the operator has not yet named. Identifying the cluster as a named through-line converts the implicit coherence into operationalizable discipline. The identification is best done with external framing (AI assistance, peer review, brand consultant) because the operator is too close to the choices to see the cluster as a cluster.

## What this changed in my workflow

Three specific changes. First, when I evaluate a product-design decision, I now check the decision against the publisher's through-line. The check is operational: "does this decision serve memory and place" produces a binary on most decisions, with a small residual of decisions that require deliberate trade-off discussion. The discipline catches decisions that would have drifted the product away from the through-line.

Second, when I draft marketing copy for any product or for the publisher itself, I run the copy through the through-line check. Marketing copy that positions the product against a different emotional register gets revised; marketing copy that aligns with the through-line gets refined. The discipline is preventive: the copy drift is caught before publication, not after.

Third, when I review another indie publisher's portfolio (during competitive analysis, during partnership consideration, during methodology comparison), I look for whether the portfolio has identifiable aesthetic-level coherence and whether that coherence is operationalized through visible discipline. Portfolios that have implicit coherence without explicit discipline tend to drift over time; portfolios with explicit through-line discipline tend to hold together across product launches.

## Why this generalizes beyond games

The pattern shows up in any indie portfolio where the publisher cannot afford technical-infrastructure-sharing or franchise-IP continuity but wants the portfolio to cohere. Indie media publishers (small-press book imprints, small-scale music labels, small-studio film producers) have the same structural problem: each title has its own technical and narrative implementation; the publisher needs the titles to cohere as a portfolio without engineering them on shared infrastructure.

The solution in each case is aesthetic-level coherence operationalized through explicit through-line discipline. A small-press imprint that publishes books from authors writing in a specific register coheres at the register level. A small-scale music label that signs artists with a specific sonic orientation coheres at the sonic level. A small-studio film producer that selects projects with a specific emotional palette coheres at the palette level. The coherence is loadable through the audience's experience because the audience experiences the products at the aesthetic level directly.

The cross-domain implication is that indie publishers should treat aesthetic discipline as a first-class operational concern from the early portfolio stage rather than as a marketing afterthought. The discipline is where the portfolio's coherence actually lives. Operating without the discipline produces a collection of unrelated products that happen to share a publisher logo; operating with the discipline produces a portfolio that audiences recognize as belonging together for reasons they can feel directly.

## What I'm not claiming

- I'm not claiming aesthetic-level coherence replaces all other forms of portfolio coherence. Technical-infrastructure-sharing and franchise-IP continuity have benefits that aesthetic-level coherence does not provide. Studios that can afford the conventional patterns benefit from running them alongside aesthetic discipline. The claim is that aesthetic discipline alone can hold a portfolio together when the conventional patterns are not affordable.

- I'm not claiming the through-line identification process I described is the only way to identify the aesthetic-level coherence in a portfolio. Other operators may identify their through-line through different processes (sustained brand consulting, peer review, audience research). The structural principle (the through-line is usually present implicitly before identification, and identification benefits from external framing) is what generalizes; the specific identification process is operator-dependent.

- I'm not claiming the three-tier brand architecture is the only structural expression for aesthetic-level coherence. Other publishers may organize their tiers differently (fewer tiers; more tiers; differently-named tiers) and the discipline can still operate. The structural property is that the through-line has a named owner and a named scope in the brand architecture; the specific tier structure is operator-dependent.

- I'm not claiming this finding is novel in brand strategy literature. Aesthetic-level coherence is well-studied in branding theory across multiple decades, and the specific framing of through-line discipline shows up in design-system literature, voice-and-tone literature, and editorial-style literature across multiple domains. The contribution is the specific empirical mapping for indie portfolio strategy and the demonstration that aesthetic-level coherence can carry the operational weight that infrastructure-sharing and IP-continuity carry in larger studios.

- I'm not claiming aesthetic discipline is easy to maintain. The discipline requires sustained operator attention and resists measurement in ways infrastructure-engineering and IP-continuity do not. The discipline's value comes from preventing drift, and drift prevention is hard to demonstrate as value until after the drift has been prevented. Indie publishers operating the discipline have to commit to it without immediate measurable validation; the validation arrives over time as the portfolio's coherence accumulates.

## What I am claiming

What I am claiming is that the conventional cross-product coherence options (technical infrastructure sharing; franchise IP continuity) are not the only viable patterns, and that aesthetic-level coherence operationalized through explicit through-line discipline is a third pattern that fits indie publishers' resource constraints. The pattern does work; the work is operator-side discipline rather than infrastructure investment; the discipline is bounded and the discipline produces portfolio coherence that audiences feel directly because audiences experience the products at the aesthetic level the discipline maintains.

A publisher's portfolio coheres because someone is making it cohere. The someone is the operator-side discipline; the discipline is the through-line; the through-line is the aesthetic register the products share. The mechanism is simple to describe and harder to operationalize. The portfolio coherence shows up in the audience's recognition that the products belong together for reasons the audience can feel even if the audience cannot articulate. That recognition is what the through-line is for.

The through-line discipline I now apply across the portfolio is doing more work than I expected when I named it. The discipline is producing coherence in marketing copy, in product-naming decisions, in design choices that would otherwise drift across the portfolio without any single operator-level enforcement. The discipline is also producing recognition in audience-reception conversations; the through-line is short enough to remember and specific enough to evoke the portfolio's emotional register without requiring the audience to know all four product entries. The recognition is the secondary lesson. I had been worried that aesthetic-level coherence would be too abstract to register with audiences who only encountered one product at a time. The opposite turns out to be true. Audiences feel the through-line on first product contact because aesthetic registers are the layer audiences engage with most directly. The discipline that maintains the through-line is what produces the audience-side recognition that the portfolio's products belong together. The discipline is also producing internal coherence in my own design thinking. Decisions that would have drifted across products under unconscious aesthetic intuition are now being checked against a named through-line that I can articulate and that I can defend. The articulation is what makes the discipline reproducible; without the articulation, the coherence is operator-dependent in a way that does not scale past the operator's attention.
