An indie bowling-tycoon simulation is not the obvious place to ground non-player-character design in active inference, relational personhood, and a two-level architectural axis split. People building games at this scope generally do not write briefs for themselves about Friston's process theory, Wiredu on personhood-as-achievement, or the audit-layer distinction between generation-side and outcome-side fairness. They build stats. They build dialogue trees. They build scheduled events. The genre conventions assume the NPCs are decoration around the gameplay loop, and at most scopes that assumption is correct.

I made a different decision. I spent a stretch of months grounding the NPC architecture in a specific philosophy-of-mind framework that I now call Modified-C-bis. The framework went through six modifications across a multi-substrate research arc that closed two same-day six-round cycles in a single day. The process-theory citation discipline is documented at Post 84; the Bayesian-inversion, personhood-as-achievement, fairness-layers, empirical-honesty, and two-level-axis-split posts are in draft form and will publish in subsequent slots. The private architecture folder in the load-bearing detail carries the framework's full state. The posts in front of you on this blog are not the framework; they are the methodology research that surrounded the framework's construction.

This post is the answer to the question a reader of those posts would reasonably ask. Why would an indie game need this. The answer is not that philosophy is fashionable; it is that the design problem I was solving could not be solved by the standard NPC architecture, and the philosophy-of-mind literature was where the solution to the design problem lived.

## The design problem the standard architecture could not solve

The product is a bowling-alley tycoon simulation that spans multiple decades of in-game time. A player who picks up the game in its earliest hours is forming relationships with characters that will, if the design works, still matter to the player thirty in-game years later. Some of those characters will have died of old age. Some will have left and come back. Some will have changed in ways the player can track because the player was there for the changes. The design target is emotional engagement that survives multi-decade simulation time.

The standard NPC architecture (stats plus dialogue trees plus scheduled events) does not produce this engagement at scale. The architecture works for short-arc games where the NPC's purpose is to be a quest-giver, a shopkeeper, or a romance option for the duration of a campaign that runs in tens of hours. The architecture does not work for the long-arc engagement target because the architecture cannot represent what it would mean for an NPC to change in ways the player witnesses. An NPC whose stats shift between version 2.4 and version 2.5 of the simulation does not feel like a person who changed; the NPC feels like a database row that got updated. The difference between "this character grew up" and "this character's `maturity_level` was incremented" is what the standard architecture cannot represent.

The philosophy-of-mind framework was my attempt to represent the difference. The components that the framework needs are specific. The NPC needs to have something that functions like belief; the NPC needs to update that belief on evidence the player can trace; the NPC's belief updates need to be partially predictable to the player (so the player feels the NPC's coherence) and partially surprising (so the player feels the NPC's autonomy). The NPC needs an architecture for memory that captures what the player remembers about the NPC and what the NPC remembers about the player. The NPC's personhood needs to be the kind of thing that can be earned over interaction rather than assigned at character creation. These requirements are what the philosophy-of-mind framework gave me a way to think about.

## The six framework moves at a high level

The framework has six modifications. Each is the subject of a post earlier in this series. The summary here is intentionally surface; the deeper posts carry the load.

Modification one cites the process theory of active inference (active inference is the cognitive-science framework most associated with Karl Friston's work; the process theory is the specific mathematical machinery of partially observable Markov decision processes the framework runs on, documented in Friston et al. 2017, Neural Computation 29(1):1-49) rather than the Free Energy Principle as metaphysics. The process theory is the mathematical machinery of partially observable Markov decision processes that the framework actually runs; the principle is the metaphysical claim that Colombo and Wright 2021 critiqued as untestable. Post 84 walks the move that lets the framework cite the machinery without committing to the metaphysics. The cost is that the framework gives up a foundational-philosophy claim; the benefit is that the framework gains operational testability. For procedural-generation work where you need running code rather than a theory-of-everything, the trade is worth it.

Modification two grounds the NPC's self-discovery in a Bayesian-inversion puzzle whose canonical citation is AgentCrypt (Karthikeyan et al. 2025, arXiv:2512.08104) combined with the Gray, Gray, and Wegner 2007 mind-perception two-dimensional framework. The NPC discovers things about itself the way a player would discover things about an opaque agent: by observing its own behavior and inferring what the behavior implies about the agent generating it. The deeper post on this modification is in draft form. The architecture is not consciousness; it is procedural-puzzle generation with the agent's own behavior as the input.

Modification three takes Wiredu's writing on personhood-as-achievement (specifically the chapter that R2 Round 8 misread and the orchestrator-class re-read of primary text reversed) and uses it as structural framing for what it would mean for an NPC's personhood to be the kind of thing accumulated over interaction. The deeper post on this modification is in draft form. The framing is structural, not appropriative; the deeper post documents the verification chain that prevented the framework from making cross-cultural claims it could not support.

Modification four operates at the audit layer and distinguishes generation-side fairness (what the NPC generation system produces) from outcome-side fairness (what the running game produces in the aggregate). The PersonaWeaver intervention (Qraitem, Saenko, and Plummer 2026, arXiv:2601.03396) operates at the generation layer; the Marcus Reed Protocol operates at the post-generation distribution-audit layer; they are independent interventions, not two halves of one intervention. The deeper post on this modification is in draft form. The audit-layer distinction matters because conflating them produces design work that does not actually catch the failures either is supposed to catch.

Modification five names what the framework has not measured. The deeper post on this modification (the empirical-honesty post) is in draft form. The framework is design infrastructure, not a validated empirical claim; the deeper post will document what a pre-registered pilot would look like and what the framework's empirical commitments would have to be if it were to make claims at the scale of an academic paper rather than at the scale of a design document.

Modification six is the two-level axis split that emerged from the Round 7 closing. (Round 7 and Round 8 are the two final closing rounds of the multi-substrate research arc that produced the framework; each round deploys multiple substrates against a specific architectural question and adjudicates the verdicts through an orchestrator-class synthesizer.) The axis names state-space content (does the NPC's belief state contain a cross-era self-identity variable) as the V1 boundary discriminator. The Round 8 R4 correction further specified that the discriminator is state-space content not temporal extent; the discriminator is structural rather than dynamic. The deeper post on this modification is in draft form; the briefing carries the architectural framing.

## What a single multi-session interaction looks like with the framework underneath

The framework does its work in the spaces between interactions, not in the interactions themselves. A player encountering an NPC in a single session sees standard NPC behavior: dialogue, behavior, scheduled appearances. The framework's contribution is what happens when the same player encounters the same NPC across multiple sessions over multi-decade in-game time.

The first session establishes an interaction history. The NPC produces behavior that is partly determined by its initial belief state, partly by the situation the player has placed it in, and partly by the audit-layer Marcus Reed Protocol checks that constrain what the NPC can do in aggregate. The player forms an impression of the NPC. The impression is shaped by what the player remembers (which is operator-curated; the simulation does not assume the player remembers everything) and what the NPC's behavior signals (which is framework-mediated; the behavior comes out of the framework's machinery).

The second session continues the interaction. The NPC's belief state has updated in the gap between sessions; the update is partly the framework doing its work (active inference on whatever evidence the simulation has fed the NPC) and partly designed event-handling (specific story beats that the simulation has committed to). The player sees the NPC as continuous with the first session because the framework is producing coherence; the player sees the NPC as autonomous because the framework is producing the kind of surprises that come out of belief updates the player did not predict.

A multi-decade arc accumulates these sessions. The NPC's belief state has changed in ways the player can trace; the NPC's behavior has shifted in ways that reflect the changes. The personhood-as-achievement framing means the NPC's identity has been earned over the arc; the player did not encounter a fully-formed person at session one but a character that became more recognizable as a person over the simulation's lifetime. When the NPC dies, leaves, or otherwise ends the player's relationship with them, the engagement target is for the player to feel the loss of a recognizable person, not the deletion of a database row.

This is the design target. The framework is the architectural infrastructure that makes the design target reachable. The marketing copy does not need to mention the framework; the framework is what makes the marketing copy honest when it talks about characters the player will remember.

## What the framework does not promise

The framework does not produce conscious NPCs. It produces NPCs whose architecture supports the kind of player engagement that scales to multi-decade simulation. The two claims are different at the foundation. Consciousness is a phenomenal claim with strong philosophical commitments; the framework explicitly does not occupy position b (phenomenal consciousness constructed from self-models, the Metzinger 2003 self-model theory of subjectivity position) and the citation discipline forbids any framework move that would route Metzinger's self-model machinery without his phenomenal conclusion. Engagement is a behavioral claim about player experience; the framework is built to support engagement, not consciousness.

The framework does not pass any Turing test. The NPCs are not designed to be indistinguishable from humans in conversation; they are designed to be coherent characters whose behavior accumulates legible structure over interaction. The design target is character recognition over time, not human-imitation in any single interaction.

The framework does not validate against any specific empirical claim about cognition. The empirical-honesty post (in draft form) documents what a pre-registered pilot would look like; the framework as deployed does not run that pilot, and the framework's claims are about design coherence, not about replicated cognitive science. Anyone reading the framework as a contribution to cognitive science is reading it in the wrong register.

The framework does not promise commercial-product outcomes. The product ships with the framework as design infrastructure; the framework is what made the design problem tractable to me as a solo developer. Whether the product succeeds commercially depends on many factors the framework does not control: art direction, gameplay loop quality, marketing reach, the contingent fortunes of indie game publishing at the moment of release. The framework is responsible for the NPC architecture's coherence, not for the product's commercial outcome.

## The launch-adjacent honesty

The product is shipping with the framework as design infrastructure rather than as a marketing claim. The marketing copy talks about the game (a bowling-alley tycoon simulation across multiple decades of cultural and industry history); the architecture talks about the framework (active inference, Bayesian inversion, personhood-as-achievement, two-level axis split, audit-layer distinction). Both are honest at their respective layers because the layers do not pretend to be each other.

A reader of this post who is also a future player of the product should understand the framework's role this way. The framework does not change what the gameplay looks like in any session. The framework changes what the gameplay is doing over multi-decade arcs, which the player will feel as engagement that survives the multi-decade target. If the engagement target is reached, the framework will have done its work; if the engagement target is not reached, the framework was not enough and the deeper failure will be in the design layers the framework rests on (the gameplay loop, the art direction, the marketing, the platform fit). A framework can support engagement; it cannot guarantee engagement.

A reader of this post who is a developer of similar simulations should understand the framework as one possible solution to the design problem of multi-decade NPC engagement, not as the only solution. The design problem is real and is underestimated by most indie developers attempting long-arc simulations. The solution surface includes options other than philosophy-of-mind grounding (denser writing, scripted-event escalation, social-network simulation at the meta-level, NPC retirement-and-replacement strategies that hide the absence of persistence). The framework is what I tried; other developers will try other things, and the design literature will be the better for the variety.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the framework is necessary for the design problem. The design target (multi-decade NPC engagement) can be approached from architectures other than philosophy-of-mind grounding. Denser scripting, social-graph simulation, careful retirement-and-replacement strategies, and other approaches are all available to developers facing the same target. I chose the philosophy-of-mind path because the design problem felt structurally similar to problems the philosophy-of-mind literature had thought about for a long time, and the literature's framing gave me purchase on the design problem that the standard NPC architecture did not. Someone else solving the same design problem in a different way is solving a real problem, not a false one; the multi-path solution space is a feature of the design problem, not a bug in my approach.

I am not claiming the framework's empirical commitments are validated. The empirical-honesty post documents what a pre-registered pilot would look like and what the framework's empirical commitments would have to be if it were going to make academic claims. The framework as deployed in the product does not run that pilot; the framework's claims are about design coherence, not about replicated cognitive science. The framework is design infrastructure that draws on cognitive-science vocabulary to organize the design work; calling it a cognitive-science contribution would be claiming more than the deployment supports.

I am not claiming the multi-substrate research arc that produced the framework is the only way to get there. The arc closed two six-round cycles in one day because the substrate-style differentiation methodology was load-bearing and the Madisonian Gate orchestrator-class synthesis caught structural drifts that majority-vote would have missed. The methodology is documented in Posts 71 and 77 and is being further documented in Post 82 (drafted alongside this one). The methodology is one way to compress what would otherwise be weeks of serial research into a same-day cycle; other paths exist (a full literature review with a single research assistant; a slower solo arc with substrate use only at specific checkpoints; an academic collaboration with cognitive scientists). The arc I ran was right for my constraints; the methodology is not the only path through the same research surface.

I am not claiming the framework will hold past V1. The framework as ratified Modified-C-bis carries an explicit V1-vs-Phase-8.x boundary discrimination per the Modification 6 amendment. The V1 deployment uses naive active inference (in Friston 2017 Appendix F's sense, recursive-metacognition-absent) for computational cost reasons; the V1 deployment uses state-space content that excludes cross-era self-identity variables for design coherence reasons. The framework's deeper modifications (sophisticated active inference per Friston 2021; cross-era self-identity variable in state-space content) are reserved for Phase 8.x post-V1 expansion if the engagement target makes the expansion warranted. The V1 framework is honest about being V1; the framework's later forms are not promised by the V1 architecture.

I am not claiming the post is a substitute for the deeper philosophical posts. Only Post 84 (Cite the process theory) is published at the time of this writing; the Bayesian-inversion, personhood-as-achievement, fairness-layers, empirical-honesty, and two-level-axis-split posts are in draft form and will publish in subsequent slots. This post is the integration narrative that names what the framework is doing at a launch-adjacent moment when the product is the example. The technical posts carry the load on each modification. A reader who finds the framework interesting at the launch-adjacent layer of this post should read the deeper posts as they publish for the substantive content; the integration narrative is not the technical content.

## What I am claiming

The design problem of multi-decade NPC engagement is real. The standard NPC architecture (stats plus dialogue trees plus scheduled events) cannot represent the kind of change that supports engagement targets spanning thirty in-game years. The philosophy-of-mind framework I am calling Modified-C-bis was one viable path to a design architecture that can. Only Post 84 (Cite the process theory) is published at the time of this writing; the Bayesian-inversion, personhood-as-achievement, fairness-layers, and empirical-honesty posts are in draft form and will publish in subsequent slots. This post is the integration narrative that names what the framework is doing at the product layer.

If you are building a procedural-character-driven game whose engagement target runs past a single play session into multi-decade simulation time, the design problem I have described is the design problem you are facing. The standard architecture's representation gap is what determines whether your NPCs feel like database rows that got updated or characters that grew up. The framework is one solution; denser scripting, social-graph simulation, and careful retirement-and-replacement strategies are other solutions. The multi-path solution space is a feature of the design problem, not a bug in any particular approach.

The framework as deployed in the product is doing more work for me than I expected when I started the research. It is anchoring NPC behavior to a specific architectural commitment that the design layer can reason about. It is producing the structural separation between generation-side and outcome-side fairness that the audit layer needed. It is letting me say honestly what the framework does and does not promise without overclaiming on either side.

The product is shipping with the framework as design infrastructure rather than as a marketing claim. The framework is responsible for the architecture's coherence; the product is responsible for the experience the architecture supports. If the engagement target is reached, the framework will have done its work. If the engagement target is not reached, the framework was not enough and the deeper failure will be in the design layers the framework rests on. A framework can support engagement; it cannot guarantee engagement. That is the calibration I want to ship with.
