## Hook

I borrow structural elements from cross-cultural traditions for procedural-character design. I do not implement those traditions. The difference matters, and this post is my public commitment to maintaining the difference through a specific operational discipline.

## The five-element disclosure discipline (canonical statement)

The discipline has five components stated below in canonical form. Each component is operational. The discipline does not eliminate cross-cultural interpretive risk; the discipline bounds the risk to specific retraction-condition-handleable failure modes. The discipline is published openly so that critics can evaluate the framework against the discipline's commitments rather than against unstated standards.

### Element 1: Acknowledgment language

The framework's posts and architecture documents use phrasings that frame the engagement as structural borrowing rather than as implementation or representation. Specifically: "I draw on structural elements of [tradition]" or "I borrow structural inspiration from [tradition]" or "the framework declines the use of [tradition's specific framework] through quantitative crossing of demographic variables, while retaining [tradition's structural element] at the philosophical-grounding layer." The posts and documents do not use "I implement [tradition]" or "I use the [tradition] view" or any phrasing that conflates structural borrowing with operationalization or with representation.

The acknowledgment language is operational discipline. Specific phrasings produce specific reader expectations; the framework's commitment is to phrasings whose reader expectations match what the framework actually does. The phrasings are not optional; the discipline applies wherever the framework's content engages any cross-cultural tradition.

### Element 2: Primary-text-only citation

The framework's cross-cultural claims cite primary texts directly. Substrate paraphrases of primary texts do not substitute for the operator's own primary-text reading. The discipline is operational because substrate paraphrase chains produce specific kinds of errors that primary-text reading catches; the framework's research arc has documented multiple primary-text reversals of canonical state that majority-vote substrate synthesis would have ratified incorrectly (the F-17 Wiredu universalist reversal and the F-14 Friston Appendix F reversal documented in posts 0110 and 0117 in this arc).

### Element 3: No cross-cultural vocabulary in code identifiers

The framework's implementation uses neutral terminology for variables, function names, type names, and property names. Specifically: no Akan, Yoruba, Zulu, Sanskrit, Mandarin, Spanish, or any other culturally-loaded vocabulary in `case` values, function names, type names, or property names. Neutral identifiers: `pair_memory`, `relationship_edge`, `core_event`, `reflective_summary`, `cooperation_trait`. The cross-cultural philosophy stays at the framework-paper and research-paper layer; the code layer stays neutral.

The discipline matters because code identifiers travel into developer tooling, version control, documentation generators, and downstream code review; the cross-cultural vocabulary in any of those locations creates representational exposure that the framework cannot defend at the moment-of-deployment scale. The discipline is operational; the framework's implementation discipline includes pre-commit checks that catch culturally-loaded identifiers and flag them for replacement; the discipline is enforced at the code-review stage.

### Element 4: Opening and closing humility paragraphs

Every framework post that invokes any cross-cultural tradition opens and closes with a humility paragraph. The opening paragraph specifies the post's specific cross-cultural scope, the kind of borrowing the post is doing, the kind of representation the post is not making, and restates the retraction condition. The closing paragraph restates the structural-borrowing-only framing, acknowledges what is not being claimed, and restates the retraction condition.

The opening humility paragraph appears at the top of this post (immediately after the disclaimer in the metadata block). The closing humility paragraph appears just below the "What I am claiming" section. The discipline is operational; the humility framing is not optional; the framework's posts that engage cross-cultural traditions without the framing would be unstructured engagement rather than disciplined engagement.

### Element 5: Post-publication response discipline and retraction condition

If a knowledgeable critic (in-tradition scholar; advocate; community member) shows material misrepresentation of any cross-cultural tradition in any framework post: the framework retracts the cross-cultural framing within 14 days of receipt of the critique. The technical content of the post stays if it stands independently; partial retraction is acceptable when the framing fails but the technical content is independent. "Material misrepresentation" is judged by primary-text defensibility; substrate-paraphrase defenses do not carry weight in the judgment.

The communication channels for critique are: GitHub issues on the framework's research repo; direct email; X/Twitter for amplification. The framework's commitment to the response-discipline is operational; the discipline does not just bound the framework's exposure, it commits the framework to a specific response procedure with a specific timeline.

The retraction condition is what makes the discipline accountable. The discipline is not just a set of phrasings the framework adopts; the discipline includes the specific response procedure the framework commits to in the event of critique. The accountability is what distinguishes the discipline as operational from the discipline as aspirational.

## Why this discipline exists

I am a solo indie developer, not a peer-reviewed scholar. The framework's commitments are publication-grade for solo-operator research-blog publication; the commitments are not scholarship-grade for peer-reviewed academic-journal publication. The discipline is appropriate to the publication context.

Cross-cultural traditions deserve respect on their own terms, not as raw material for game design. The framework's commitment to structural-borrowing-only is the framework's operational expression of the respect; the alternative (operationalizing the traditions' substantive content without the scholarship the operationalization would require) would be ornamental deployment at best and extraction at worst.

The framework would prefer no cross-cultural framing over careless cross-cultural framing. The discipline is the framework's commitment to do better than careless; the discipline is what makes the framework's cross-cultural engagement defensible at the publication-context standards rather than at scholarship standards.

The discipline is the framework's commitment to do better than careless even when the framework's solo-operator context does not require the broader scholarship standards. The commitment is operational; the commitment is published openly so the framework is held accountable to a specific public standard.
