## Component A — Operator-side role architecture as canonical artifact

The canonization-scope asymmetry the canonical record had named was structural. The AI-side role architecture was canonical at `role_output_schemas.md` (canonical artifact, v1.1, with five canonical roles and explicit Status: Canonical at the file-header level). The operator-side functional roles enumerated at [Post 18](018-what-running-on-auto-pilot-revealed-about-framework-structure.md) were at observation level — captured at `lessons-learned.md` but not wrapped in a canonical artifact with file-header canonization status. The asymmetry was named at `research_queue.md` Topic 14 and remained open across several sessions. The canonical record audited itself, named the gap, and held the gap open until the canonization fired.

Decision 1C closed the gap. `operator_role_archetypes.md` v1.0 was canonized on 2026-05-10 with five operator-side archetypes ratified canonical — The Architect, The Helmsman, The Sentinel, The Cartographer, The Steward — plus one held as research with explicit revisit criteria: The Herald. The hybrid ratification was Council-of-Three unanimous on path (c) across substrates. The empirical foundation for ratification was the existing per-archetype evidence enumerated at Post 18; what Decision 1C added was the canonical artifact wrapping that evidence in a versioned file with explicit Status: Canonical at the file-header level.

The v1.0 to v1.1 progression added per-archetype empirical-foundation depth (134 to 223 lines; ~1,800 to 3,952 words). Filed at `lessons-learned.md` line 1585.

Three sub-decisions within Component A warrant naming because each surfaced a substrate-side or operator-side bias the canonization had to navigate.

**Tarot framing versus archetype naming.** The substrate's initial recommendation favored Tarot Major Arcana register-parallel anchoring — The Magician, The Hierophant, The Hermit, and so on. The register parallel was aesthetically clean and substrate-coherent across the role enumeration. The operator surfaced the question "which holds truer to actual roles?" and prompted honest per-role re-examination. Archetype framing won substantively across most roles. The Magician's broad function did not match The Architect's specific framework-design work; The Hierophant's institutional register did not match The Helmsman's operational steering; The Hermit's solitude framing did not match The Cartographer's methodology-research function. The substrate's Tarot recommendation was a substrate-side anchoring bias caught by operator query rather than discovered by substrate-side reflection. The pattern matters because the substrate had no internal signal that the Tarot register was overdetermined by its own pattern-matching rather than by the actual roles being named. Filed at `lessons-learned.md` line 1320.

**Sage collision avoidance via Cartographer.** The methodology-researcher role had a naming candidate that collided with an existing AI-side canonical name. Sage is already canonized at `role_output_schemas.md` v1.1 as Benjamin/Teacher's Pet's embedded function. The operator chose Cartographer to avoid AI-side embedded function collision. The structural observation: AI-side canonization can preempt operator-side naming territory. This was the first canonical-record-discipline step toward closing Topic 14 canonization-scope asymmetry, named months before Decision 1C fired the canonization itself. The observation surfaced naturally because the operator-side architecture was being built in awareness of the AI-side architecture; the asymmetry the canonical record had named meant the operator-side work had to navigate AI-side canonical territory rather than treating the two as independent.

**Hybrid ratification as corrective template.** Five canonical plus one research-held with explicit revisit criteria. The Herald role's per-element evidence was thinner than the five canonical archetypes; canonizing all six uniformly would have inflated canonization status beyond empirical foundation. The hybrid ratification preserves per-element empirical-foundation asymmetry rather than smoothing it under a single canonical declaration. Per-element ratification became canonical-record-discipline precedent: when a multi-element artifact's components have different empirical foundations, ratification fires per-element rather than per-artifact. The discipline is conceptually parallel to the multi-fire bar at the anti-pattern catalogue level — both refuse to canonize at uniform status when empirical foundation varies across elements.

Component A's structural outcome: the operator-side canonical artifact now exists parallel to the AI-side canonical artifact. The canonization-scope asymmetry is closed at the file-existence level. Topic 14 at `research_queue.md` is partially closed; the deeper question of whether canonization scope can drift between substrate-led and operator-led work over time remains open as cross-arc accumulation candidate.

## Component B — Date-boundary discipline at canonical-record-boundary scope

The canonization at Decision 1C required propagating new names through the canonical record. The bulk-rename work touched 76+ pre-2026-05-08 dated entries at `lessons-learned.md` and supporting artifacts. At Stage 1.5 of the canonical-record bulk-rename arc, a rollback caught Version History blocks that had mutated alongside current-state content. The Version History blocks were historical narrative — descriptions of what happened on specific prior dates, including references to the prior names. Mutating them silently corrupted the canonical record's own historical source for the prior names.

The discipline that emerged: canonical-record bulk-rename work must distinguish current-state body content (which mutates) from historical narrative content (which preserves verbatim). Date-boundary at 2026-05-08 served as operational cutoff. Content describing what happened on or before the boundary preserves original names. Current-state framing uses new names. The boundary at 2026-05-08 was the canonization date itself; anything pre-canonization preserves the pre-canonization naming.

The deeper framing: this is anti-pattern #10 (canonical-source discipline) applied at canonical-record-boundary scope. The canonical-source discipline warns against rewriting canonical source material to match later understanding. The canonical record IS its own historical source for the prior names. Mutating its historical narratives to match the post-canonization naming silently corrupts the source. The same anti-pattern that applies to external canonical sources applies to the canonical record's own historical content during bulk-rename work.

The discipline's articulation matters because the temptation to silently mutate everything was real. Historical narrative entries describing prior naming would have read more consistently to a reader who only knows the post-canonization names. But "more consistent reading" is exactly what canonical-source discipline warns against: the inconsistency between historical naming and current-state naming is empirical record, not stylistic blemish. Filed at `lessons-learned.md` line 488.

Stage 3 of the bulk-rename arc applied the discipline systematically across the 76+ pre-boundary entries. Component A's canonization fired cleanly because Component B's discipline preserved the canonical record's own historical narrative through the propagation work. The within-arc rollback at Stage 1.5 was the empirical fire that surfaced the discipline; the systematic Stage 3 application was the operational implementation. [Post 21](021-s009-corrective-sub-stage-arc.md) documents the corrective sub-stage framing the date-boundary discipline inhabits.
