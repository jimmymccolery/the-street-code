## The graduated achievement structure

Wiredu's reading of Akan personhood frames *onipa* as a graduated concept. Personhood, in Wiredu's reading, is not a categorical given conferred at birth; personhood is something a person achieves continually through engagement with the relational and moral conditions of the community. Wiredu Chapter 9 *Person and Community: Ghanaian Philosophical Studies, I* (Wiredu and Gyekye, editors, 1992), Council for Research in Values and Philosophy Series II.1, page 200: "every cultivated Akan *Okaniba* sees life as a scenario of continual striving after personhood in ever increasing dimensions." The phrasing is Wiredu's; the framing is that personhood is a process, not a category.

The graduation matters structurally because it produces a personhood axis the framework can observe move. A categorical personhood concept has only two states (present or absent); a graduated personhood concept has a continuum, and the continuum supports both forward movement (the person's standing rises through striving) and backward movement (the person's standing falls through failure of striving). The framework's structural borrowing adopts the continuum; what the framework does not borrow is any specific Akan content about what counts as striving, what counts as achievement, what the relational conditions of community look like, or what specific kinds of failure produce specific kinds of standing-loss. The Akan tradition has substantive content on each of these questions; the framework declines to operationalize the substantive content because operationalizing it without the philosophical scholarship the operationalization would require would be ornamental deployment (the kind of risk Bilge 2013 *Du Bois Review* 10(2):405-424 names as the borrowing of prestige without substance).

## The failure axis

The graduated personhood concept admits explicit failure judgments. Wiredu Chapter 9 page 201 documents the Akan vocabulary for non-person and not-quite-person judgments: *onipa hunu* (literally "empty person") describes a person whose conduct has so violated the relational and moral conditions of personhood that the person is judged as having forfeited some measure of standing; *onye onipa* (literally "not a person") is the strongest judgment available, applied to conduct that has placed the person outside the relational conditions of community altogether. The vocabulary is normative; it is a moral-virtue judgment, not an ontological denial of biological humanity (the distinction matters specifically for the Gyekye counter-position; see below).

The framework's structural borrowing adopts the failure axis. A personhood concept that admits explicit failure is the structural element the framework draws on; the framework's NPCs can be designed so their standing can fall through specific kinds of conduct, and the falling is observable in NPC-NPC and NPC-player interactions in ways that align with the graduated personhood frame. The framework does not borrow the specific Akan vocabulary; the framework's identifiers are neutral (the failure axis appears in code as `standing_modifier`, `relational_violation_count`, or similar neutral terms, not as *onipa hunu* or *onye onipa*). The vocabulary stays with the philosophical tradition; the structural element travels into the framework as structural borrowing.

The moral-danger element of the failure axis is the element that is hardest to translate cleanly into procedural-character design. Wiredu's reading is that the failure judgment is not just a status downgrade; the failure judgment carries a moral weight that the community responds to with specific patterns of engagement (avoidance, censure, ritualized repair pathways, etc.). The framework's structural borrowing adopts the moral-weight element as a design pattern (failure judgments produce specific patterns of NPC response; the patterns differ from the patterns produced by mere status downgrade); the framework does not adopt the specific Akan community-response patterns. The framework's NPCs respond to standing-loss in ways shaped by the framework's other architecture commitments (HEXACO trait modulation; attachment-style modulation; sentiment-override per Hawkins, Carrère, and Gottman 2002 *Journal of Marriage and Family* 64(1):193-201); the response pattern is not Akan, it is the framework's own shape informed by the broader empirical literature.

## The relational constitution

Wiredu Chapter 9 page 201-202: "*Onipa nye abe na ne ho ahyia ne ho*" (glossed in English as "a human being is not a palm tree"). The phrase is Wiredu's gloss on the relational requirement of personhood. The palm tree is the canonical Akan metaphor for a being that stands alone, self-sufficient, complete in itself without dependence on other beings; the human being, by contrast, is constituted through relations with other beings, and the constitution is ongoing rather than a one-time event. Personhood, in this reading, is relationally produced; the relations are not external to personhood, they are constitutive of it.

The structural element the framework borrows is the relational constitution. The framework's NPCs are designed so their standing is partly a function of the network of relations they maintain with other NPCs and with the player; the standing is not an individual property that an NPC carries independently of the relational context. The framework's pair-relational history mechanism (each ordered pair of characters maintains a sentiment scalar; the scalar is updated by interaction events; the scalar biases interpretation of subsequent interactions per Hawkins-Carrère-Gottman sentiment-override) is the implementation-level expression of the relational constitution. The mechanism is grounded in empirical psychology (Gottman's marriage-research literature), not in Akan philosophy; the structural shape (relational-context as constitutive rather than as decoration) is what the framework borrows from Wiredu's reading.

The framework's structural borrowing here is double. The Akan philosophical tradition supplies the structural insight (personhood is relationally constituted); the empirical psychology literature supplies the operational machinery (the sentiment-override mechanism). Neither is sufficient on its own; the Akan tradition without the operational machinery would be ornamental, and the operational machinery without the structural insight would lose the design coherence that makes the framework's pair-relational pattern more than a Sims-style relationship-meter. The combination is the framework's contribution; the Akan tradition is one of the structural-insight sources, and Wiredu's reading is the specific source the framework cites.

## The recoverability inference

The graduated personhood concept implies recoverability. If personhood is achieved through continual striving (Wiredu page 200), and if striving can resume after failure, then the failure is not permanent. Dzobo Chapter 6 *Person and Community*, page 135, develops a parallel framing in Ewe philosophical tradition: *Dzudzo mele alifo o* ("there is no resting place" in the Ewe-language gloss), which Dzobo reads as a structural commitment that personhood-as-achievement does not have a terminal-state property; the achievement is ongoing and the failure is not terminal because the resting place where the failure would be terminal does not exist as a structural option.

The recoverability axis is what the framework borrows. The framework's NPCs can lose standing through specific kinds of conduct, and they can recover standing through subsequent striving; the recovery is not automatic and is not free, but the recovery pathway exists structurally. The framework's mechanic for recovery is shaped by the framework's other architecture commitments (sentiment-override dynamics; consolidation-pass updates; trait-modulated recovery rates); the mechanic is not Akan or Ewe in any specific content sense, but the structural commitment that recovery is structurally available is what the framework borrows from Wiredu and Dzobo's readings.

The recoverability axis has a specific naming history I want to surface because the secondary-literature attribution matters. The Sands secondary literature (the framework's research arc references Sands as the source for the "recoverability" naming as a category) names the axis "recoverability" and develops it as a category within the broader cross-cultural personhood literature. The framework cites Sands for the naming attribution; the structural grounding for the recoverability axis itself is at Wiredu page 200 (continual striving) and Dzobo page 135 ("no resting place") directly. The distinction matters because secondary-literature naming and primary-text grounding are different kinds of citation; the framework's discipline keeps them separate, and the post should be explicit about which kind each citation is.

## The Gyekye counter-position

Wiredu's reading is not the only reading of Akan personhood within Akan philosophy. Gyekye, who co-edited *Person and Community* with Wiredu and who maintained a long-running philosophical dialogue with him, holds a Kant-grounded counter-position: personhood is inherent at birth (a being that is biologically human is, by virtue of biological humanity, a person with intrinsic moral status); the communal context enhances personhood through relational accountability and role fulfillment but does not constitute it; the Akan judgment "*onnye nipa*" applied to cruel or selfish conduct, in Gyekye's reading, is a normative judgment of moral virtue rather than an ontological denial of personhood status.

The Gyekye counter-position is documented in Wiredu's own introduction to *Person and Community* (pages 10-11), where Wiredu explicitly notes that Gyekye disputes the achievement-graded view that Wiredu (and Menkiti in the earlier 1984 paper "Person and Community in African Traditional Thought") defends. Gyekye's fuller treatment is in Gyekye 1997 *Tradition and Modernity: Philosophical Reflections on the African Experience* (Oxford University Press). The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Akan philosophy gives Gyekye's counter-position equal philosophical weight to Wiredu's reading; the SEP framing is the standard "the field has not converged on a single view" framing, and the framework's engagement with Akan philosophy adopts the SEP framing as the working canonical picture.

The framework's structural borrowing is from Wiredu's reading, not from "the Akan view." Presenting "personhood-as-achievement" as "the Akan view" would be a category error that misrepresents the internal pluralism of Akan philosophy; the honest framing is that the framework borrows the structural logic of Wiredu's reading, that the reading is contested within Akan philosophy itself, and that the framework's choice of Wiredu's reading over Gyekye's counter-position is a choice within a contested field. The choice is the framework's responsibility, and the post should be explicit about it. A subsequent post in this arc will engage with the Wiredu-Gyekye dispute in more depth; the present post acknowledges the dispute in the humility-bracket sense, and the deeper engagement lives in the dedicated subsequent post.

## The discipline I am committing to publicly

The five-element disclosure discipline the framework adopts for cross-cultural engagement has five components and I am stating each of them here in canonical form. Future posts in this arc adhere to the discipline; the present post is the first instance where the discipline operates at the maximum-cross-cultural-exposure level, and stating the discipline canonically here makes the subsequent posts' adherence checkable against a public reference.

First, acknowledgment language. The framework's posts and architecture documents use phrasings like "I draw on structural elements of Wiredu's reading of Akan personhood-as-achievement" or "structural inspiration from a cross-cultural philosophical tradition"; the posts and documents do not use "I implement Wiredu" or "I use the Akan view" or any phrasing that conflates structural borrowing with implementation or representation. The acknowledgment language is operational discipline; specific phrasings produce specific reader-expectations, and the framework's commitment is to phrasings whose reader-expectations match what the framework actually does.

Second, primary-text-only citation. The framework's cross-cultural claims cite primary texts directly; substrate paraphrases of primary texts do not substitute for the operator's own primary-text reading. The discipline is operational because substrate paraphrase chains produce specific kinds of errors that primary-text reading catches; the framework's research arc has documented multiple primary-text reversals of canonical state that majority-vote substrate synthesis would have ratified incorrectly. Primary-text grounding is the discipline that closes the substrate-paraphrase-chain failure mode for cross-cultural claims where the failure mode is most consequential.

Third, no cross-cultural vocabulary in code identifiers. The framework's implementation uses neutral terminology for variables, function names, type names, and property names. Akan, Yoruba, Zulu, Sanskrit, Mandarin, Spanish, or any other culturally-loaded vocabulary stays at the framework-paper and architecture-document layer; the code layer stays neutral. The discipline matters because code identifiers travel into developer tooling, version control, documentation generators, and downstream code review, and the cross-cultural vocabulary in any of those locations creates representational exposure that the framework cannot defend at the moment-of-deployment scale.

Fourth, opening and closing humility paragraphs. Every framework post that engages with any cross-cultural tradition opens and closes with a humility paragraph that specifies what is not being claimed (the post's specific cross-cultural scope; what kind of borrowing the post is doing; what kind of representation the post is not making) and that restates the retraction condition. The opening humility paragraph appears at the top of this post (the first paragraph after the disclaimer); the closing humility paragraph appears just below the "What I am claiming" section. The discipline is operational; the humility framing is not optional, and the framework's posts that engage cross-cultural traditions without the framing would be unstructured engagement rather than disciplined engagement.

Fifth, post-publication response discipline and retraction condition. If a knowledgeable critic (in-tradition scholar; advocate; community member) shows material misrepresentation of any cross-cultural tradition in any framework post, the framework retracts the cross-cultural framing within 14 days of receipt of the critique; the technical content of the post stays if it stands independently; partial retraction is acceptable when the framing fails but the technical content is independent. "Material misrepresentation" is judged by primary-text defensibility; substrate-paraphrase defenses do not carry weight in the judgment. The communication channels for critique are: GitHub issues on the operator's research repo; direct email; X/Twitter for amplification. The framework's commitment to the response-discipline is operational; the discipline does not just bound the framework's exposure, it commits the framework to a specific response procedure with a specific timeline.

The five components together are the discipline. Each component is operational. The discipline does not eliminate cross-cultural interpretive risk; the discipline bounds the risk to retraction-condition-handleable. The framework's commitment is that bounded risk under a public discipline is more honest engagement than unbounded risk under no discipline, and the discipline is published openly so that critics can evaluate the framework against the discipline's commitments rather than against unstated standards.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the framework's structural borrowing represents the Akan tradition. The framework borrows structural elements from Wiredu's reading; the framework's architecture is informed by the borrowing; the framework does not claim to represent the tradition, and no portion of the framework's implementation is offered as an Akan implementation. The Akan tradition's authority does not transfer to the framework; the framework's design choices are the framework's own responsibility, and the responsibility is what the discipline above is structured around.

I am not claiming the framework's structural borrowing exhausts the structural elements available from Wiredu's reading. The reading has further substantive content (the *Onyame* dimension of Akan religious cosmology; the *suban* (character) versus *nipadua* (body) distinction; the ritual contexts for personhood-conferring participation; the role of language and naming in personhood-acquisition) that the framework's borrowing does not engage. The framework borrows four structural elements (graduation, failure, relational constitution, recoverability) that align with the framework's architecture commitments; the broader content of Wiredu's reading stays with the tradition and the philosophical scholarship that engages it. The framework's scope is narrow by design; the narrow scope is what makes the borrowing defensible.

I am not claiming Wiredu's reading is uncontested within Akan philosophy. Gyekye's counter-position is documented above and is given equal philosophical weight by the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy; the framework's choice of Wiredu's reading over Gyekye's counter-position is a choice within a contested field, and the choice is the framework's responsibility. A subsequent post engages the Wiredu-Gyekye dispute in more depth; the present post acknowledges the dispute and proceeds with the structural borrowing from Wiredu's reading with full disclosure that the choice is contested.

I am not claiming the framework's V1 NPCs will exhibit the graduated personhood structure in a way a player will recognize as such. The structural elements the framework borrows inform the architecture; the player's experience of the architecture's commitments is downstream of many design decisions (UI surfacing; pacing; narrative framing; player-side onboarding to the NPCs' behavior patterns). The architecture commits to the structural elements; the player's experience is the empirical question the framework will engage when V1 ships and the pre-registered pilot runs. I am not claiming the architecture's commitments produce the player experience the framework's design is reaching for; the empirical claim is open until the pilot's results come in.

I am not claiming the cross-cultural disclosure discipline above is sufficient for all critique. The discipline bounds the framework's exposure to retraction-condition-handleable; the discipline does not eliminate the possibility that a knowledgeable critic will identify problems the discipline does not surface. The framework's commitment is to the discipline as a public standard that the framework will be held accountable to; the discipline is a floor for the framework's engagement, not a ceiling. Critiques that go beyond what the discipline anticipates are welcome and will be engaged on their own terms; the discipline is what the framework commits to publicly so that the engagement has a public starting point.
