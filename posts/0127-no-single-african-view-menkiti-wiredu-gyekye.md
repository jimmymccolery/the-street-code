## Hook

## What Wiredu's view is

Wiredu Chapter 9 *Person and Community: Ghanaian Philosophical Studies, I* (Wiredu and Gyekye, editors, 1992; Council for Research in Values and Philosophy Series II.1) develops the achievement-graded view of Akan personhood. The relevant passages from the primary text (verified against the framework's Round 8 R4 closing-round re-read):

Page 200: "every cultivated Akan *Okaniba* sees life as a scenario of continual striving after personhood in ever increasing dimensions." The passage establishes the graduated achievement structure; personhood is not a categorical given conferred at birth; personhood is something the person achieves continually through engagement with the relational and moral conditions of the community.

Page 199: sympathetic impartiality "is the first principle of all morals" and "the logical basis of the golden rule." The passage establishes the universalist framing of the procedural moral test that operates within the achievement-graded personhood structure (the F-17 reversal confirming the universalist framing is documented in post 0117 in this arc).

Pages 201-202: the relational-constitution requirement framed through the metaphor "*Onipa nye abe na ne ho ahyia ne ho*" (glossed in English as "a human being is not a palm tree"; the palm tree is the canonical Akan metaphor for a being that stands alone, self-sufficient, complete in itself without dependence on other beings; the human being, by contrast, is constituted through relations with other beings). The passage establishes the relational-constitution element of the framework's structural borrowing.

The Akan judgment "*onnye nipa*" applied to cruel or selfish conduct is, in Wiredu's reading, a graduated personhood-status judgment. The judgment is not an ontological denial of biological humanity; the judgment is a moral-virtue assessment that the person has, through specific conduct, fallen below the graduated personhood threshold the relational community recognizes. The judgment's structural shape supports the failure-axis element of the framework's structural borrowing (post 0112 in this arc).

Wiredu's reading aligns with Menkiti's earlier work. Menkiti, I. A. (1984), "Person and Community in African Traditional Thought," in Wright (editor) *African Philosophy: An Introduction*, argues that personhood is acquired through participation in communal life and ritual incorporation; the Menkiti reading shares the achievement-graded structural commitment with Wiredu's reading despite differences in tradition-specific content (Menkiti's Nigerian/Igbo background; Wiredu's Ghanaian/Akan background). The Menkiti-Wiredu agreement on the achievement-graded structure across two African philosophical traditions has been cited (in subsequent philosophical literature) as evidence for the structure's broader applicability across African philosophy.

## What Gyekye's counter-position is

Gyekye's counter-position is documented in the *Person and Community* volume that Gyekye co-edited and in Gyekye's fuller treatment of the broader Akan philosophical project in subsequent work. Specific anchors:

Wiredu's own introduction to *Person and Community* (pages 10-11) explicitly notes that Gyekye disputes the achievement-graded view that Wiredu and Menkiti defend. The dispute is documented within the volume itself; the volume does not present a unified "Akan view" but rather presents Wiredu's reading alongside Gyekye's counter-position with explicit philosophical engagement between the two readings.

Gyekye 1997, *Tradition and Modernity: Philosophical Reflections on the African Experience*, Oxford University Press, develops Gyekye's broader philosophical project including the Kant-grounded personhood reading. The book is Gyekye's fullest single-author treatment of the philosophical commitments that Gyekye's counter-position is grounded in.

The substantive content of Gyekye's counter-position: personhood is INHERENT at birth. A being that is biologically human is, by virtue of biological humanity, a person with intrinsic moral status. The grounding is Kant-influenced: the universalist moral framework Kant develops in the *Groundwork* and the *Critique of Practical Reason* extends to all rational beings; Gyekye reads the Akan moral tradition as compatible with the Kantian framework's grounding of moral status in the rational nature of the moral agent rather than in the agent's specific achievement-graded performance.

The communal context, in Gyekye's reading, ENHANCES personhood through relational accountability and role fulfillment but does not CONSTITUTE it. The community context matters for the person's moral development; the community context does not change the person's underlying moral status; the moral status is given at the biological-humanity level and the communal context shapes how the person expresses the status through engagement with the community.

The Akan judgment "*onnye nipa*" applied to cruel or selfish conduct, in Gyekye's reading, is a normative judgment of moral virtue rather than an ontological denial of personhood status. The judgment criticizes the conduct as morally deficient; the judgment does not claim that the person has fallen below a personhood threshold; the judgment claims that the person has failed at moral virtue while remaining a person whose moral status is intact.

The Wiredu-Gyekye dispute is over the structural commitment of Akan personhood. Wiredu (and Menkiti) read the tradition as committed to graduated achievement; Gyekye reads the tradition as committed to Kant-grounded inherent personhood with relational enhancement. The two readings are philosophically substantive; both readings have textual grounding in the broader Akan moral-philosophy literature; both readings have been defended in extensive philosophical work; the field has not converged on either reading as definitive.

## The Menkiti-Wiredu agreement and intra-African philosophy more broadly

Menkiti (Nigerian and Igbo background) and Wiredu (Ghanaian and Akan background) agree on the achievement-graded view from different cultural starting points. The convergence across two African philosophical traditions is significant; the convergence is sometimes cited in the broader literature as evidence for the achievement-graded view's broader applicability across African philosophy beyond any single tradition's specific content.

The Menkiti-Wiredu convergence does not exhaust African philosophy. The broader African philosophical landscape is plural; African philosophical traditions span multiple specific cultural contexts (Akan; Yoruba; Zulu; Igbo; Maasai; Amharic; and many others; each tradition has its own specific philosophical content); the broader Pan-African philosophical work engages with multiple traditions plus the cross-cultural philosophical dialogue between African traditions and Western philosophy plus the diaspora philosophical work that engages African-derived philosophy in non-African contexts.

Presenting any single view as "the African view" is a category error. African philosophy is plural; the pluralism is at the cultural-tradition level (within each specific African philosophical tradition) and across the broader African philosophical landscape (across traditions). The framework's discipline acknowledges the pluralism; the discipline does not present any single view as universal across African philosophy; the discipline is operational across the framework's broader cross-cultural engagement.

The acknowledgment matters for the framework's structural-borrowing discipline.
