## What the WEIRD critique is

Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan 2010, "The weirdest people in the world?" *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 33(2-3):61-83, documents the WEIRD critique systematically. The paper's substantive content: most psychology research samples WEIRD populations (approximately 80%+ of publications per the paper's empirical survey at the time of publication; the WEIRD-sample-overrepresentation continues in subsequent years per ongoing literature reviews); WEIRD samples represent a small fraction of the world's population (approximately 12% per the paper's demographic analysis at the time of publication); WEIRD samples are unusual on multiple cognitive and behavioral dimensions (perception including the Müller-Lyer optical illusion susceptibility; fairness allocation in economic-game settings; individualism-versus-collectivism patterns; analytic-versus-holistic reasoning patterns; IQ measurement patterns); generalizing from WEIRD samples to "humans" as a category is a methodological error that produces specific kinds of cross-cultural-generalization failures.

The critique applies wherever psychology underwrites a procedural-character architecture. The framework's architecture uses HEXACO traits (validated initially in WEIRD samples; subsequent cross-cultural extensions exist with mixed findings); attachment style (Fraley, Waller, and Brennan 2000 ECR-R; WEIRD-sample validation; cross-cultural attachment literature is mixed; Mikulincer and Shaver 2016 *Attachment in Adulthood* second edition documents the cross-cultural complications); Russell circumplex valence-arousal (Russell 1980; WEIRD-sample validation; cross-cultural emotion-research literature has substantial complications); Gottman sentiment-override (Hawkins, Carrère, and Gottman 2002; U.S. married-couple sample; cross-cultural marriage-research literature is mixed). The accumulator's dimensions and the trait sources feeding the accumulator are each grounded in literature that has WEIRD-sample validation as the primary empirical base; the cross-cultural generalization complications are real for each grounding.

The accumulator's specific dimensions (valence; arousal; stress per the DC1 split documented in post 0118 in this arc) were validated on WEIRD samples first. The cross-cultural emotion-research literature has documented some patterns that hold across cultures (valence-arousal as broadly applicable affective dimensions; specific facial-affect recognition patterns at high cross-cultural reliability; specific physiological-stress-response patterns at moderate cross-cultural reliability) and some patterns that vary across cultures (specific emotion-category boundaries; specific arousal-modulation contexts; specific cultural-display rules for affect expression). The framework's adoption of the dimensions is supported by the cross-culturally-applicable elements; the framework's adoption is also exposed to the cultural-variation elements; the schema-placeholder approach is the framework's operational response to the exposure.

## The Crenshaw intersectionality angle

Crenshaw, K. (1989), "Demarginalizing the intersection of race and sex: A Black feminist critique of antidiscrimination doctrine, feminist theory and antiracist politics," *University of Chicago Legal Forum* 1989(1):139-167, and Crenshaw, K. (1991), "Mapping the margins: Intersectionality, identity politics, and violence against women of color," *Stanford Law Review* 43(6):1241-1299, develop the intersectionality framework as a critique of single-axis demographic analyses. The framework's substantive content: single-axis demographic frameworks (race-only OR gender-only OR class-only) marginalize multi-axis lived experience because lived experience operates at the intersections rather than at single axes; Black women's experience, Crenshaw's canonical example, cannot be derived by combining "Black experience" plus "women's experience" separately because the intersection produces specific patterns that neither single-axis framework captures.

For procedural characters, the intersectionality framework has architectural implications. Storing a single "ethnicity" field OR a single "gender" field misses the multi-axis identity that the intersectionality framework documents as load-bearing for lived experience. Storing MULTIPLE positional tags per character (the schema-placeholder approach the framework adopts; the specific tag schema is documented below) lets the framework represent intersectional experience without collapsing to single-axis. The collapsing-to-single-axis failure mode is what the intersectionality framework names; the multi-tag schema-placeholder is the framework's operational response.

The framework's V1 game-logic does not branch on the intersectional tags. The V1 commitment is to reserve the schema space for the multi-tag representation; the V1 game-logic does not operationalize the intersectionality framework through quantitative crossing of demographic variables (the operationalization would require scholarship the framework's solo-operator publication context does not have access to; the operationalization would risk ornamental deployment per Bilge 2013 *Du Bois Review* 10(2):405-424). The schema-placeholder approach is the V1 commitment; the post-V1 work that would operationalize the tags is a separate research-program scope.

## The Cross Nigrescence angle
