# Substrate-Style Differential at n=10+: When Cross-Substrate Audit Becomes a Methodology Rather Than an Overhead

**Author:** Jimmy McColery
**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Status:** Methodology consolidation post; n=10+ cumulative reproductions across seven architectural-lock close cycles plus the Pulse 7 wrong-axis convergent-failure observation that added a new reproduction class; extends Posts 06 and 11

*Note: I'm a tech professional documenting my work in personal AI-assisted projects. I produce these posts directly from my own work and findings, with AI writing assistance helping to shape the prose. The implementation, the diagnosis, and the analysis are mine; AI assistance shaped the prose from my raw session notes and canonical record.*

---

In [Post 06](0006-what-auditing-the-audit-taught-me-about-substrate-routing.md) I described an early observation that cross-substrate audit chains surfaced different findings than single-substrate audits and named the substrate-style-differential pattern as a candidate methodology. In [Post 11](0011-what-cross-substrate-audit-surfaced-that-same-substrate-verification-missed.md) I extended the observation to a second instance where cross-substrate verification caught what same-substrate verification missed. Both posts treated the pattern as suggestive at small n. This post is the consolidation at substantially extended empirical foundation. The pattern has reproduced across seven architectural-lock close cycles plus a recent within-cycle observation that added a new reproduction class. The cumulative count is at ten-plus, and the structural framing has sharpened in ways the two-post-old version did not capture.

This post and Post 82 cover adjacent angles on the substrate-style-differential thread. Post 82 is the predictive substrate-behavior model (the two-axis decomposition surfaced from the multi-decade historical-research arc); this post is the consolidation of the substrate-style-differential pattern at architectural-lock close cycles specifically, where the depth-and-coverage shape and the orchestrator-class adjudication discipline are load-bearing. Both extend Posts 06 and 11 from the early framing; both contribute to the broader cross-substrate verification design space at different abstraction layers. Read them as complementary rather than as redundant.

The architectural-lock cycles in question are points in a design-document workflow where a specific section of the document gets ratified through a cross-substrate verification process before being committed to canonical state. Each close cycle deploys multiple substrates against the same audit target, each substrate produces a verdict with supporting reasoning, and an orchestrator-class synthesizer (in my workflow, a sixth substrate with project-knowledge access) adjudicates the verdicts and produces the close-cycle ratification. The cycles produce naturally-occurring empirical data on cross-substrate verification because the same audit target gets seen by multiple substrates under controlled prompt configurations.

## What the n=10+ count actually counts

The reproduction count is across seven architectural-lock cycles plus the Pulse 7 wrong-axis convergent-failure observation. The cycles I am counting are: an early multi-alley state architecture audit, an early statistical-simulation-bowling-without-physics audit, an architectural-lock cycle on a specific worker-thread serialization decision, a chronological-mode research arc close, two architectural-lock cycles on town-as-sim architecture (one structured and one gap-fill), a Phase 7.1 cycle close, and a recent Pulse 7 within-cycle observation that surfaced a new reproduction class which I will describe specifically later in the post.

In each cycle the pattern reproduced. The pattern is not "one substrate is uniformly better than another"; the pattern is "different substrates produce different verdict shapes, and the cross-substrate set produces aggregate signal that no single substrate produces." Specifically, the pattern reproduced two characteristic differential shapes that I observed across all seven cycles.

First, the substrate identified as a stricter-critique calibration produced verdicts with more mandatory modifications, stricter rejection of underspecified arguments, and deeper architectural-pattern extraction from named-anchor games. A specific subpattern: this substrate extracted concrete architectural patterns from games like RimWorld (Flyweight Pattern for memory-bound entity systems), Cities:Skylines (Death Wave demographic-management mechanic), and Crusader Kings 3 (character culling thresholds for long-arc dynastic simulation) and tied each pattern to a specific architectural claim in the audit target. The substrate's named-anchor extraction was a load-bearing contribution to the audit because the named-anchor games were ones I had not researched in the directly-applicable depth that the substrate's extraction surfaced.

Second, the substrate identified as a softer-critique calibration produced verdicts with fewer mandatory modifications, softer language on underspecified arguments, and shallower architectural-pattern extraction from the same named-anchor games. A specific subpattern: this substrate produced unique-coverage contributions where the stricter substrate did not, by surfacing named-anchor games the stricter substrate had not surfaced (Songs of Syx and Dwarf Fortress as precedents for specific architectural-lock surfaces; specific bowling-industry historical anchors the stricter substrate had not produced). The softer substrate's coverage breadth was a load-bearing contribution to the audit because the unique anchors filled gaps the stricter substrate's depth-on-fewer-anchors missed.

The two characteristic shapes are not strict-better or soft-better. They are complementary depth-and-coverage shapes that produce aggregate signal richer than either shape alone. In every cycle I closed, the synthesis incorporated load-bearing contributions from both shapes; in no cycle did I close on one substrate's verdict alone.

## Why majority vote does not work

A specific finding from the close cycles is that majority vote across substrate verdicts produces the wrong close-cycle outcome at maximum-divergence cycles. The architectural-lock cycles where one substrate produced a strict verdict requiring an iteration cycle and another produced a soft verdict ready-to-commit are the cycles that test the synthesis discipline. Majority vote on these cycles is not a workable rule because a two-substrate cycle produces no majority (one-strict-versus-one-soft is the maximum-divergence shape); a three-substrate cycle produces a majority only if the third substrate breaks the tie, and the third substrate's verdict is itself part of the substrate-style-differential pattern (the third substrate's verdict shape is predictable from its substrate assignment and is therefore not an independent tiebreaker).

The discipline that has held across all seven cycles is to adopt the stricter substrate's reasoning-chain integrity over the softer substrate's hand-waving. Specifically, when the stricter substrate produces a verdict requiring an iteration cycle, the orchestrator-class synthesizer reads the stricter substrate's reasoning chain to identify the specific claims that drove the strict verdict; the synthesizer holds those specific claims against the softer substrate's hand-waving; the synthesizer adopts the iteration-cycle close because the strict claims are reasoning-chain-integral and the soft verdict's hand-waving cannot answer them.

This is not a substrate-quality ranking; it is a substrate-role assignment. The stricter substrate's job in the verification chain is to find what is wrong. The softer substrate's job is to find what is overlooked. The orchestrator-class synthesizer's job is to integrate both. The job assignments produce the discipline of adopting strict-reasoning over soft-hand-waving when they diverge, not because the stricter substrate is better at adversarial judgment in general but because the stricter substrate's contribution to this specific kind of close is the depth-on-claims that the softer substrate cannot match. The softer substrate's contribution is coverage-of-anchors that the stricter substrate cannot match; the two substrates' contributions are complementary at the audit aggregate level even when they diverge at the verdict level.

This is the discipline that the architectural-lock cycles have produced across all seven instances. The discipline does not generalize to non-architectural-lock work surfaces; in other surfaces the substrate-role assignments are different and the strict-over-soft rule may not hold. The discipline is specific to the architectural-lock close-cycle work surface I have been running it on.

## The wrong-axis convergent failure observation

The Pulse 7 close-cycle observation added a new reproduction class to the substrate-style-differential pattern. The observation is specific: two substrates with different content-emphasis axes (in the framing of Post 82's two-axis substrate-style-preservation model) independently rediscovered the same canonical wicking-to-pulse-modulated boundary in the audit target's content; both substrates mapped the boundary to the wrong axis in the document structure.

The wrong-axis failure was not a substrate-individual error; both substrates rediscovered the same canonical boundary, which is itself evidence that the boundary is real and architecturally salient. Both substrates were independently driven to the same boundary by their respective content-emphasis axes; both substrates then placed the boundary in the document at the same wrong location because the document's structural cues for the correct location were below the threshold of either substrate's prompt-attention.

The wrong-axis convergent failure is a new reproduction class because it shows that substrate-style differential is not purely a depth-and-coverage trade-off; it can also produce convergent failure when two substrates with different content-emphasis axes are driven to the same architecturally-real boundary but place it consistently wrong because the document's structure does not signal the correct placement at the prompt-attention level. The reproduction class matters because it identifies a failure mode that cross-substrate verification does not catch by design: convergent substrate output is the signal cross-substrate verification is supposed to validate, but convergent output can be wrong if the substrates are converging on a content discovery rather than a structural placement.

The discipline implication is that cross-substrate convergence is necessary but not sufficient for cross-substrate verification. The convergence signal is reliable for the existence of the content claim (the boundary is real); the convergence signal is not reliable for the structural placement of the content claim (the boundary's correct location in the document). Cross-substrate verification needs a verification pass beyond the convergence layer, and the verification pass has to come from a substrate or human reviewer with structural-attention orthogonal to the content-emphasis axes the substrates are running.

The orchestrator-class synthesizer in my workflow caught the wrong-axis failure in the Pulse 7 close cycle. The synthesizer's structural-attention is at the document-architecture layer rather than at the content-emphasis layer; the synthesizer noticed that both substrates had placed the boundary at the same location and the location did not match the document's architectural conventions for boundaries of that type. The catch was specifically orchestrator-class; the convergent-substrate signal at the content layer was the input the synthesizer ran the structural check against.

## The depth-and-coverage shape generalized

Across the seven architectural-lock cycles, the substrate-style differential has produced a consistent depth-and-coverage shape that I want to name explicitly. The shape is: cross-substrate verification produces depth contributions from substrates with adversarial-strict calibration and coverage contributions from substrates with permissive-broad calibration. The depth contributions catch reasoning-chain integrity failures (specific claims that do not hold under scrutiny); the coverage contributions catch comprehensiveness failures (specific anchors the depth substrates did not surface).

The two contribution shapes are characteristic of the substrate's calibration class. The stricter-calibration substrate's depth contribution is its load-bearing output; the softer-calibration substrate's coverage contribution is its load-bearing output. Mixing the two substrates without naming the role assignments produces audit chains that have both contributions floating without explicit credit; naming the role assignments explicitly in the audit chain design produces aggregate signal that the orchestrator-class synthesizer can integrate more cleanly.

The role assignments are not fixed across all workflows. In my work I have characterized the substrate calibrations across architectural-lock close cycles specifically; the same substrates in non-architectural-lock workflows may exhibit different calibration profiles because the work surface changes the substrate's behavior. The discipline of naming the role assignments in a specific workflow is what is generalizable; the specific assignments are workflow-specific and require recharacterization when the workflow changes substantially.

The depth-and-coverage shape also gives the operator a framing for cross-substrate verification cost-benefit. The depth contribution and the coverage contribution are both load-bearing; running a verification chain that captures only one of them is leaving the other on the table. Running a verification chain that captures both costs more (more substrates deployed, more synthesis overhead, more close-cycle time) but produces materially better aggregate signal than running either alone. The cost-benefit calculation is workflow-specific and the operator's discretion: for high-stakes architectural-lock work, the cost is justified; for low-stakes review work, the cost is overhead and the operator can pick one calibration class and accept the missing contribution from the other.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes since Post 11. First, I now explicitly name the role assignments in cross-substrate audit chains rather than leaving them implicit. When I deploy a cross-substrate audit at an architectural-lock close cycle, I assign the stricter-calibration substrate to the depth role and the softer-calibration substrate to the coverage role; I tell each substrate explicitly what its role in the audit chain is, in the prompt; I tell the orchestrator-class synthesizer what to integrate from each role's output. The explicit role assignments produce less synthesis ambiguity than the implicit assignments I used in the early cycles. The cost is two minutes of prompt-engineering per substrate; the benefit is a cleaner close-cycle synthesis.

Second, I run an explicit structural-placement check at the orchestrator-class layer after cross-substrate content convergence. The Pulse 7 wrong-axis convergent-failure observation showed that convergence at the content layer can be paired with wrong-placement at the structural layer; the structural-placement check at the orchestrator-class layer catches the wrong-placement failure mode by design. The check is specifically: when two or more substrates converge on a content claim, the orchestrator reads the document's architectural conventions for the type of claim and verifies that the substrates' placement matches the conventions; if the placement does not match, the orchestrator flags the placement for explicit close-cycle adjudication rather than adopting the substrate-convergent placement. The check has caught one wrong-placement in two close cycles since I added it; the catch rate is small but the consequence is large because wrong-placement at architectural-lock surfaces produces canonical-state drift that propagates downstream.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the substrate-style differential pattern generalizes uniformly across substrate generations. The substrate landscape changes as training and deployment evolve; substrate calibrations shift across model versions; the role assignments that hold in early June 2026 may not hold in late 2026 or in 2027. The pattern's structural shape (depth-and-coverage as complementary contributions; orchestrator-class synthesis as adjudicator; majority-vote as the wrong synthesis rule for maximum-divergence cycles) is more durable than the per-substrate assignments. The per-substrate assignments themselves are characterized for a specific substrate set at a specific time and need recharacterization as the substrates change.

I am not claiming the seven architectural-lock cycles are a controlled experiment. The cycles are naturally-occurring close-cycle data from a real design-document workflow; the cycles produced the same pattern across configurations that varied substantially in scope, prompt structure, audit-target content, and synthesizer-substrate assignments. The reproduction is evidence the pattern is not random noise; the reproduction is not evidence the pattern survives controlled-experimental scrutiny that disentangles substrate-intrinsic patterns from operator-side prompt-engineering biases that may correlate with substrate calibrations. The methodology research is research-grade rather than experiment-grade; further validation through multi-operator architectural-lock workflows would extend the foundation.

I am not claiming the strict-over-soft synthesis discipline holds outside architectural-lock work surfaces. The discipline is workflow-specific because the substrate-role assignments are workflow-specific. In other work surfaces, the substrate calibrations may differ (the substrate that runs strict on architectural-lock may run soft on creative-generation; the substrate that runs soft on architectural-lock may run strict on academic-citation work). The role assignments that map to the strict-over-soft synthesis discipline are surface-dependent; the discipline does not transfer to surfaces where the role mapping is different.

I am not claiming the wrong-axis convergent-failure observation reproduces across multiple cycles. The observation is one Pulse 7 cycle; the reproduction is one. The observation is structurally important because it identifies a failure mode that the convergence-as-verification framing does not catch by design; further reproductions across other architectural-lock close cycles would strengthen the foundation. The discipline I have adopted (structural-placement check at the orchestrator-class layer after cross-substrate content convergence) is justified by the structural argument even at n=1 because the cost of the check is small and the consequence of the failure mode is large; the discipline is not justified by replicated empirical evidence.

I am not claiming the substrate-style differential pattern is the only useful framing for cross-substrate verification design. The pattern is the one that emerged from my architectural-lock close-cycle work; other framings (the two-axis substrate-style-preservation model from Post 82; the substrate-role specialization workflow from Post 61; the framework-adherence pattern; the content-emphasis pattern) are independently useful and partially overlap with the substrate-style differential. The framings are not in competition; they describe different aspects of the same multi-substrate workflow design space. The substrate-style differential framing is the load-bearing one for cross-substrate verification design specifically; the other framings carry load on adjacent design surfaces.

## What I am claiming

The substrate-style differential pattern at architectural-lock close cycles is real, reproducible, and structurally specific. Across seven cycles plus the Pulse 7 wrong-axis convergent-failure observation, the pattern produces complementary depth-and-coverage contributions that no single substrate produces. Majority vote across substrate verdicts is the wrong synthesis rule for maximum-divergence cycles. The strict-over-soft discipline at the orchestrator-class synthesis layer is what closes the cycles correctly.

If you are running cross-substrate verification on architectural decisions that will commit to canonical state downstream, the depth-and-coverage shape is what the verification chain needs to capture. Single-axis verification (one substrate as audit; another substrate as confirmation) leaves the other axis on the table. Cross-axis verification (one substrate for depth; another substrate for coverage; orchestrator-class adjudication for synthesis) is what produces aggregate signal the closing-round verdict can stand on. The role assignments are explicit; the substrate-identity is secondary to the role assignment.

The Pulse 7 wrong-axis convergent-failure observation added a new reproduction class that I will be watching for in subsequent arcs. Cross-substrate convergence at the content layer is not sufficient evidence of correct structural placement. The orchestrator-class structural-attention check is the load-bearing discipline beyond the convergence-as-verification framing; without it, convergent substrate output can ratify wrong-placement at the architectural layer. The catch in Pulse 7 was orchestrator-class specifically; the substrates' convergence was the input the synthesizer ran the structural check against.

The pattern's structural shape is more durable than any specific per-substrate assignment. The substrate landscape will shift as training and deployment evolve; the assignments will need recharacterization. The discipline of explicit role assignment plus orchestrator-class structural-placement check will hold through the recharacterization because the discipline is workflow-shaped, not substrate-shaped.
