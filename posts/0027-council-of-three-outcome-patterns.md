## Opening

Decision 1A asked whether drafting-drift should be canonized as a separate anti-pattern row or as a refinement extending existing anti-pattern #10's scope. ## Pattern 1: Minority-position selection (Decision 1A)

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1376, Pattern 1 is "minority-position selection (Decision 1A: Gemini path (a) over Claude + Grok 2-of-3 majority path (b))."

The decision question for 1A was whether to canonize drafting-drift as (a) a separate anti-pattern row in `_anti_patterns.canonical.md`, or (b) a refinement extending anti-pattern #10's scope to explicitly include drafting-from-memory-of-canonical-state as a sub-class. Per `lessons-learned.md` line 1495 Decision 1A entry: "Council outcome: 2-of-3 majority recommended path (b) refinement of #10; Gemini dissented toward path (a) separate row. Operator decision: path (a) separate row per Gemini's reasoning, against Council majority."

The operator decision basis is recorded at `lessons-learned.md` line 1505. The structural reasoning: "Anti-pattern #10's existing framing operates at 'skipping the canonical source' level (verify-before-stating). Drafting-drift operates at 'having canonical access but not reading it before drafting' level (verify-before-mutating-or-quoting). Memory-of-canonical-state and canonical-state-read are distinct verification modalities; treating them as equivalent produces the drift." Gemini's dissenting framing read the verification modalities as operating at different operational layers; the recovery procedures differ; the citation-precision and closeout-time variants surfaced empirically distinct as sub-classes of drafting-drift specifically rather than of canonical-source discipline generally. The operator selected separate row on the basis that the cross-reference cascade is structurally cleaner when sub-rows #24a (citation-precision) and #24b (closeout-time variant) attach to a parent row dedicated to drafting-drift rather than to a refined #10 covering multiple modalities.

What makes minority-position selection structurally distinct is the operator-side empirical reasoning load. When the operator selects against Council majority, the canonization decision record carries the burden of articulating why majority reading was set aside — what canonical-state evidence the operator weighted differently, what structural reasoning the majority did not produce, what cascade implications informed the selection. The reasoning becomes part of the canonical record at decision-entry scope and at deployment-package scope.

## Pattern 2: Unanimous on path, split on file strategy (Decision 1B)

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1376, Pattern 2 is "unanimous-on-path-with-majority-on-file-strategy split (Decision 1B: 3-of-3 path (a) first-class canonical promotion; 2-of-3 majority — Claude + Gemini — on new canonical file vs Grok minority on existing-file update)."

The decision question for 1B was whether to canonize the 5-rule operational discipline rules artifact as (a) first-class canonical via formal ratification, or (b) formally retained as operational substrate without first-class canonical promotion. Per `lessons-learned.md` line 1537 Decision 1B entry: "Council outcome: 3-of-3 unanimous on path (a) first-class canonical promotion. File strategy split: Claude + Gemini (2-of-3 majority) recommended new canonical file at `operational_discipline_rules.canonical.md`; Grok (1-of-3 minority) recommended existing-file status header update on `operational_discipline_rules_2026-05-07.md`. Operator decision: path (a) first-class canonical promotion; file strategy follows 2-of-3 majority (new canonical file)."

Pattern 2 splits the canonization decision into two layers. The principal decision (canonize as first-class canonical) reached substrate unanimity; the implementation strategy (new file with `.canonical.md` suffix vs existing dated-file status header update) split the Council. The operator applied unanimous reading at principal level and majority reading at file-strategy level.

The operator decision basis at the file-strategy split is recorded at `lessons-learned.md` line 1548: first-class canonical artifacts deserve the `.canonical.md` filename convention (matching `_anti_patterns.canonical.md`) for discoverability, and the dated artifact preserves historical empirical foundation cleanly when separated from current canonical state. The majority framing (Claude + Gemini) read canonical promotion as a structural transition warranting filename-level signaling; the minority framing (Grok) read canonical promotion as a renaming/restatusing event that could happen within the existing dated file. The operator weighted the structural-transition framing on the basis that future canonical artifact additions at the same category level would benefit from the explicit convention.

## Pattern 3: Unanimous with same per-element split (Decision 1C)

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1376, Pattern 3 is "unanimous-with-same-per-element-split (Decision 1C: 3-of-3 path (c) hybrid with same 5/1 archetype split across substrates — all three substrates ratified The Architect, The Helmsman, The Sentinel, The Cartographer, The Steward and held The Herald as research)."

The decision question for 1C was whether to canonize the 6-archetype operator role archetypes artifact as (a) ratify file-header canonical declaration, (b) downgrade to research, or (c) hybrid per-archetype (ratify subset meeting empirical bar; hold subset as research). Per `lessons-learned.md` line 1584 Decision 1C entry: "Council outcome: 3-of-3 unanimous on path (c) hybrid ratification; same 5/1 split across all three substrates (Claude / Grok / Gemini all recommended ratifying The Architect, The Helmsman, The Sentinel, The Cartographer, The Steward as canonical and holding The Herald as research). Operator decision: path (c) hybrid ratification per Council unanimous + per-archetype empirical foundation asymmetry."

Pattern 3 is structurally distinct from Pattern 1 (operator against Council majority) and Pattern 2 (Council unanimous on path with split on strategy). In Pattern 3 the three substrates produced the same per-element split independently: same five archetypes ratified as canonical, same one archetype held as research. The convergence is not at the decision-path level alone — it extends to the granular per-element ratification level, with the same element treated the same way by each substrate.

The operator decision applied the unanimous Council reading at path level and the same per-element split at element level. Decision 1C also surfaced seven operator decisions at mid-checkpoint (A through G per `lessons-learned.md` line 1628): file-level header framing; per-archetype status flag format; Architectural commitment update; Composition rules update; Connected work cascade; anti-pattern catalogue preamble metadata-line; Herald revisit criteria framing. The Council outcome supplied the canonization decision; the operator decisions handled implementation scope. The deliberation work at Decision 1C was less about Council outcome interpretation than about cascade scope decisions, because the substrate convergence at element level was already high-confidence.

## Why the 1C pattern is structurally diagnostic

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1376: "Pattern 3 is structurally diagnostic: same per-element split across substrates suggests substrate-class-independent empirical signal rather than substrate-stylistic-priming artifact."

The Council of Three architecture decision at `master-setup/council_of_three_architecture_decision_2026-05-07.md` rests on substrate-class-distance as the criterion for verification independence. Topic 22 in `research_queue.md` tracks substrate-stylistic-priming as the outstanding empirical concern: substrate convergence may be an artifact of shared prior context rather than evidence of independent verification. [Post 19](0019-when-the-audit-substrate-isnt-independent-enough.md) treats the substrate-stylistic-priming pattern in depth.

When three substrate classes produce the same per-element split independently — same five archetypes ratified, same one archetype held back — the convergence at granular element level is harder to explain via substrate-stylistic-priming than convergence at decision-path level alone. Decision-path convergence can plausibly emerge from shared prior context shaping how each substrate frames the question. Per-element split convergence requires each substrate to evaluate each element against canonical-state evidence and produce the same evaluation independently. The granularity makes the convergence less likely to be an artifact of priming and more likely to reflect a substrate-class-independent empirical signal in the canonical record itself.

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1390: "pattern 3 (unanimous-with-same-split) may be empirical falsification evidence against substrate-stylistic-priming explanation when same per-element split appears across substrate classes." The claim is bounded — Pattern 3 is one observation within one arc; cross-arc replication is unverified. The pattern set is preliminary at n=3 within single arc per the Topic 35 Status framing at line 1415. But the diagnostic potential is what makes Pattern 3 structurally interesting: it supplies empirical evidence stream relevant to the Topic 22 outstanding question at a granular level the other patterns do not.

[Post 20](0020-council-of-three-third-leg-fallibility.md) treats the broader Council-of-Three architecture rationale where the substrate-class-distance question lives. The third-leg-fallibility framing covers what happens when substrate-class-distance fails; Pattern 3 is evidence of what substrate-class-distance produces when it operates as designed.

## Operator-judgment scaffolding per pattern

Per `research_queue.md` Topic 35 line 1389: "Operator decision calibration benefits from pattern recognition: minority-position selection requires stronger operator-side empirical reasoning than unanimous-with-same-split selection."

The three patterns carry different operator-decision-calibration implications, and the deliberation overhead per pattern is uneven.

Minority-position selection (Pattern 1) requires the operator to articulate empirical reasoning that goes beyond Council reasoning, because the operator is selecting against the majority's reading. The reasoning gets surfaced explicitly in the canonization decision record. Decision 1A's operator basis at `lessons-learned.md` line 1505 includes the modality-distinction structural argument and the cross-reference cascade cleanliness argument — both operator-side framings rather than majority Council framings. The operator carries the weight of justifying selection against Council majority, and the canonical record carries the justification.

Unanimous-on-path-with-majority-on-file-strategy (Pattern 2) splits operator-judgment work across two decision layers. The principal decision rides on Council unanimity; the file-strategy decision rides on Council majority with operator-side validation. The operator-side reasoning at principal level can be lighter — substrate unanimous reading is high-confidence input. The operator-side reasoning at file-strategy level applies majority reading combined with operator-side validation of the majority's structural-transition framing.

Unanimous-with-same-per-element-split (Pattern 3) is the lowest-deliberation-overhead Council outcome pattern. The operator applies Council reading with high confidence given the substrate-class-independent signal at element level. The deliberation work at Decision 1C was correspondingly redirected from Council outcome interpretation to cascade scope decisions (the seven operator decisions A through G per `lessons-learned.md` line 1628 — file-level header framing, per-archetype status flag format, and so on). The Council outcome supplied the canonization decision; operator decisions handled implementation scope.

The implication is that pattern recognition at Council outcome scope feeds operator deliberation calibration. Recognizing Pattern 1 prompts the operator to articulate explicit empirical reasoning against majority. Recognizing Pattern 2 prompts the operator to separate principal-decision and implementation-strategy operator-side reasoning. Recognizing Pattern 3 prompts the operator to redirect deliberation overhead from Council interpretation to cascade scope. Without pattern recognition, all three patterns get treated as undifferentiated "Council deployed; operator decided" — and the operator-side reasoning structure that fits each pattern's shape doesn't get cleanly applied.
