I tried to use one personality trait to drive multiple things in the framework's procedural-character architecture. The literature says stop. Different traits drive different cognitive functions. The first-pass V1 specification used HEXACO honesty-humility (commonly abbreviated as HEXACO H; HEXACO is a six-dimensional personality model that adds an honesty-humility dimension to the standard Big Five framework; the model was developed by Ashton and Lee) as the trait source for both cooperation-and-exploitation behaviors (which is what the empirical literature supports HEXACO H for) and relational-event encoding (which is what the empirical literature does not support HEXACO H for). The conflation produced a specific kind of architectural confusion that broke a multi-NPC interaction scenario in test runs; the broken scenario surfaced the need for the re-source; the re-source moved relational-event encoding to attachment style per the canonical attachment-theory literature.

This post walks through the re-source. The walkthrough is operationally specific so that the implementation consequence is legible to other developers working on similar architectures; the consequence is not abstract, it is a change in which trait scalars feed which encoding filters, and the change has visible effects on character behavior at scale. The conflation failure mode is general beyond this specific case; the discipline that catches it is to check the psychometric literature for each cognitive function the architecture's traits drive, and to route the trait sources from the literature's empirical foundation rather than from whichever trait the developer happens to know best.

## The original HEXACO-everywhere mistake

The framework's first-pass specification used HEXACO H for three different functions: cooperation (whether a character cooperates or defects in mixed-motive interactions), generosity (whether a character extends or withholds resources in voluntary giving situations), and relational-event encoding (how richly a character encodes the relational content of interaction events into long-term memory). The first two functions are within HEXACO H's empirical scope; the third is not.

The cooperation and generosity functions are supported by HEXACO H's validation literature directly. Hilbig and Zettler 2009, "Pillars of cooperation: Honesty-humility, social value orientations, and economic behavior," *Journal of Research in Personality* 43(3):516-519, established the HEXACO H dimension's predictive validity for cooperation behaviors in economic-game settings; the paper's empirical contribution is the demonstration that HEXACO H scores predict cooperation behavior independently of the Big Five dimensions that earlier cooperation-research had used. Thielmann and Hilbig 2015, "The traits one can trust: Dissecting reciprocity and kindness as determinants of trustworthy behavior," *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin* 41(11):1523-1536, extended the cooperation finding to trustworthy-behavior contexts; the paper's empirical contribution is the demonstration that HEXACO H scores predict reciprocity and kindness behaviors specifically, distinguishing them from broader cooperation patterns. Zettler, Thielmann, Hilbig, and Moshagen 2020, "The nomological net of the HEXACO model of personality: A large-scale meta-analytic investigation," *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 15(3):723-760, consolidated the HEXACO H validation across multiple cooperation-and-exploitation behavior classes; the meta-analytic evidence is broad enough to support the framework's adoption of HEXACO H as the trait source for the cooperation and generosity functions.

The relational-event encoding function is not in HEXACO H's empirical scope. HEXACO H measures honesty-humility as a trait dimension; it does not measure how characters cognitively process and encode relational events; the literature on what does drive relational-event encoding is a different literature (attachment theory) with a different empirical foundation. The framework's first-pass specification used HEXACO H for relational encoding because HEXACO H was the trait the operator-side architecture work had developed familiarity with; the conflation was operator-driven (a familiar trait was extended beyond its empirical scope) rather than literature-driven. The literature is unambiguous when checked directly; the conflation is what the directly-checked literature catches.

The conflation broke a specific multi-NPC interaction scenario in the framework's test runs. The scenario involved four characters with the same HEXACO H profile but different attachment patterns; under the first-pass spec the four characters produced indistinguishable relational-encoding patterns (because HEXACO H was driving the encoding and the four characters were identical on HEXACO H); the indistinguishable encoding produced indistinguishable downstream pair-relational behavior; the scenario's design intent (characters whose relational-engagement patterns differ in observable ways) failed at the encoding layer. The failure surfaced in test-run review; the failure is what triggered the re-source; the re-source is what corrected the underlying conflation.

## What HEXACO H is actually for

HEXACO H is empirically validated as the trait dimension for exploitation-versus-cooperation behaviors. The HEXACO model itself was developed by Ashton and Lee through factor-analytic work on personality-descriptor lexicons across multiple languages; Ashton and Lee 2009, "The HEXACO-60: A short measure of the major dimensions of personality," *Journal of Personality Assessment* 91(4):340-345, established the short-form HEXACO-60 instrument that the broader HEXACO validation literature uses for measurement. The H dimension specifically captures honesty (truth-telling vs deception in interpersonal situations), fairness (equitable resource distribution vs greed-driven distribution), greed-avoidance (modesty vs status-seeking through material accumulation), and modesty (humility about one's own standing vs grandiosity).

The cooperation-prediction validity is what makes HEXACO H operationally useful for procedural characters. A high-H character is predicted by the literature to cooperate in mixed-motive situations more often than a low-H character; a high-H character is predicted to extend voluntary generosity more often than a low-H character; the predictions are empirical claims that the validation literature has tested across multiple game-theoretic and economic-decision settings. The framework's architecture uses HEXACO H as the trait scalar for cooperation and generosity behaviors because the empirical evidence supports the use; the architecture's decision-scoring filter for cooperation-versus-defection actions reads the character's HEXACO H scalar and modulates the candidate action's expected free energy accordingly.

The honesty-humility framing matters specifically for procedural character design because the framing aligns with what game design surfaces typically need from cooperation-versus-defection patterns. Characters that "are more cooperative" in a way the player can observe over time should also be characters whose cooperation reads as sincere rather than as calculated; HEXACO H captures both the cooperation prediction and the sincerity prediction in a single trait dimension. The framework's adoption of HEXACO H is grounded in the dimension's empirical content and in the dimension's fit with the game-design surface; the alternative trait sources (Big Five Agreeableness; Big Five Conscientiousness; specific narrower-scope cooperation instruments) do not fit the design surface as cleanly.

## What attachment style is actually for

Attachment style is empirically validated as the trait substrate for relational-event encoding and retrieval. Attachment theory's canonical anchors are Bowlby 1969/1982 *Attachment and Loss, Volume 1: Attachment* (Basic Books) and Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters, and Wall 1978 *Patterns of Attachment* (Lawrence Erlbaum Associates); these works established the theoretical foundation and the empirical observation methodology that subsequent attachment research extends. The contemporary measurement instrument the framework uses for attachment-style operationalization is Fraley, Waller, and Brennan 2000, "An item response theory analysis of self-report measures of adult attachment," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 78(2):350-365; the paper developed the ECR-R instrument (Experiences in Close Relationships, Revised), an 18-item self-report measure that produces attachment-avoidance and attachment-anxiety scores as the two principal attachment dimensions.

The two dimensions are operationally distinct. Attachment avoidance captures discomfort with closeness and reluctance to depend on others; the dimension correlates with cognitive patterns where the agent allocates less attention to relational signals, encodes less detail in relational events, and retrieves relational content less easily during subsequent processing. Attachment anxiety captures worry about partner availability and fear of rejection; the dimension correlates with cognitive patterns where the agent over-encodes threat-relevant relational signals, over-attributes negative intent in ambiguous interactions, and shows selective recall of relationship-threatening events. The two dimensions can vary independently; characters can be high on both (fearful-avoidant pattern), high on one and low on the other (anxious-preoccupied or dismissive-avoidant patterns), or low on both (secure pattern).

The relational-encoding-prediction validity is what makes attachment style operationally useful for procedural characters. The validation literature (Mikulincer and Shaver 2016 *Attachment in Adulthood: Structure, Dynamics, and Change*, second edition, Guilford Press, is the canonical synthesis) documents that attachment style predicts specific encoding-and-retrieval patterns for relational content across multiple measurement paradigms. The patterns the literature documents are exactly the patterns the framework's procedural-character architecture needs for the relational-encoding function; the re-source from HEXACO H to attachment style is the architecture's alignment with what the literature actually supports for the function.

## Implementation: trait-modulated encoding filter

The framework's character-state architecture has a trait-modulated encoding filter that operates at the Layer 1 to Layer 2 boundary (the boundary between raw event memory and encoded-and-reconstructed perspective memory; the framework's three-layer memory architecture is documented in subsequent posts). The filter reads the character's trait scalars and modulates how richly an incoming interaction event is encoded into Layer 2 storage. The first-pass specification routed all encoding modulation through HEXACO H; the corrected specification routes the modulation through the trait scalars that the literature supports for each function.

The corrected routing is operationally specific. Cooperation and exploitation actions (whether the character cooperates or defects in mixed-motive interactions; whether the character extends or withholds resources) are modulated by HEXACO H (no change from the first-pass spec; the literature supports this routing). Generosity-ladder actions (the specific behavioral expressions of voluntary giving that the framework's game-design surface uses) are modulated by HEXACO H (no change; same literature support). Relational-event encoding (how richly the Layer 1-to-Layer 2 filter encodes the relational content of an interaction event) is modulated by attachment avoidance and attachment anxiety (corrected from the first-pass spec; routed per Fraley-Waller-Brennan and Mikulincer-Shaver literature). Pair-history accumulation (how the framework's pair-relational sentiment scalars update under interaction events) is modulated by attachment style (the same attachment scalars drive both the per-event encoding and the cross-event accumulation).

The corrected routing produces different downstream behavior at the multi-NPC interaction scale. Characters with the same HEXACO H profile but different attachment styles now produce distinguishable relational-encoding patterns; the distinguishable encoding produces distinguishable downstream pair-relational behavior; the scenario the first-pass spec broke now works because the trait sources align with what the encoding function actually needs. The behavioral expressions are visible in test runs; characters with secure attachment encode relational events richly and retrieve them accurately; characters with anxious attachment encode threat-relevant content with elevated weight and show the over-pessimistic appraisal pattern the literature documents; characters with avoidant attachment encode less relational detail and retrieve it less easily; characters with fearful-avoidant attachment combine the thin encoding with the over-pessimistic appraisal pattern.

## The Fraley and Hudson 2014 caveat

Attachment styles in early adulthood are stable on the order of years, not lifetime. Fraley and Hudson 2014, "Attachment stability: A review and meta-analysis," documents the stability literature; the meta-analytic synthesis shows that attachment styles are moderately stable across multi-year intervals but show meaningful change over decade-scale intervals, particularly in response to significant life events (relationship formation; relationship dissolution; loss of a primary attachment figure; major shifts in life circumstance). The framework should NOT treat attachment as immutable; the framework should treat attachment as stable-but-modifiable.

For procedural characters in a multi-decade tycoon sim, attachment style may shift in response to major life events the simulation models. A character whose marriage dissolves in the framework's simulation might shift toward an avoidant attachment pattern over the subsequent game-years; a character who forms a stable supportive partnership might shift toward more secure attachment over time; the shifts are the literature's empirical pattern translated into the framework's multi-decade simulation context. The framework's implementation includes the shift mechanism as a V1.x refinement candidate; the V1 critical-path adopts the stable-attachment-style spec because the V1 budget does not absorb the shift-mechanism implementation work, and the V1.x refinement is where the shift mechanism would land if the post-V1 player-facing evidence supports the refinement's value.

The stability caveat matters for the framework's audit-layer apparatus. The framework's mandatory test surface includes an amnesia re-initialization test (the test that resets the character's accumulated state and verifies the character returns to era-baseline behavior; the test is one of the framework's commitments to keeping the character architecturally bounded); the test's correctness depends on the attachment-style baseline being well-defined for each character. The stability caveat extends the baseline definition to a stable-but-modifiable framing; the amnesia re-init test treats the attachment baseline as the initialization value rather than as a fixed value, and the V1.x refinement would add the shift-mechanism dynamics on top of the initialization baseline.

## What the re-sourcing unlocks

Characters with the same HEXACO H profile but different attachment styles now behave differently in relational contexts. The four behavioral patterns are operationally distinct in the framework's V1 implementation; the patterns are what the corrected trait-source routing produces. A secure-attachment character encodes relational events richly (the encoding pathway reads the character's low avoidance and low anxiety as supporting full-bandwidth encoding) and appraises relational signals accurately (the appraisal pathway reads the character's secure-attachment profile as supporting accurate appraisal rather than the over-pessimistic appraisal that anxious-attachment produces). An anxious-attachment character also encodes relational events richly (the encoding pathway reads the character's low avoidance as supporting full-bandwidth encoding) but appraises relational signals over-pessimistically (the appraisal pathway reads the character's high anxiety as supporting over-pessimistic appraisal in ambiguous situations). An avoidant-attachment character encodes relational events thinly (the encoding pathway reads the character's high avoidance as supporting low-bandwidth encoding) and shows under-attentive appraisal (the appraisal pathway reads the character's high avoidance as supporting reduced attention to relational signals). A fearful-avoidant-attachment character combines thin encoding with over-pessimistic appraisal (the encoding pathway reads the character's high avoidance; the appraisal pathway reads the character's high anxiety; the combination produces the specific pattern the literature documents).

The four patterns produce visible character-behavior differences in test runs. The framework's design surface (the multi-decade tycoon sim's NPC population) benefits from the variation because the variation aligns with the design surface's commitment to characters whose relational-engagement patterns differ in observable ways across the simulation's scope. The first-pass spec produced characters whose relational-engagement patterns were uniform on the relational axis (because the encoding was driven by HEXACO H rather than by attachment style); the corrected spec produces the variation the design surface needs. The implementation consequence is operationally visible; the consequence is not abstract literature-citation discipline; the consequence is character-behavior variation at the test-run level.

## V1 implementation cost

The schema change is bounded. Attachment style is operationalized as a two-dimensional latent (avoidance and anxiety scalars per character; both scalars in the [0, 1] range per the standard ECR-R operationalization). The ECR-R 18-item self-report instrument can seed each character's initial attachment scalars (the framework's character-generation pipeline produces the ECR-R answer set as part of the character's initial state; the answer set is converted to the avoidance and anxiety scalars through the standard ECR-R scoring procedure). The encoding filter adds the attachment-modulator (the filter's existing HEXACO H modulator stays; the attachment-modulator is added in parallel for the relational-encoding pathway specifically).

The implementation cost estimate at the framework's planning phase was roughly 16 to 28 hours of BUILD-side work. The estimate includes the schema addition, the character-generation pipeline integration, the encoding-filter modification, the audit-layer extension to include attachment-baseline initialization, and the test-run validation. The estimate does not include the V1.x shift-mechanism implementation; the shift mechanism is post-V1 critical-path and the cost estimate for that work is separate. The 16-28 hour V1 cost is operationally tractable within the framework's V1 budget; the cost is what the re-source actually costs at the implementation layer.

## Cross-cultural caveat

ECR-R is WEIRD-sample-derived (Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan 2010, "The weirdest people in the world?" *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 33(2-3):61-83, documents the WEIRD critique systematically; WEIRD stands for Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic). Cross-cultural validity of attachment instruments has been studied (Mikulincer and Shaver 2016 provides a substantial cross-cultural-attachment discussion in chapter form; the cross-cultural attachment-distribution literature is mixed at the population-distribution level even where the construct's validity holds at the within-culture level); the WEIRD-defaults problem applies wherever the framework's character-generation pipeline produces characters across cultural-context tags that span beyond the ECR-R's validation samples.

The framework's broader handling of the WEIRD-defaults problem is documented in a subsequent post in the cross-cultural-disclosure sequence. The handling at the attachment-style layer specifically is: the framework's character-generation pipeline includes cultural-context-tag metadata as schema-placeholder fields per the broader sequence's commitments; the V1 spec does not branch on the tags for attachment-style operationalization (the V1 spec uses the ECR-R-based operationalization uniformly); the post-V1 work that would add cultural-context-aware attachment dynamics is a separate research-program scope that the framework has not committed to at V1.

The caveat matters for the framework's accountability. The framework's character-generation pipeline ships with the ECR-R-based attachment operationalization; the framework's documentation discloses the WEIRD-sample validation limitation; the framework's commitment is that the operationalization is the V1 working spec and that subsequent post-V1 work would engage the cross-cultural attachment literature for any specific cultural-context-tag adjustments. The accountability is what the disclosure produces; the disclosure is what makes the V1 ship-decision defensible against the cross-cultural-validity question.

## What this changed in my workflow

Two specific changes. First, when the framework adopts a trait source for a specific cognitive function, I now check the psychometric validation literature for the specific function before the adoption is ratified. The discipline catches the use-the-familiar-trait failure mode (the operator-driven extension of a familiar trait beyond its empirical scope); the discipline produces trait-source routings whose empirical foundation is the published literature rather than the operator's prior familiarity. The cost is a literature check per trait-source decision (typically 30 minutes to two hours for a focused literature scan); the benefit is that the trait sources align with the literature's empirical content rather than with the operator's prior commitments.

Second, when the framework's test runs surface unexpected uniformity in character behavior (characters whose design specifications would predict varied behavior producing uniform behavior in test runs), I now treat the uniformity as a possible trait-source-routing failure mode rather than as a tuning issue. The discipline reads the uniformity as a structural-level diagnostic that the architecture's trait sources may be driving multiple functions when the literature would route them to different sources; the diagnostic is what catches conflations like the HEXACO H over-extension at the test-run level. The discipline is small (a diagnostic question that runs whenever test-run uniformity surfaces); the discipline catches the kind of failure mode that easy-to-tune-away framings would otherwise absorb.

## What I am not claiming

The validation literature is broader than HEXACO H; Big Five Agreeableness is a well-established alternative; specific narrower-scope cooperation instruments exist for specific design surfaces. The framework adopts HEXACO H because the dimension's fit with the game-design surface is operationally cleaner than the alternatives the framework considered; the choice is operator-judgment, not literature-mandated. Other procedural-character frameworks may legitimately route cooperation-and-exploitation behaviors through different trait sources; the framework's adoption is one defensible choice among several.

The framework adopts attachment style because the dimension's empirical foundation for relational-event encoding specifically is the cleanest the framework's literature scan surfaced; the choice is operator-judgment supported by the literature, not literature-mandated.
