## Rule 1: Standing direction

The rule statement, verbatim from the artifact: "The operator establishes persistent direction at session start that governs all subsequent prompt-delivery work." The standing direction format names six components: take recommended approach for every round; audit and grade work via the five-dimension rubric (spec clarity, failure-mode targeting, length-to-value, internal coherence, external-truth grounding); A+-quality calibration with explicit re-read step; include Claude Code prompt code block for recommended approach in every drafting-work turn; no deferrals; surface honest weakness flags.

The operational consequence is that the assistant produces a single recommended path forward without per-decision deliberation. Honest weakness flags surface alternative options for operator pushback rather than getting buried in deferred-decision framing. The rubric audit applies to every prompt before delivery, not selectively.

The empirical foundation, captured at `lessons-learned.md` line 973: "Operator's standing direction 'take recommended approach for every round; no defer' empirically supported as productive workflow constraint when paired with rubric-audit + re-read discipline." The line sits inside the end-of-session methodology research observations entry from 2026-05-07, which captured the session-discipline observations that became Rules 1 and 3.

The discipline this rule encodes is workflow-constraint specificity. A standing direction is not a vague preference. It is a six-component specification governing prompt-delivery work, paired with two adjacent disciplines (rubric audit, re-read pass) without which the standing direction degrades into preference rather than constraint. Rule 1 captures the specification; Rules 2 and 3 capture the adjacent disciplines.

## Rule 2: Pre-delivery re-read step

The rule statement, verbatim from the artifact: "Before every Claude Code prompt delivery, the assistant performs explicit re-read pass on prompt text + code block + content claims to surface drift before delivery." The operational consequence, captured verbatim at the artifact's Rule 2 section: "Re-read pass catches drift items that initial drafting + flag pass missed. Empirically necessary but not sufficient — re-read pass must extend recursively to find-string drafting (not just substantive content claims) when applicable."

The empirical foundation, captured at `lessons-learned.md` line 912 within the Topic 20 addendum: "Pre-delivery re-read pass empirically necessary but not sufficient — re-read pass surfaced edits that initial draft + flag pass missed but didn't catch all drift instances; verify-canonical-first applied recursively to prompt drafting (not just substantive content claims) is the additional discipline that the multi-fire pattern reveals as load-bearing."

This rule is one of the two rules whose empirical foundation overlaps with the drafting-drift canonical promotion arc documented in [Post 25](0025-drafting-drift-canonical-promotion-arc.md). The artifact's cross-references section names the link explicitly: research_queue.md Topic 20 supplies empirical evidence supporting Rule 2 and Rule 4. The drafting-drift fires that accumulated across substantive content drafting, Phase 5 correction prompt drafting, and canonical-state-update prompt drafting were the empirical record that surfaced pre-delivery re-read as necessary discipline. The same record surfaced its insufficiency on its own. The rule encodes both the necessity and the limit: re-read pass is required, but verify-canonical-first applied recursively is the additional discipline that catches what re-read alone misses.

The relationship is bidirectional. Post 25 traces the empirical accumulation; this rule encodes the discipline the accumulation supports. The accumulation does not stop because the rule is canonized. The rule is the codification of what the accumulation revealed about pre-delivery work.

## Rule 3: A+-quality calibration target

The rule statement, verbatim from the artifact: "Every Claude Code prompt achieves A+ aggregate per 5-dimension rubric before delivery. Sub-A+ items surface as honest weakness flags rather than getting delivered silently." The operational consequence: quality calibration is non-negotiable across substantive session work. Honest weakness flags allow operator pushback on calibration items where operator priorities differ from assistant's read.

The empirical foundation, captured at `lessons-learned.md` line 973: "Sustained A+-quality calibration across ~14-hour session work via re-read passes, rubric audits on every prompt before delivery, and verify-canonical-first discipline applied to find strings (not just substantive content)." The line is the same end-of-session observations entry that supplied Rule 1's foundation; the observations covered the sustained-discipline capacity demonstrated across the session's fourteen hours of work.

The discipline this rule encodes is quality-bar fixity. The bar is set at A+ aggregate, not per-dimension. Sub-A+ items get flagged honestly rather than getting delivered with the dimension's weakness obscured. The rule's load-bearing element is the bilateral structure: A+ aggregate plus honest weakness flags. Without the flags, the bar would degrade silently; without the bar, the flags would have no anchoring. The two operate as a pair.

The rule does not claim every prompt actually achieves A+ aggregate. It claims the prompt either does, or its sub-A+ dimensions are surfaced as flags so the operator can decide whether to push back on calibration. The flag is the honesty mechanism. The rule prohibits silent delivery of work the assistant knows is sub-bar.

## Rule 4: Catch-and-fix at point of detection

The rule statement, verbatim from the artifact: "When drift surfaces during section drafting (or any work-in-progress state), fix at point of detection rather than batching to final corrections phase." The operational consequence: cheaper than batched corrections; preserves cumulative draft accuracy; reduces downstream propagation. Each correction at section level uses smaller find strings, less downstream propagation, and benefits from immediate cross-reference coherence checks.

The empirical foundation, captured at `lessons-learned.md` line 975: "Catch-and-fix at point of detection (per-section verification + immediate correction) preserves cumulative draft accuracy more cheaply than batched corrections at end. Discipline empirically demonstrated across 6 banked-post-#4 corrections + 1 banked-post-#6 correction + 2 banked-post-#3 corrections."

This is the second rule whose empirical foundation overlaps with the drafting-drift arc. The nine fires the line cites (six in banked post #4, one in banked post #6, two in banked post #3) are part of the same fire accumulation [Post 25](0025-drafting-drift-canonical-promotion-arc.md) traces. Banked post #4's six fires are specifically the citation-precision sub-class fires that Post 25 documented as empirically distinct sub-pattern. Rule 4 turns that empirical record into discipline encoding: when these fires surface during section drafting, the discipline is to fix immediately rather than carry the drift forward for batched correction at the end of drafting.

The rule's economy claim is testable. Batched corrections at end produce larger find strings, more downstream propagation, and cross-reference coherence work that has to wait until the batch runs. Per-section corrections produce smaller find strings, less propagation surface, and immediate coherence checks. The rule asserts the second profile is empirically cheaper. The session evidence (nine fires across three banked posts caught and corrected at section level) supports the claim within the session arc; cross-session generalization is not yet empirically tested.

## Rule 5: Per-section verification plus Phase 5 batched review combined approach

The rule statement, verbatim from the artifact: "Citation-heavy posts benefit from per-section verification at section drafting level + Phase 5 batched review at end, not Phase 5 batched review alone. Citation density triggers escalation to per-section verification discipline." The operational consequence: per-section discipline catches citation-precision drift earlier than batched review; substantially reduces Phase 5 surface area. Phase 5 batched review remains operationally important for cross-section coherence checks and final quality assurance.

The empirical foundation, captured at `lessons-learned.md` line 965: "Per-section verification + Phase 5 batched review combined approach: Empirically demonstrated more effective than Phase 5 batched review alone for citation-heavy posts. Banked post #4 (~10 academic citations) produced 6 distinct citation-precision fires caught at section drafting level (Steps 7.4-correction through 7.11-correction-2) vs zero would have surfaced at Phase 5 batched review alone. Same end-state alignment achievable both ways but per-section verification reduces correction overhead per fire (smaller find strings, less downstream propagation, immediate cross-reference coherence checks)."

Rule 5 extends Rule 4 with a specific trigger condition. Rule 4 names catch-and-fix at point of detection as a general workflow constraint. Rule 5 names citation density as a specific trigger that escalates section-drafting work to mandatory per-section verification. The two rules operate at different scales of generality. Rule 4 is a workflow rule for any drift surfaced during work-in-progress. Rule 5 is a trigger-conditional escalation for citation-heavy work specifically.

The empirical claim is structural rather than count-based. Same end-state alignment is achievable through Phase 5 batched review alone or through per-section verification plus Phase 5 batched review combined; the difference is correction overhead, not output quality. Per-section verification catches drift when find strings are still small and propagation is still local. Phase 5 alone catches drift after the full document exists, when find strings have grown and propagation has spread. The rule asserts the combined approach is the cheaper path to the same end state for citation-heavy work.

The rules are operationally load-bearing for the work I did during the 2026-05-07 session and the work I have done in the days since under the same configuration. Whether they would be operationally load-bearing for a different operator working with different substrate compositions on different work classes is not what the empirical foundation establishes. The rules are canonical for this framework. The framework is one operator's deployment.

The five rules are not the complete operational discipline taxonomy.
