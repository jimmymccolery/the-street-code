## What Wiredu's sympathetic impartiality is

Wiredu Chapter 9 "The Moral Foundations of an African Culture" in *Person and Community: Ghanaian Philosophical Studies, I* (Wiredu and Gyekye, editors, 1992; Council for Research in Values and Philosophy Series II.1) page 199 documents the principle directly. The relevant passages (verified against the primary text in the framework's Round 8 R4 closing-round re-read): sympathetic impartiality "is the first principle of all morals" and "the logical basis of the golden rule" (page 199; corroborated per F-17 reversal of the prior arc's F-13 uncorroborated status).

The principle is a procedural moral test. The test operates as follows: when an agent faces a candidate action affecting one or more parties, the agent imagines the outcome from each affected party's position (including the position of any party who would be harmed by the action; the position of any party who would benefit; the position of any party who would be morally indifferent to the action); the agent evaluates whether the outcome would be acceptable to the agent regardless of which position the agent occupied; if acceptable under sympathetic imagination from all positions, the action is morally permissible; if not acceptable from some position, the action is not morally permissible.

The principle has structural similarity to other procedural moral tests across multiple traditions. Kantian universalizability ("Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law"; Kant 1785 *Groundwork of the Metaphysic of Morals* is the canonical source) operates as a procedural test where the agent imagines the action as a universal law applied to all rational agents; the test's verdict is the action's moral permissibility. Rawlsian original position ("Choose principles of justice as if you do not know your own position in society"; Rawls 1971 *A Theory of Justice* is the canonical source) operates as a procedural test where the agent imagines choosing principles under a veil of ignorance about their own social position; the test's verdict is the principles' justice. The three tests share the structural shape of a deliberative imaginative procedure that produces a per-action or per-principle verdict.

Wiredu's contribution to the procedural-test literature is the grounding of the test in Akan philosophical tradition with relational personhood and Akan-tradition-specific moral vocabulary. The grounding produces the test's specific framing within the broader Akan moral philosophy; the grounding does not change the test's structural shape; the test remains a procedural moral test in the same category as the Kantian and Rawlsian tests structurally, even while differing from them in philosophical-tradition substantive content.

The disclosure discipline (the five-element discipline from post 0124 in this arc) applies to this engagement maximally.

## What HEXACO honesty-humility is

HEXACO honesty-humility (commonly abbreviated as HEXACO H; the trait dimension within the HEXACO six-dimensional personality model that captures honesty-fairness-greed-avoidance-modesty patterns) is an individual-difference trait dimension. The HEXACO model is grounded in Lee and Ashton 2004, "Psychometric properties of the HEXACO Personality Inventory," *Multivariate Behavioral Research* 39(2):329-358; the short-form HEXACO-60 instrument is grounded in Ashton and Lee 2009, "The HEXACO-60: A short measure of the major dimensions of personality," *Journal of Personality Assessment* 91(4):340-345.

The H dimension specifically captures: honesty (truth-telling vs deception in interpersonal situations); fairness (equitable resource distribution vs greed-driven distribution); greed-avoidance (modesty vs status-seeking through material accumulation); modesty (humility about one's own standing vs grandiosity). The dimension is measured by self-report or peer-report on the validated psychometric instruments (the HEXACO-60 short-form; the longer HEXACO-100 full-form; specific facet-level instruments).

The dimension predicts cooperative behavior in economic games. Hilbig and Zettler 2009, "Pillars of cooperation: Honesty-humility, social value orientations, and economic behavior," *Journal of Research in Personality* 43(3):516-519, established the predictive validity for cooperation behaviors in economic-game settings. Thielmann and Hilbig 2015, "The traits one can trust: Dissecting reciprocity and kindness as determinants of trustworthy behavior," *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin* 41(11):1523-1536, established the extension to trustworthy-behavior contexts. The validation literature is broad enough to support the framework's adoption of HEXACO H as the trait source for cooperation and exploitation behaviors in procedural characters.

The dimension is dispositional. The dimension represents a tendency that the agent has rather than a deliberative act the agent performs. The trait is something the agent IS rather than something the agent DOES; the trait's expression in behavior is statistical across populations of situations rather than per-action; the trait does not produce per-action moral verdicts; the trait predicts cooperation-behavior frequencies across populations of mixed-motive interactions.

## The category error walkthrough

Mapping Wiredu's sympathetic impartiality onto HEXACO H is a category error. The mapping conflates a procedural moral test (something the agent DOES; a deliberative imaginative procedure that produces a per-action verdict) with an individual-difference trait dimension (something the agent HAS; a dispositional tendency that predicts behavior statistically across populations of situations). The conflation is structural; the conflation is not implementation-detail; the conflation produces a specific failure mode at the architecture's expressive layer.

Different objects. A procedural moral test is something the agent DOES. A trait is something the agent HAS. Mapping "doing" onto "having" confuses levels of analysis. The framework's first-pass spec attempted the mapping; the framework's deliberation cycle (Round 7 R6 Section 3) caught the category error and produced the Option (c) drop documented in post 0117 in this arc.

Different evaluation logic. A procedural moral test passes or fails per-case (this specific candidate action satisfies the test or doesn't; the test produces a binary verdict at the per-action level). A trait dimension is gradient (the agent has a high or low score on the trait dimension; the score is continuous; the trait does not produce per-action binary verdicts). The two evaluation structures are different categories; you cannot grade a per-case binary test on a continuous gradient without losing the per-case binary content of the test's verdicts.

Different causal direction. A trait might predispose an agent to apply a procedural test more often (the agent's high-H trait might predict that the agent reflects on action permissibility more carefully than a low-H agent would); the trait IS NOT the test. Having high H does not mean the agent sympathetically-impartially evaluated something; having high H means the agent tends to be cooperative across populations of mixed-motive situations. The trait's predictive validity for procedural-test application is a separate empirical question from the trait's identity with the test; the framework's mapping had conflated the prediction with identity, and the conflation is the category error.

Procedural test's universal claim does not transfer at the right level. Wiredu argues sympathetic impartiality is universal (F-17 corroborated per post 0117 in this arc) at the procedural-test level: the test's structure is universal across human cultures because morality is universal across human culture; the test's specific application differs across cultures because the cultural context differs. HEXACO H scores transfer across cultures with empirical caveats: the trait's measurement validity has been studied cross-culturally with mixed findings; the trait is not the test, and the test's universal-structure claim does not transfer to the trait's measurement-validity claim. Conflating the two would import the test's structural-universalism into the trait's measurement-validity claim in ways that the literature does not support.

The four points above together compose the category error. The error is structural; the error is not bibliographic; the error has implementation consequence. The framework's first-pass spec would have routed Wiredu's procedural moral test through the HEXACO H trait dimension; the routing would have produced V1 NPCs whose "sympathetic impartiality" was statistical cooperation rather than per-action procedural moral evaluation; the implementation would have delivered something genuinely different from what the philosophical framing claimed. The gap between framing and delivery is the harm.

## The ornamental-deployment risk

Bilge 2013, "Intersectionality undone: Saving intersectionality from feminist intersectionality studies," *Du Bois Review: Social Science Research on Race* 10(2):405-424, names the risk class the framework's category-error mapping would have produced. Bilge's paper is about a different specific case (the academic appropriation of intersectionality from its grounding in Black feminist theory); the structural shape of the risk class is broader than the specific case. The risk class is ornamental deployment: borrowing the prestige of a source tradition while implementing none of the substance.

The ornamental-deployment risk applies to the Wiredu mapping case directly. A V1 implementation that scored individual utility with no communal-relational accountability mechanic, while citing Wiredu in the design documents as the philosophical grounding for the generosity trait, is ornamental. The philosophical citation adds reputation to the framework; the mechanic implements no communal accountability that would deliver on the Wiredu citation's substantive content; the player experiences no actual Wireduan personhood-as-striving; the player experiences statistical generosity tuning that the design documents have re-labeled as Wireduan to invoke the philosophical reputation.

Worse than no mapping. The framing matters specifically: a V1 implementation that did not cite Wiredu at all would simply be a V1 implementation with no philosophical-grounding claim; the implementation would be evaluated on its own technical merits, and the cross-cultural-engagement question would not arise. A V1 implementation that cites Wiredu but implements no communal accountability is worse than the no-citation case because the citation makes a claim the implementation cannot deliver. The dishonesty of the citation is the harm; the dishonesty is what Bilge's framework names as ornamental.

The cross-cultural appropriation literature beyond Bilge is broader than what the framework's research arc has engaged in depth. The broader literature would extend the ornamental-deployment framing into related discussions (decolonial-theory critiques of academic engagement with non-Western traditions; specific anthropological-research-ethics standards; specific cross-cultural-philosophy methodology guidelines). ## The lesson generalized

The category-error pattern is general beyond Wiredu's sympathetic impartiality. Specific examples:

Treating Kantian universalizability as a "deontology trait slider" is the same category error. A character with a high "Kantianness" score would, on the framework's hypothetical first-pass spec, be more likely to follow universalizable maxims; the trait is not the test; the test is the procedural deliberative imaginative procedure that the character would have to actually perform per-action; the trait is the statistical tendency. The mapping is structurally wrong for the same reason the Wiredu-HEXACO H mapping was wrong.

Treating Rawlsian original position as a "fairness trait slider" is the same category error. A character with a high "Rawlsianness" score would, on a hypothetical first-pass spec, be more likely to choose principles under a veil of ignorance; the trait is not the test; the test is the procedural counterfactual imaginative procedure; the trait is the statistical tendency. Same structural error.

Treating Confucian role ethics as a "filial-piety trait dimension" is the same category error. A character with a high "filial-piety" score would, on a hypothetical first-pass spec, be more likely to fulfill the obligations of their specific role within the relational structure; the role ethics is not a trait, it is a deliberative procedure for evaluating role-appropriate action; the trait is the statistical tendency. Same structural error.

Treating Ubuntu communal accountability as a "communalism trait slider" is the same category error. A character with a high "Ubuntu" score would, on a hypothetical first-pass spec, be more likely to act with communal accountability; the Ubuntu framework is not a trait, it is a relational philosophy of personhood that operates through deliberative procedures; the trait is the statistical tendency. Same structural error.

In each case, the procedural test is a deliberative practice. The trait is a tendency. Confusing them is the structural error. The error generalizes across multiple cross-cultural traditions; the error generalizes across multiple Western-philosophical traditions; the error is a class of mistake the cross-cultural philosophy literature has named (Bilge 2013 names the risk class; the philosophy-of-personality literature documents the broader categorical distinction); the error is operationally consequential for procedural-character design because the error produces implementations that claim philosophical-grounding content the implementations do not deliver.
