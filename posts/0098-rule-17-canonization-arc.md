# From Stale Knowledge to Canonical Rule: How a Discipline Gets Promoted in Six Fires

**Author:** Jimmy McColery
**Date:** 2026-06-10
**Status:** Meta-post on operational-discipline canonization arc; concrete worked example through six fires across five days that promoted an ad-hoc observation into a numbered Rule with structured trigger taxonomy; generalizes to any operational-discipline canonization arc, not just CW project knowledge refresh

*Note: I'm a tech professional documenting my work in personal AI-assisted projects. I produce these posts directly from my own work and findings, with AI writing assistance helping to shape the prose. The implementation, the diagnosis, and the analysis are mine; AI assistance shaped the prose from my raw session notes and canonical record.*

---

Most operational disciplines in long-project work emerge ad-hoc. The operator notices a specific failure pattern; the operator files an observation as a feedback memory; the pattern fires again under different conditions; the operator updates the memory; the pattern keeps firing; the operator promotes the pattern from feedback memory to numbered canonical Rule with explicit trigger taxonomy and propagation discipline. The promotion arc has structure, and the structure is worth documenting because the structure generalizes across discipline domains in any framework that runs long enough to accumulate operational-discipline patterns.

This post documents one specific discipline's promotion arc as a worked example. The discipline is Rule 17 in my framework's operational-discipline rules: the CW Project Knowledge Refresh Discipline. The discipline addresses a specific failure mode in long-running orchestrator-class AI workspaces where the workspace's canonical-state knowledge drifts out of sync with the project's actual canonical record, and queries against the workspace produce calibrated-feeling outputs that are grounded in the stale knowledge rather than in the current state. The discipline canonized 2026-05-31 at the framework's third Architecture Revision session. The canonization rested on six fires across five days; the six fires are what the canonization arc looks like in practice.

## The promotion arc has structure

The structure has five stages that I have observed across multiple discipline canonization arcs in my framework. The stages are not strictly sequential; some arcs fold stages together; but the five stages capture the canonical shape that emerged across my discipline-canonization observations.

Stage one is ad-hoc observation: the operator notices a specific failure pattern in one work class, files an observation as a feedback memory or as a single-fire note, and continues with the work that surfaced the pattern. The observation may or may not turn into anything; most observations at this stage do not become canonical rules because the pattern does not reproduce under different conditions.

Stage two is reproduction across distinct work classes: the pattern fires again in a different work class than the original observation. The reproduction is what distinguishes the observation from a one-off coincidence; the cross-class reproduction is the signal that the pattern is structural rather than circumstantial. The operator updates the feedback memory with the second fire and begins watching for additional fires.

Stage three is rule-candidacy threshold: the pattern reproduces enough times that the operator promotes the candidate from feedback memory to numbered-rule candidacy. The threshold in my framework is roughly n=3 reproductions across distinct work classes; other frameworks may use different thresholds. The threshold matters because it is what separates ad-hoc observations from canonical rules; below the threshold, the pattern is filed; at the threshold, the pattern enters Architecture Revision deliberation queue.

Stage four is Architecture Revision ratification with structured trigger taxonomy: an Architecture Revision session deliberates the candidate and ratifies it as a numbered Rule with explicit trigger taxonomy. The trigger taxonomy is what makes the rule operationally deployable: the rule names the conditions under which it fires, the actions the operator should take when the conditions are present, and the calendar cadence (if any) the rule enforces. The structured trigger taxonomy is what distinguishes a numbered Rule from a less-structured operational note.

Stage five is propagation to canonical artifact: the ratified Rule lands in the framework's operational-discipline canonical artifact (in my framework, the rules live in a specific canonical file that other rules cross-reference). The propagation is what makes the rule available to future operations; without it, the ratified rule is in the Architecture Revision record but not in the workflow's active discipline set.

The five stages span the full lifecycle from ad-hoc observation to active operational discipline. The lifecycle takes weeks or months depending on the pattern's reproduction rate; some patterns reach Stage five quickly because they fire frequently, others take longer because the patterns are bound to specific conditions that arise less often.

## The Rule 17 specific timeline

The Rule 17 promotion arc compressed the five stages into roughly five days. The compression was unusual; most discipline arcs in my framework take longer. The compression was possible because the pattern fired in five distinct work classes in five consecutive working days, which is a higher reproduction rate than most patterns produce.

The first fire was 2026-05-26 at the close of a deliberation cycle covering one of the framework's project workspaces. The orchestrator-class workspace had been auditing the project against its own knowledge of the project's canonical state; the audit produced findings calibrated against knowledge that was several days stale. The operator caught the staleness by cross-referencing the audit findings against the canonical record directly; the staleness was the load-bearing failure that made the audit's findings less reliable than they appeared. The first fire produced the ad-hoc observation at Stage one.

The second fire was 2026-05-27 or 2026-05-28 (the dates blurred across the deliberation cycle) during a worker-thread streaming serialization deliberation with four substrates. The Council deployed against the deliberation question; the orchestrator-class workspace's project-knowledge surface produced framing that referenced canonical state from before the project's most recent architectural-lock close. The pattern reproduced at a different work class than the first fire; the pattern moved from audit-class to Council-deliberation-class. The reproduction crossed Stage two.

The third fire was 2026-05-29 during city-sim research substrate deployments. The research arc deployed four substrates against a topic; the orchestrator-class workspace's framing of the topic referenced canonical state that did not reflect the most recent project mutations. The reproduction at a third work class (research-class) crossed Stage three's threshold. The operator promoted the pattern from feedback memory to rule-candidacy.

The fourth fire was 2026-05-30 morning during a Madisonian Gate architectural-lock close cycle. The Council deployed against the architectural-lock question; the orchestrator-class workspace's synthesis referenced canonical state from before the most recent architectural-section mutation. The pattern fired at a fourth work class (architectural-lock close-class).

The fifth fire was 2026-05-30 evening during a UI-vision close cycle. The cycle's substrate work referenced canonical state from before the most recent UI-vision mutation. The pattern fired at a fifth work class (UI-vision close-class).

The 2026-05-31 Architecture Revision session ratified the pattern as Rule 17 with a structured trigger taxonomy: a hard-trigger (pre-deployment verification question that runs against the orchestrator-class workspace before any new substrate research arc deploys); a soft-trigger (a 24-48 hour post-canonical-lock refresh of the orchestrator-class project knowledge); a calendar-trigger (a weekly minimum refresh cadence regardless of mutation activity). The trigger taxonomy is what makes the rule operationally enforceable across the framework's diverse work classes.

The ratified rule propagated to the framework's operational-discipline canonical artifact the same day. The rule has fired multiple times since canonization; the fires have caught project-knowledge staleness before it propagated into downstream substrate analysis at multiple subsequent work cycles.

## What the trigger taxonomy contributes

The trigger taxonomy is what distinguishes a numbered Rule from a less-structured operational note. A note that says "refresh project knowledge sometimes" is operationally unenforceable; the operator has no specific decision point at which the note fires, and the note's discipline is at the operator's discretion. A rule with explicit hard-, soft-, and calendar-triggers is operationally enforceable; the rule names the specific decision points (pre-deployment verification; post-canonical-lock window; calendar cadence) and the operator's discipline is to execute the trigger at the decision point.

The Rule 17 hard-trigger is the most load-bearing of the three. The pre-deployment verification question runs against the orchestrator-class workspace before any new substrate research arc deploys; the question tests whether the workspace's project knowledge reflects the most recent canonical state. The verification takes a minute or two; the verification catches stale-knowledge deployments before they propagate downstream. Without the hard-trigger, the rule would be reactive (catching staleness after it has produced downstream effects); with the hard-trigger, the rule is proactive (catching staleness before it produces downstream effects).

The Rule 17 soft-trigger is the longitudinal discipline. The 24-48 hour post-canonical-lock refresh window ensures the orchestrator-class workspace's project knowledge updates after each canonical-state mutation; without the refresh, the workspace's knowledge would drift further from the canonical record each mutation. The soft-trigger keeps the workspace's knowledge close to the canonical record under steady-state mutation activity.

The Rule 17 calendar-trigger is the minimum-cadence safety net. The weekly minimum refresh ensures the workspace's project knowledge is never more than a week stale regardless of mutation activity. The calendar-trigger catches the case where the canonical state has not mutated recently but the workspace's knowledge has nonetheless decayed from training-data updates, indexing issues, or other ambient drift sources.

The three triggers together produce robust enforcement of the discipline. Any of the three triggers alone would leave failure modes that the other two prevent; the three together form a defense-in-depth structure that catches stale-knowledge deployments at multiple decision points.

## What this generalizes to

The Rule 17 canonization arc generalizes to any operational-discipline canonization in a framework that runs long enough to accumulate operational-discipline patterns. The five-stage structure applies across discipline domains; the specific timeline varies by pattern reproduction rate; the trigger taxonomy varies by the discipline's operational shape.

Several disciplines in my framework have gone through equivalent canonization arcs. The multi-substrate research workflow methodology (Rule 11) canonized through an arc covering cross-substrate Council deployments at architectural-deliberation-class scope. The Madisonian Gate verdict classification discipline (Rule 14) canonized through an arc covering substrate-verdict-divergence cycles. The atomic mutation discipline (Rule 13) canonized through an arc covering canonical-file mutation cascades. Each Rule's arc follows the five-stage structure with different fire counts, different timelines, and different trigger taxonomies, but the structural shape is consistent.

The generalization to frameworks beyond mine depends on the framework having the structural property the canonization arc rests on: a canonical record with explicit numbered rules, an Architecture Revision process for rule ratification, and an operational-discipline canonical artifact that ratified rules propagate to. Frameworks without these structural properties produce different canonization arcs, but the underlying lifecycle (ad-hoc observation through cross-class reproduction through threshold canonization to active enforcement) is what the five-stage structure names.

## What this changed in my workflow

One change since the Rule 17 arc closed. When I notice a new failure pattern in one work class, I now begin watching for cross-class reproduction explicitly rather than treating the observation as a one-off coincidence. The pattern may or may not reproduce; the cross-class watching is what surfaces the reproduction when it happens.

A second change is that I now structure feedback memories with explicit reproduction-count tracking. The feedback memory for a candidate-canonical-rule pattern includes a running list of fires, each fire's work-class, and the cumulative reproduction count. The structured tracking makes the canonization candidacy decision more transparent: when the count crosses the n=3 threshold, the candidacy enters the Architecture Revision deliberation queue automatically rather than requiring the operator to remember to escalate.

A third change is that I now read Architecture Revision sessions partly through the lens of which patterns are at which stage. The session's deliberation queue has patterns at different stages; the patterns at Stage three or four get the most attention because they are at the canonization-decision point. The structured reading helps me prioritize the session's time across the queue.

## What I am not claiming

I am not claiming the five-stage structure exhaustively captures all canonization arcs. Some patterns skip stages (a pattern that fires across five work classes at once may compress Stages two and three into a single event). Some patterns include additional stages (a pattern with cross-framework reproduction may include a stage of cross-framework validation before canonization). The five stages capture the canonical shape I have observed across my framework's discipline canonizations; other frameworks may produce different arc shapes.

I am not claiming the n=3 threshold for canonization candidacy is the right threshold across all frameworks. The threshold in my framework reflects a specific judgment about the cost of premature canonization (canonizing a pattern that turns out to be circumstantial rather than structural) versus the cost of delayed canonization (waiting too long to canonize a pattern that is producing downstream failures). The threshold is workflow-specific; other workflows with different cost profiles may use higher or lower thresholds.

I am not claiming the Rule 17 trigger taxonomy is the canonical structure for all operational disciplines. Some disciplines need only one trigger (hard- or calendar-); some disciplines need different trigger types (event-driven; condition-driven; risk-driven). The hard-soft-calendar structure fits Rule 17 because the discipline addresses staleness that has multiple sources; other disciplines with single-source failure modes have simpler trigger structures.

I am not claiming the Rule 17 arc's five-day timeline generalizes. The compression was unusual; most discipline canonization arcs in my framework take weeks or months. The five-day compression was possible because the pattern fired in five distinct work classes in five consecutive days, which is a higher reproduction rate than most patterns produce. Operators in other frameworks should expect their canonization arcs to take longer than Rule 17's; the arc's structure generalizes, the arc's timeline does not.

I am not claiming the canonization arc is the only way disciplines enter the framework. Some disciplines are added through direct Architecture Revision deliberation without an ad-hoc reproduction history; some disciplines are inherited from other frameworks the operator has worked with. The canonization arc is one entry pathway among several; the entry pathway shapes how the discipline is empirically grounded but does not determine the discipline's operational value.

## What I am claiming

Operational disciplines in long-project work generally emerge through a five-stage canonization arc: ad-hoc observation produces an initial fire; cross-class reproduction validates the pattern as structural; rule-candidacy threshold promotes the pattern from feedback memory to numbered-rule candidacy; Architecture Revision ratification produces a numbered Rule with structured trigger taxonomy; propagation to canonical artifact makes the rule available across the framework's work classes. The five-stage structure is the canonical shape I have observed across multiple discipline canonizations in my framework.

If you are running long-project work and accumulating operational-discipline patterns, the five-stage structure is a useful lens for thinking about which patterns are at which stage and which canonization decisions are coming up. The structure does not prescribe specific thresholds or timelines; the structure names the lifecycle that patterns go through as they move from ad-hoc observation to canonical rule. The Rule 17 worked example is one concrete arc; the broader claim is that the arc's structure generalizes across discipline domains in frameworks that run long enough to accumulate patterns.

The structured trigger taxonomy is what distinguishes a numbered Rule from a less-structured operational note. Rules with explicit hard-, soft-, and calendar-triggers are operationally enforceable; notes without specific decision points are at the operator's discretion. Frameworks accumulating operational-discipline rules should expect to invest in the trigger taxonomy structure because the structure is what makes the rules deliverable rather than aspirational.
